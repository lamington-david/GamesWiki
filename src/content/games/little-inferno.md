## Design Notes

**Comfortable mechanics as a trap.** Little Inferno is built around the observation that many games — particularly mobile and casual games — reward players with escalating stimuli for doing very little. Burning items in the fireplace produces satisfying crackles, colourful effects, and catalogue unlocks; the feedback loop is deliberately pleasant and low-effort. Tomorrow Corporation then uses the game's final act to make the player examine what they've been doing. The comfort is not incidental; it's the rhetorical setup. The game cannot make its point about passive game design without first demonstrating it.

**Satire through mechanics, not text.** Where most game satire comments on games through dialogue or framing, Little Inferno embeds its critique in the player's literal actions. You burn things. You receive new things to burn. You unlock more things to burn. The mechanics are the argument — players cannot observe Little Inferno's satirical target from a safe distance because they are performing it. This distinguishes it from games that describe passive consumption as a theme; Little Inferno makes the player enact it, then implicates them in the enactment.

**Atmosphere through reduction.** The game's visual world is reduced to a handful of elements: the fireplace, the falling snow outside, the occasional letter from characters who exist only in text. This radical reduction of context is itself a design statement — the cosy domestic comfort is a closed loop, and the absence of a larger world to engage with is the point. The choice to strip away exploration, navigation, and external stakes makes the fireplace feel like the entire world, which is exactly the condition the game is critiquing.

**The role of the catalogue and narrative delivery.** Little Inferno delivers its story entirely through item descriptions and letters, never through cutscenes or dialogue options. The items in the catalogue have names and descriptions that accumulate into a portrait of a world outside the room — one the player can infer but never visit. This indirect storytelling approach builds genuine curiosity about a world the game deliberately withholds, making the final revelation of that world feel earned. It's a lesson in how constraint in what the player is shown can build stronger desire to know than direct exposition.

**Tomorrow Corporation's design inheritance.** Kyle Gray, Kyle Gabler, and Allan Blomquist came from World of Goo — a physics puzzle game whose final moments also break genre convention to make a point about consumption. Little Inferno extends this tendency toward design as argument: games that use their mechanics to mean something rather than simply to be enjoyable. Both games demonstrate that the most memorable indie games of the 2010s were often those that used the player's own actions as the medium for their message.
