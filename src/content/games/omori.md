## Design Notes

OMORI's core design strategy is a long con. It spends hours establishing RPG Maker conventions — turn-based battles, overworld exploration, cheerful towns — then uses those conventions as the mechanism for a betrayal.

**Comfort as setup.** The bright colours and chibi art style of Headspace are a deliberate tonal mismatch with the game's subject matter. OMOCAT uses the aesthetic warmth of classic RPGs (Earthbound is an obvious touchstone) to build player comfort, making the shifts into horror and grief more destabilising.

**Colour as emotional language.** Headspace is saturated with colour; the Real World desaturates as the game progresses. Enemy designs in Headspace are childlike abstractions; emotional enemies later in the game are grotesque and personal. The colour palette does expository work so the writing doesn't have to.

**The battle system mirrors psychology.** The game's unique Emotion system (Happy, Sad, Angry) creates rock-paper-scissors combat loops, but also maps directly onto the protagonist's suppressed emotional states. Mechanics are metaphor.

**RPG Maker as aesthetic choice.** OMOCAT was explicit that RPG Maker wasn't a compromise — it was chosen to signal membership in a lineage of personal, DIY games (Yume Nikki, Ib) that use the engine's limitations as part of their atmosphere.
