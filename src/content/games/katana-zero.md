## Design Notes

**Never wasting the player's time.** Justin Stander's stated core design principle is that no player time should be wasted. This manifests in near-instant restarts after death, no loading screens between retries, short level lengths calibrated so that any attempt takes seconds rather than minutes, and dialogue that can be interrupted mid-sentence. That last detail — the player can cut off NPCs before they finish speaking — is a literal implementation of the principle at the smallest scale. Design is often better understood through its smallest details than its largest ones: the dialogue interrupt system is a microcosm of a philosophy applied consistently everywhere in the game.

**Slow-motion as planning, not reaction.** The game's drug-induced time-slowing mechanic is diegetically motivated — the assassin protagonist uses a substance that lets him perceive the future — but its design function is to create a planning phase within the action. Slowing time allows the player to assess enemy positions, projectile trajectories, and attack sequencing before committing. This turns what would otherwise be reflex-based action into a hybrid of planning and execution. The replay system, which shows each completed level as a smooth uninterrupted run, is both a reward and a visual confirmation that the planning was correct — it collapses the player's stop-start problem-solving into the appearance of mastery.

**One-hit death as commitment design.** Both the player character and enemies die in one hit. This symmetry is a design commitment: it creates parity of stakes that makes each engagement feel meaningful on both sides. It also means difficulty is expressed through level design — enemy count and placement — rather than enemy health pools. Unlike games where learning health-pool attack rhythms is the core progression, Katana ZERO's challenge is about information and positioning. The player who dies repeatedly has not yet understood the room, not a player who needs more power. This keeps the skill ceiling about pattern recognition and execution rather than stat accumulation.

**Narrative unreliability as mechanical design.** The story is told from the perspective of an assassin whose memory and perception are distorted by the drug he depends on. This unreliability is not just a story technique; it is mechanically integrated. The player completes missions without understanding their context, mirroring the protagonist's incomplete understanding of his situation. The dialogue choice system is also unreliable: some options that appear to be player choices are revealed to be things the character did not actually say, or things he decided only after the conversation. The game uses the architecture of player choice to create a character who is not fully under the player's control — a precise design tool for expressing a psychological state through a mechanical layer.

**Solo development as design coherence.** Katana ZERO was developed by one person over approximately six years. Solo development constraints are visible in the design: levels are numerous but short; the visual style is detailed but pixel-based; the game is mechanically deep within a narrow domain rather than broad across many systems. But these constraints produced remarkable coherence: the game's aesthetics, mechanics, narrative, and difficulty all reinforce the same emotional register. Solo development makes compromises between competing design visions impossible, and the result is a game that feels authored rather than assembled. The transferable lesson is that design coherence is easier to achieve when the decision-making process is unified, regardless of team size.
