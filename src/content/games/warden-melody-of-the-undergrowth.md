## Design Notes

**N64-era design as an explicit target.** Cardboard Keep stated from the outset that Warden was an attempt to recreate the feeling of third-person action-adventure games from the Nintendo 64 era — specifically games like Banjo-Kazooie and The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time. This is a design target with very specific properties: large, explorable areas filled with environmental puzzles and collectibles, a character with a readable moveset, and a tone that blends whimsy with genuine stakes. Naming a reference era is a useful design constraint because it defines not just what to build but what to exclude; Warden's decisions about pacing, camera, and puzzle structure were all shaped by this explicit reference point.

**Shapeshifting as ability gating.** The player controls a Warden Spirit who can inhabit the bodies of different child characters, each with distinct abilities needed to progress through the environment. This is an ability-gating system in the tradition of Zelda's item acquisition — different powers unlock different parts of the world — but the framing as spirit possession adds a narrative layer to a mechanical structure. The shapeshifting also allows multiple playable characters without requiring multiple complete movesets from scratch; each form is differentiated, but the core movement logic is shared. For a three-person team, this is an economical approach to variety.

**Small-team ambition and its visible costs.** Warden was made by three people across 30 months with a network of remote contributors. The reviews noted both genuine strengths — well-designed puzzles, environments with personality, a clear sense of what the game is trying to be — and evident limitations, particularly in combat, which was described as clunky and slow. This is a common pattern in games made by teams whose ambition exceeds their resources: the parts of the game that received concentrated attention work, and the parts that didn't show. Warden is useful as a design reference precisely because the seams are visible; it clarifies what a three-person team can credibly deliver and what requires either more resources or scope reduction.

**Post-launch iteration through community feedback.** Cardboard Keep was active in addressing community-identified problems after release, using Steam patches to fix issues the player base raised. This is a design practice enabled by digital distribution that was unavailable to the N64-era games Warden took as its model — the ability to ship a good-enough version and then improve it in response to actual player behaviour. Cardboard Keep used this process to close the gap between what they intended and what they shipped. The final game reflects both the team's original vision and the corrections required by real players; for students of small-team development, it's an example of the patch-based design iteration that has become standard in independent game development.

**Australian independent development context.** Warden represents an unusual ambition for Australian independent game development in the mid-2010s — a fully 3D action-adventure with a significant scope, made in Canberra by a team with no publisher backing. The game attracted attention from Australian gaming press specifically because of this context: a 30-month 3D project from a capital-city studio of three people was newsworthy in a development landscape that more commonly produced smaller 2D titles. As a reference point, Warden documents what was achievable for an ambitious Australian indie team at a specific moment in time, and the trade-offs that moment required.
