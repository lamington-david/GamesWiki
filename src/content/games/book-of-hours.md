## Design Notes

**The card as a universal primitive.** Book of Hours inherits Weather Factory's foundational design decision: everything in the game is a card, and every meaningful interaction is a card placed into a slot. Books, skills, people, seasons, memories — all represented as draggable objects in shared space. This collapses the traditional RPG split between inventory, dialogue, and world interaction into a single spatial vocabulary, making the system learnable through feel rather than menu-reading.

**Combat-free does not mean tension-free.** Weather Factory made a deliberate choice to remove combat entirely from Book of Hours, but the design still creates pressure through time, skill availability, and the gradual decay of seasonal cycles. The absence of violence creates space for a different kind of mastery — one rooted in planning, catalogue management, and understanding which books unlock which skills. The game demonstrates that stakes can come from information asymmetry and curiosity rather than health bars.

**Obscurity as invitation.** Like Cultist Simulator before it, Book of Hours withholds explanation as a design feature. The player catalogues books whose effects are opaque until the right skill is applied, and the game's lore accretes through reading rather than cutscenes. This approach asks players to treat not-knowing as pleasurable — a stance that demands trust from the audience and rewards patience with the satisfaction of genuine discovery rather than prompted revelation.

**The library as system metaphor.** Setting the game in a library is not merely aesthetic. The act of cataloguing, restoring, and reading mirrors the player's actual relationship with the game's systems — you learn the world by organising it. This alignment between the character's role and the player's activity creates what Kennedy calls a 'sense of place' where the fiction and the mechanics reinforce the same emotional register.

**Kindness as design constraint.** Kennedy described Book of Hours as "the kindest game I've ever made," and the decision to make it kind shaped nearly every other choice: the removal of fail states, the absence of enemies, the visual warmth of the library setting. Treating kindness as a first-class design goal — rather than a consequence — demonstrates how emotional tone can function as a structural constraint that resolves ambiguity across hundreds of smaller decisions.
