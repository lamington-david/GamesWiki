## Design Notes

**Unfiction as design genre.** Shipwrecked 64 presents itself as a recovered copy of a lost Nintendo 64 game from 1997, developed by the fictional Cogware Games in partnership with the fictional Broadside Animation Studio. This unfiction framing — a fake object with a fake history — shapes every design decision. The interface mimics N64-era menus; graphics degrade like a poorly preserved cartridge; "glitches" are scripted horror events dressed up as technical failures. The unfiction wrapper transforms horror moments from jump scares into discoveries, because players must work out whether something is a designed scare or a genuine bug. That interpretive uncertainty is itself the horror.

**"Every copy is personalized" as gameplay mechanic.** Squeaks D'Corgeh has cited the "every copy of Mario 64 is personalized" iceberg meme as direct inspiration for the game's core loop. Shipwrecked 64 changes across playthroughs: different events trigger in different runs, ARG elements respond to out-of-game actions, and the "real" history behind the fictional game's development reveals itself piecemeal across multiple sessions and community interactions. No single player holds the complete picture, which transforms the game's community into an interpretive layer. The personalization mechanic is structural, not cosmetic — it makes communal play the only way to see the full design.

**Human cruelty as the actual horror.** The game's surface horror — cute mascot animals, an eerie island, N64-era visual degradation — functions as a delivery vehicle for its actual theme: labour exploitation. The lore behind the fictional Broadside Animation Studio involves overworked employees, financial abuse, and corporate indifference to human suffering. This dark subtext hits harder than the game's supernatural elements because it is recognisable. The horror design insight is that real institutional cruelty is more disturbing than fictional monsters, and that mascot characters — already culturally loaded with associations of cheerful corporate entertainment — make the contrast more jarring, not less.

**Mascot horror as critique, not exercise.** The developer has publicly criticised the mascot horror genre as formulaic and wasteful of its premises, arguing that most games in it are poor. Shipwrecked 64 is an argument in game form: it uses the genre's visual language while rejecting its structural conventions (the repeated office-check gameplay, the predictable animatronic scares, the jump scare rhythm). The game takes its setting seriously as a vehicle for narrative and thematic ambition rather than as an aesthetic backdrop for mechanical repetition. The result demonstrates that mascot horror's visual tools are far more flexible than the genre conventions that currently dominate it.

**ARG elements as community-binding design.** Shipwrecked 64's ARG layers — hidden messages, out-of-game resources, community-decoded secrets — serve a design function beyond horror: they transform a solo-played horror game into a shared investigation. Players who discover unsettling elements in isolation don't stay isolated; they bring findings to community hubs where collective knowledge fills the picture. This community-binding structure is particularly effective for horror, because sharing frightening discoveries with others both reinforces the content's impact and creates social bonds around the experience. The ARG isn't promotional material; it is the game's social architecture, and the community is the game's final, largest player.
