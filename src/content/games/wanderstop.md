## Design Notes

**Subverting cozy genre expectations through a resistant protagonist.** Most cozy games assume the player wants to engage with the gentle activities on offer. Wanderstop's central design move is to give you a protagonist, Alta, who actively doesn't want to be there. She's a burned-out fighter who finds herself in a tea shop and resists the cozy world at every turn. This means the mechanics aren't enjoyable in the usual cozy way — they're instruments of a character arc. The player grows to appreciate tea-making not because it's inherently pleasant to play, but because it is genuinely changing Alta. The cozy form becomes a narrative tool rather than a tone.

**Friction without time-based stress.** Davey Wreden and the team identified a core design problem: how do you create meaningful friction in "a mundanity simulator" without resorting to timers or pressure that would contradict the cozy aesthetic? Their answer was emotional friction — the protagonist's resistance — rather than mechanical friction. There are no days running out, no crop yields at risk. The difficulty is emotional: getting Alta to accept stillness. This is a principled design decision about what kind of obstacle belongs in a game about burnout recovery.

**Ritual and repetition as vehicles for meaning.** Lead Designer Steven Margolin built the tea-making around the idea that repeated mundane actions accumulate meaning rather than become boring. Unlike traditional farming sims where repetition serves loop-based progression (upgrade the farm, unlock more crops), in Wanderstop repetition is the point — each cup of tea is a ritual that earns its meaning through recurrence. This is a deliberately different theory of why players keep doing the same thing, focused on emotional resonance rather than systemic reward.

**Burnout as lived experience shaping design.** Davey Wreden has spoken openly about being exhausted after The Stanley Parable and The Beginner's Guide — that burnout is the origin of Wanderstop's themes, not a theme chosen from the outside. This creates a specific kind of authenticity in the design: the game's mechanics of forced rest, of sitting with discomfort rather than solving it, reflect a designer who actually went through what the protagonist goes through. The resulting game is more unsettling than most cozy games precisely because the knowledge behind it is real.

**Cozy mechanics as a design space to interrogate.** The broader lesson Wanderstop offers is that genre mechanics can be subject matter. Rather than using cozy game conventions to create relaxation, Ivy Road used them as material to examine — what do these mechanics assume about the player? What happens when those assumptions are made visible? The game functions as both a cozy game and a critique of what cozy games ask of their players. This mode of working — using a genre's vocabulary to question the genre — has precedent in other Annapurna Interactive titles, but Wanderstop applies it with unusual specificity.
