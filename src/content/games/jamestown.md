## Design Notes

**Authenticity and accessibility as false opposition.** When Final Form Games set out to make a bullet hell shoot-em-up, the prevailing assumption was that accessibility and authenticity were incompatible — making a shmup more approachable meant compromising the difficulty and mechanical depth that defined the genre. The team refused this choice. They studied Cave's catalogue deeply (DoDonPachi, Progear, Deathsmiles), internalised the genre's unstated rules about wave pacing and hitbox size, then deliberately broke the ones they judged to be gatekeepers rather than essential features. The result is a game that shmup veterans recognise as authentic while being genuinely playable for newcomers.

**Setting as creative permission.** The premise — Jamestown colony, but on Mars — is both a marketing hook and a design decision that shaped the whole project. The setting freed the team from obligations to historical accuracy or genre convention, allowing them to write diverse characters, create unusual enemy factions (British colonists, Spanish conquistadors, indigenous Martians), and build a melodramatic story without any of it feeling out of place. The alternate-history framing gave the team permission to be entirely themselves rather than imitators of Japanese developers, which ultimately made the game more distinctive rather than less.

**Co-op design across skill gaps.** Jamestown specifically tackled the problem of players with different shmup competency playing together — a design challenge the genre had historically ignored by simply not having co-op. Their solution centres on the Vaunt system: collecting gold dropped by enemies charges a shared meter that, when activated by any player, multiplies the score for the whole party. Expert players can farm gold aggressively; beginners participate without being a burden. Death doesn't end a co-op run but costs shared lives, and a revive mechanic lets skilled players carry struggling partners. The design creates space for asymmetric skill sessions without making less experienced players feel like liabilities.

**The Western studio's genre study.** Final Form Games made Jamestown with no formal game development background, just years of playing Japanese bullet hell games. The STG Gameside interview reveals how much time the team spent studying "the thousand little unstated rules" of the genre — the specific pacing of enemy waves, the rhythm of boss attack patterns, how hitbox size affects difficulty feel. Getting a shmup right as outsiders required deep engagement with primary sources (the Cave and Raizing catalogues) rather than casual familiarity. This research-intensive approach to genre authenticity is a model for any development team working in a genre that isn't their native tradition.

**Vaunt as score design.** The Vaunt system rewards aggressive play with score multipliers but requires collecting gold at close range, which means flying into danger voluntarily. The system is entirely optional — players can complete the game without ever activating Vaunt — but high scores are unreachable without it. This is elegant score design: Vaunt creates a meaningful skill ceiling without making lower-skill play feel incomplete. The mechanic teaches players to value gold collection organically without mandating it, creating a natural difficulty gradient that scales with player ambition rather than with a mode selection screen.
