## Design Notes

**Sacrifice as mechanic and metaphor.** Inscryption began as a Ludum Dare entry for the theme "Sacrifices Must Be Made," and the sacrifice mechanic — spending weaker creatures to summon stronger ones, paying blood to play powerful cards — never left the design. What changed was the thematic depth around it. By the finished game, sacrifice is not just a resource system but a psychological pattern that the narrative exploits: the player is constantly asked to give up something valued in the present for uncertain future gain, and the cabin horror setting frames these decisions as morally weighted rather than purely strategic. Mechanics and theme being built from the same seed gives Inscryption a coherence that games with grafted-on narratives rarely achieve.

**Meta-narrative design as player honesty.** Inscryption's central structural gambit — that the player is inside a haunted game cartridge, that the card game is not the whole game, that Leshy is not the only observer — works because Mullins understands that meta-design must be earned by the game's willingness to acknowledge the player's intelligence rather than condescend to it. The game rewards players who notice inconsistencies, who investigate the cabin, who trust that nothing is decorative. This is a compact with the audience: "we will not waste your curiosity." Meta-narrative games that fail do so because they use self-referentiality as a trick rather than as a design discipline that demands the whole game be coherent at the level it's claiming to operate on.

**Three different games as a design argument.** Inscryption's three-act structure — the cabin deckbuilder, the retro RPG, the final arc — is not a gimmick but a design argument about the nature of games. Each act uses different mechanics to say different things about what card games are, what players do when they play them, and who is watching. The transition between acts is structured to feel like a rupture rather than a genre change, because Mullins wanted players to experience the disorientation of having their assumptions about a game invalidated mid-play. This teaches a design lesson about player expectation management: you can betray the genre contract if you've established that betrayal is part of the promise.

**Horror through systemic dread rather than jump scares.** Leshy, the cabin's proprietor, generates unease not through jump scares but through knowing too much — referencing things the player did off-screen, responding to actions in ways that imply surveillance, building a context where the rules of the game feel like rules imposed by something that could change them. This dread is mechanical in nature: it comes from the player sensing that the system has agency they don't fully understand. Mullins understood that horror in interactive media can come from the game itself behaving wrongly — breaking the fourth wall from the inside — rather than from audiovisual shock. The monster is the game.

**Game jam origins as structural discipline.** The Ludum Dare prototype that became Inscryption had to be made in 48 hours, which meant every element needed to justify its existence immediately. This compression produced a design core — sacrifice mechanic, card combat, one opponent — that was load-bearing enough to support years of additional development. Mullins' account of expanding the prototype reveals that the work was largely additive: building outward from a mechanically proven foundation rather than redesigning from scratch. The lesson is that a game jam entry that genuinely works as a game — not just as a proof of concept — provides better development security than a design document for a larger game that has never been tested.
