## Design Notes

**Physical coupling as the generative constraint.** PHOGS! makes a single design bet and commits to it entirely: two players share one body. Each player controls one head of a long, stretchy dog, and neither can operate independently. This coupling is not inconvenience layered on top of normal co-op — it is the game's entire generative system. Every puzzle, every comic moment, every source of friction and delight flows from this one constraint. A single strong restriction, held consistently, can produce more interesting design space than a dozen optional systems, because it forces designers and players alike to explore its edges rather than routing around it.

**Communication as mechanical output.** Most co-op games encourage communication as a strategy; PHOGS! forces it as a requirement. If one player tries to proceed without coordinating, they literally cannot move. This transforms communication from a player habit into a designed affordance — the game physically requires players to talk to each other in order to play. Bit Loom described this as deliberate: they always tried to find ways to make communication happen naturally through the mechanics rather than relying on players to organise themselves.

**Relaxed difficulty serving the social experience.** The development team made a conscious decision to pitch puzzle difficulty lower than typical puzzle-platformers. Most puzzles have multiple viable approaches, and the game prioritises discovering what is fun over enforcing what is correct. This lowers the barrier for co-op with a partner of different skill level or experience, which is the game's actual audience: couples, friends, or family members who want to play together. The design position is that the social experience of playing together is the core feature, and puzzles serve that social experience rather than the other way around.

**Iterative prototyping from an absurd premise.** The game began as a concept about tying two players together in real life, evolved through a "Dog Golf" stage, and eventually became the two-headed dog. Each iteration preserved the core insight — shared body, forced cooperation — while discarding the parts that didn't work. Following an absurd premise through multiple iterations without abandoning the underlying mechanic is one of the better models for how distinctive game concepts emerge. The unusual idea is worth protecting while everything around it is allowed to change.

**Theming as puzzle vocabulary.** The game's three worlds — Food, Sleep, and Play — each introduce physics and interactive objects tied to their theme. Food world means rollable or bouncing food items; Sleep world means floaty cloud platforms; Play world means sports equipment. Using theme to telegraph puzzle vocabulary before a mechanic is introduced allows players to predict how objects will behave. The theming is not decoration over puzzles — it is the instruction set for the puzzles, delivered implicitly through environmental language rather than tutorial text.
