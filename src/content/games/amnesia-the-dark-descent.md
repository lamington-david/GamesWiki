## Design Notes

**Helplessness as the primary mechanic.** Thomas Grip's central insight was that horror games failed because they still gave the player weapons. When the player can fight back, fear becomes a resource management problem — a tactical challenge rather than an emotional experience. Amnesia removes all combat options, forcing the player to hide, run, or do nothing at all when monsters appear. This creates a qualitatively different emotional state than armed horror: the player cannot calculate odds, cannot plan a counter, cannot feel competent. They can only endure. The design lesson is that competence and fear are antagonistic — the more capable a game makes the player feel, the less frightened they will be, and the more helpless, the more frightened.

**The sanity meter as feedback on attention.** Amnesia tracks the player's psychological state through a sanity meter that depletes when the player witnesses disturbing events or remains in darkness, and manifests as screen distortion, auditory hallucinations, and movement penalties. This system serves two functions: it mechanically punishes avoidance (the player cannot simply look away from monsters and wait them out) and it externalises the emotional state that Frictional wants the player to feel. The meter is a biofeedback loop — designed to make the player's anxiety visible and consequential, not just atmospheric decoration. When the sanity meter drops, the game becomes harder to play, which means fear is literally a mechanical condition rather than just a mood.

**Physics interaction as investment mechanism.** Amnesia lets the player physically grab, pull, and manipulate objects in the environment — opening doors by grabbing and pulling rather than pressing a button, picking up objects and carrying them. This interaction model serves a psychological function: it creates a sense of physical presence in the space. Players who have touched and moved objects in a room feel they have inhabited that room in a way that button-prompt interaction cannot replicate. When a monster enters a space the player has physically explored and handled, the threat feels personal rather than abstract. Investment comes from interaction, and Frictional engineered interaction specifically to deepen the stakes of fear.

**The monster designed to be heard, not seen.** Amnesia's creatures are most effective when the player cannot clearly see them. The game is designed around audio cues — the sound of movement, breathing, and proximity — rather than visual clarity, and the monsters themselves are deliberately poorly lit and quickly glimpsed. This is not a technical limitation but a design choice: the imagination fills gaps with something scarier than any asset could render. Grip's design principle is that suggested horror is more effective than explicit horror, and the entire encounter design is built around denying the player the clear view that would allow rational assessment. Fear lives in uncertainty; the moment the monster is fully visible, it becomes something that can be understood and therefore something less frightening.

**Breaking genre conventions as design method.** Grip's GDC Europe talk frames Amnesia's design as a series of deliberate rule violations: no combat, no scoring, minimal HUD, non-violent fail conditions. Each violation was intentional and each was the result of asking what the genre's conventions were actually for — whether they served the experience or just accumulated as inherited assumptions. This methodology — auditing existing genre conventions and removing those that don't serve the intended emotional experience — is transferable to any genre. Amnesia demonstrates that the most powerful design moves are often subtractive: not adding features to achieve an effect, but removing those that undermine it.
