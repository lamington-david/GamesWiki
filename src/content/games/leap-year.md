## Design Notes

**The player already knows everything; they just don't know it yet.** Leap Year's central design move is that all of the player's abilities are available from the first moment of play. There are no power-ups, no unlocks, no ability gates. What changes over the course of the game is the player's understanding of what they can already do. This is an inversion of the standard action-platformer progression model, where new abilities are the primary reward. In Leap Year, the reward is comprehension — the player grows more capable not because the character becomes more powerful but because the player becomes more informed. This shifts the game's reward structure from acquisition to discovery.

**Environmental teaching without text.** Linssen built the game's tutorials into its geometry. Clues about hidden abilities appear as words and symbols carved into the background of the world — not in a UI layer, not in a tutorial box, but in the fabric of the environment itself. This is a design decision that maintains the fiction of the world while conveying mechanical information: the world knows its own rules and has encoded them into its surfaces. The technique requires that the designer think simultaneously about what the player needs to know and where in the physical space that information makes structural sense.

**The Sokpop commission model.** Leap Year was commissioned by Sokpop Collective, a Dutch studio that commissions their favourite game makers to produce a game for their monthly catalogue. This model — where the developer is given a brief and a payment but full creative control — produced something that would not have existed under conventional publishing arrangements. Linssen made a game about hidden knowledge and discovery; Sokpop distributed it to an audience already primed to appreciate that kind of design. The commissioning relationship shaped what got made by removing commercial pressure to produce a conventional platformer.

**Density and compactness as design values.** The game's world is small and navigable without a map. Every room exists for a reason; no space is filler. This density is consistent with Linssen's game jam origins, where the constraint of producing a complete game in 48 or 72 hours forced the elimination of anything nonessential. In Leap Year, that discipline produces a game world where the player can hold the entire layout in memory by the end — which matters because the game's puzzles require understanding spatial relationships across the whole map, not just the current room.

**Calendar structure as collectible logic.** Calendar pages scattered through the world serve as collectibles and progress markers without imposing a narrative. The player doesn't know why the pages are there or what the calendar means — the game declines to explain its own fiction — but the calendar as object is immediately legible as something to collect and complete. This is a design shortcut: using a culturally familiar object (a calendar, a book, a set of items) to communicate collectible logic without needing to explain the fictional context. The calendar works because everyone understands what a complete calendar is, without the game needing to tell them.
