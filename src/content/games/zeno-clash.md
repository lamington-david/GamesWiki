## Design Notes

**World-first design: building Zenozoik before the game.** ACE Team's founding concept was not a game but a world — Zenozoik, a surrealist universe that draws on Latin American magical realism, Hieronymus Bosch, and the Bordeu brothers' own imagination. The Zenozoik concept predated any game design decisions by years. When ACE Team moved toward a commercial release, the question was how to design a game within this world, not how to design a world for a game. This order of operations — world-first, game-second — produces a different quality of setting. The world has an internal logic that wasn't invented to justify the gameplay; the gameplay was invented to reveal the world.

**First-person melee as genre choice.** ACE Team chose first-person brawling because the major studios of 2009 had crowded the first-person shooter space, but first-person melee was largely unexplored. This was a genuine strategic insight: if you're a small team with distinctive creative material but constrained resources, finding a genre niche where your competition is thin is worth more than competing in a space where expectations are set by Call of Duty or Halo. The melee decision also served the world: Zenozoik is a world of strange creatures encountered face-to-face, and first-person punching produces a visceral physicality that guns at distance would not.

**The modding-to-indie pipeline.** ACE Team began as modders — their first major project was Batman Doom for Doom 2, followed by The Dark Conjunction for Quake 3. The modding background gave them engine experience, a sense of what could be done with limited resources, and an audience that valued creative ambition over polish. Their first attempt at a commercial game, a larger version of Zenozoik using the Lithtech Jupiter engine, failed because the scope was too large for the team. Scaling that concept down to what became Zeno Clash — shorter, more focused, built on Source via a Valve partnership — was the critical production decision. The postmortem identifies this as the turning point: knowing what to cut was the skill.

**Maintaining artistic vision against commercial pressure.** The postmortem is candid about the pressure to make Zenozoik more legible to conventional audiences — to sand off the stranger edges of the world to make it more marketable. ACE Team's choice to hold to their original vision produced a game that looked like nothing else in 2009 and that is still visually distinctive today. The lesson is not that commercial instincts are always wrong, but that distinctiveness at launch is a form of marketing: a game that looks completely unlike everything else gets coverage, gets discussed, gets remembered. The Zenozoik world has been ACE Team's most durable asset across multiple games.

**The failed prototype as design education.** Before Zeno Clash, ACE Team built a demo of a much larger Zenozoik RPG using Lithtech that never found a publisher and was eventually abandoned. The postmortem treats this as foundational: the failed prototype forced the team to understand what was actually compelling about the world versus what was overhead they'd added to seem more commercial. When they built Zeno Clash, they knew what Zenozoik was actually for — because they'd already built a version that didn't work. Prototyping to failure, and learning from what the failure reveals, is a recurring pattern in successful indie development.
