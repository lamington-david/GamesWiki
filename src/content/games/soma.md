## Design Notes

SOMA's design challenge was using horror as a philosophical delivery mechanism without losing either the horror or the philosophy. Thomas Grip wanted players to feel personally threatened by ideas — specifically by the question of whether they would still be themselves after consciousness transfer.

**Staging the dilemma before the choice.** SOMA doesn't spring its moral confrontations on players. It carefully establishes the mechanics and rules of its world (how scans, copies, and transfers work) before placing the player in a situation where those rules produce tragedy. The horror comes from understanding the situation fully, not from surprise.

**Monsters as tragedy, not threat.** SOMA's most effective monsters are pitiful rather than menacing. They are machines or crew members whose consciousness has fractured — they believe they are human, they are wrong, and their behaviour is the result of that delusion. The horror is empathetic, not predatory.

**The value of no correct answer.** Frictional deliberately designed SOMA's major choices to have no good outcome. The game doesn't reward players who make the "right" choice with a better ending — it removes that comfort entirely. This is what gives the ending its weight: there is no version where the protagonist wins.

**Safe Mode as design statement.** The post-launch addition of Safe Mode (where monsters can't hurt you) generated significant debate. Grip's position was that the monster-based threat had become a barrier to the story for some players, and the story was the point. This is worth considering: when is mechanical difficulty in service of the experience, and when is it in the way?
