## Design Notes

**Hybrid genre design as identity.** Rock of Ages is not a tower defense game with a gimmick, nor is it a racing game with defenses bolted on — it genuinely requires both skill sets simultaneously. The player builds tower defenses during the opponent's rolling phase and rolls their boulder during the opponent's build phase, meaning the design is always asking the player to do two different things in alternation. This creates a rhythm distinctive from either source genre. Designing a hybrid that requires mastery of both halves, rather than allowing players to ignore one, is the structural challenge ACE Team solved here.

**Comedy as design coherence.** Rock of Ages is genuinely funny, and the humor is not decorative — it is what holds together a game that would otherwise be conceptually incoherent. Rolling a giant boulder through Botticelli's The Birth of Venus is, aesthetically, a collision that demands an explanation, and absurdist comedy provides it. ACE Team built the entire visual language around Monty Python-style animation and historical art parody, which created a unified world where boulder-physics destroying Renaissance paintings makes complete sense. The design lesson is that tone can serve as structural glue: a consistent comedic voice makes unlikely combinations feel inevitable.

**Art history as level design palette.** Each period and location in Rock of Ages uses the visual conventions of a specific historical era — ancient Greece, medieval Europe, the Renaissance — as both aesthetic and structural framing. This gives the game a built-in visual variety that required no original world-building: each new historical setting arrives with a fully formed visual identity and established cultural associations. Players bring existing knowledge to each era that the game can play against. This is a form of design economy: borrowing cultural capital rather than building it from scratch.

**Small studio pitching at GDC.** ACE Team's account of pitching Rock of Ages is instructive. They went to GDC without a playable demo — they had a two-minute trailer and a one-page document listing features, platforms, and development timeline. Atlus signed them. This is a specific kind of pitch discipline: distilling an unusual concept to its most legible form for a five-minute conversation. Carlos Bordeu has noted that Rock of Ages was easy to explain in a sentence ("you roll a boulder through an enemy's tower defenses") in a way that Zeno Clash — a first-person brawler in a surreal prehistoric world — was not. Pitchability shaped which project got made.

**Chilean indie development in a global market.** ACE Team is one of a small number of game studios from Latin America to achieve significant international distribution in the early 2010s, and Rock of Ages was their highest-profile release at that point. The studio's design identity — absurdism, genre experimentation, maximalist world-building — does not map obviously onto any regional game design tradition, and this independence from local or industry norms may be part of why their work is so distinctive. For designers interested in how geography relates to creative identity, ACE Team is a useful case study in what happens when a studio develops in relative isolation from industry hubs.
