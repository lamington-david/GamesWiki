## Design Notes

**Constraints as creative fuel.** The Fullbright Company built Gone Home with a team of four and a production budget that made conventional game systems impossible. Steve Gaynor has said that having immovable constraints is crucial to doing something worthwhile — the inability to make a long game, a combat game, or a puzzle game forced the entire team's energy toward what they could do: invest in the emotional texture of a single space. The Victorian house became the whole game not as a design choice but as a production reality, and the concentration of effort in that one location produced a density of storytelling detail that a larger, more varied game would have diluted.

**Level design as psychological architecture.** The Greenbriar house was designed using principles borrowed from immersive sim level design, where spatial layout does rhetorical work. Victorian houses were chosen specifically because they create a natural hub-and-spoke structure — a central foyer with hallways and stairways radiating outward — giving players multiple paths without requiring explicit waypoints or map systems. Kate Craig and Gaynor's GDC talk shows how individual rooms were art-directed as character portraits: the mother's study communicates her emotional state; Sam's room communicates teenage interiority through its specific arrangement of objects. The level is the character.

**Environmental storytelling as the only storytelling.** Gone Home tells its story through objects arranged in space. Players find no cutscenes, no living characters to speak to, no text dumps explaining events. What they find are journals, notes, receipts, photos, and possessions — the material residue of lives recently lived. The discipline required to tell a coherent story this way is significant: every object must carry meaning and must be readable to a player who has not been told what to look for. This created a design process where the writing and level design were inseparable — the writers had to think in spatial terms, and the level designers had to think in narrative terms.

**Linearity disguised as exploration.** Despite its apparently open house, Gone Home sequences its revelations carefully. The story's emotional climax depends on the player building a specific picture of Sam's situation over time, and the house's layout guides discovery without enforcing it. Gaynor describes this as making linear design feel non-linear: the path through the story is essentially fixed, but players feel agency because they can examine anything in any order. The technique is to ensure that partial information at any point is still meaningful, so that whatever the player finds first feels like the right starting point.

**The absent family as design device.** The choice to make all major characters absent — known only through their traces — is both a narrative and a production decision. An absent character can be whoever the player needs them to be for the story to land emotionally, and they can be written with a specificity that a voiced, animated character would flatten. Sam, the sister whose story the player uncovers, becomes more vivid as an absence than most game protagonists do as presences. This technique of telling character through environment rather than through direct presentation is one of Gone Home's most widely imitated contributions to the field.
