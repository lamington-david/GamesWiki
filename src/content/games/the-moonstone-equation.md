## Design Notes

**Eight years as a spare-time project.** The Moonstone Equation is the product of a single developer working evenings and weekends for roughly eight years, releasing in 2018. This extended timeline has unusual properties: Andy Buck's skills improved substantially between early and late production, the aesthetic goals could evolve to match growing capability, and the absence of external deadlines allowed the game to be finished on its own terms. Most commercial development timelines compress this process artificially, shipping at a point determined by budget rather than readiness. The Moonstone Equation is an extreme case study in what patience allows.

**Custom engine as design enabler.** Rather than use an existing game engine, Buck built a custom engine from scratch to present high-definition pixel art using modern rendering techniques. The reasoning: available engines would either produce results that didn't match the visual target, or require workarounds that added complexity without artistic benefit. For solo developers with specific technical-aesthetic goals, the cost of building custom tools can be offset by the precise control they enable — though this is a high-risk decision that extends timelines enormously and requires sustained technical motivation across years of development.

**Exploration without threat.** The Moonstone Equation has no enemies and no conventional fail states. Players are free to explore the facility and discover puzzles in any order, with nothing narrowing their attention toward avoidance and survival. The design influence of Ico — which Buck cited alongside Fez and The Talos Principle — is visible here: the player's orientation in the world comes from curiosity and observation rather than from danger. This positions the game in a design tradition of environmental puzzlers that use wonder as their primary motivator. The tradeoff is that without external pressure, some players lack a reason to push forward through difficulty.

**Puzzle vocabulary from Fez and The Talos Principle.** The game's puzzle design draws from two distinct lineages: Fez's spatial and code-based mysteries (puzzles that reward reading the game's own visual grammar as a language) and The Talos Principle's structured logical challenges. Combining these influences in a game without clear tutorial structure requires trusting players to construct their own understanding of the game's rules — a design bet that rewards exploratory players while potentially alienating those who need scaffolded introduction to each system.

**Market timing across long development cycles.** Extended solo development timelines carry a specific commercial risk: a game designed to tastes and conventions current at the start of production may land in a substantially different market at release. The Moonstone Equation began around 2010, a period before the indie puzzle explosion that followed games like The Witness and The Talos Principle, and released in 2018 into a much more crowded field. This is a structural challenge of long solo development that no amount of quality can fully address — the game that was innovative in concept may be one of many similar games by the time it ships.
