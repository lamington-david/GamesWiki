## Design Notes

**Mystery as the primary design goal.** Kentucky Route Zero deliberately replaced traditional adventure game structures — locks and keys, puzzles with solutions, goals and payoffs — with a design that prioritises unknowing. Cardboard Computer's GDC 2014 talk shows specific moments from the game that were redesigned to remove their resolutions: a puzzle that originally had an answer became one that does not. The result is that the game consistently gives the player things to wonder about rather than things to solve. This is not a failure of puzzle design but a principled rejection of it, with mystery replacing mastery as the player's relationship to the world.

**Scenography over level design.** Art director Tamas Kemenczy draws explicitly from theatrical scenography — designing stage environments for specific emotional and psychological effect — rather than from game level design traditions. Kentucky Route Zero's spaces quote specific architectural and cinematic references: brutalist buildings, Edward Hopper paintings, American Gothic imagery. Lighting defines planes of depth the way a stage manager uses gels and spots. The effect is environments that feel composed rather than built, staged rather than simulated, which produces a very different relationship between player movement and environmental atmosphere.

**Dialogue as character, not strategy.** The game's dialogue choices are generally among variations in tone, emphasis, or interpretation rather than between options with different narrative outcomes. Choosing Conway's words is not selecting a strategy; it is participating in the authorship of a character. This approach treats the player as a collaborator in writing a person rather than a director of story events. It strips dialogue of its strategic dimension — tracking relationship meters, optimising outcomes — and replaces it with something closer to how a reader inhabits a first-person novel, choosing between ways of expressing the character's voice.

**Magical realism as environmental logic.** The game's world operates on magical realist logic — a bureaucratic office exists underground, a band accompanies the player without explanation, the highway itself is impossible — but treats these elements with the same flatness as mundane details. The magic is never explained or marked as special; it coexists with the ordinary without being narratively set apart. By refusing to treat the strange as strange, the game asks players to adopt a mode of attention receptive to both the ordinary and the extraordinary simultaneously, which is the attention that American folk and literary traditions of magical realism cultivate.

**Serialization as form.** Kentucky Route Zero was released in five acts over seven years (2013–2020), and the structure of its episodes is inseparable from its meaning. The time between acts was real time; early players who returned for Act V were different people. Inter-act content was released as free web experiments — short games, videos, interactive fiction — that expanded the world without advancing the plot. This approach treats incompleteness as a formal property of the work rather than a production failure, drawing on the serial novel tradition and the experience of living through ongoing events. It is a design model for games that want to occupy a span of real time rather than a session.
