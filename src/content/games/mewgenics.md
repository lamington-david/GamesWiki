## Design Notes

**Genetics as a systemic design engine.** Mewgenics is built around a single generative idea: cats inherit traits from parents, and those traits meaningfully alter gameplay in tactical combat. A cat with the 'brittle' trait takes increased damage but costs fewer resources to field; a cat with 'nine lives' survives its first lethal hit; trait combinations interact and sometimes contradict. This genetic system is not cosmetic — it is the core design engine. Every run begins with a different genetic pool, and the player's goal is less to master a fixed set of characters than to identify what their current genetic hand is capable of and how to build a team around it. The breeding system gives McMillen an endless supply of novel problem sets without requiring new content.

**Fourteen years to the right system.** The original Mewgenics was announced as a Team Meat project in 2012, cancelled as McMillen and Tommy Refenes went separate ways, and restarted with Tyler Glaiel in 2018. The game that shipped in 2026 kept only two elements from the original concept: cat breeding and the house setting. Everything else was rebuilt from scratch, including the combat system (which shifted from brawler to RTS to turn-based tactics before reaching a final form) and the visual design. The 14-year arc is not a cautionary tale about development hell — it is an account of a designer refusing to ship until the central systems actually worked. The willingness to discard years of work when the design wasn't right is McMillen's most consistent creative trait.

**Skipping Early Access as a design decision.** Tyler Glaiel's clearest articulation of a production design choice: they deliberately avoided Steam Early Access. His reasoning was that Early Access fragments the player community across versions, robbing players of the shared discovery experience that makes a new systemic game culturally interesting. When Mewgenics launched, everyone encountered the same systems for the first time simultaneously — conversations, guides, and community knowledge developed together rather than in staggered waves. The game's first week, which produced 1 million sales and recouped development costs within hours, validated the bet. The lesson is that launch timing is itself a design decision with real consequences for community formation.

**Autobiographical design with difficult material.** McMillen's family experience — he is the parent of a child with medical complexities — directly informed the game's genetics system and its willingness to model health conditions, pain, and disability as meaningful cat traits. His design stance is that refusing to include difficult subject matter in games is a form of condescension: both to players who live with those realities and to the medium's potential for honest representation. The viral response to Mewgenics — some players horrified, many neurodivergent players describing the trait system as the first time a game made them feel seen — illustrates the specific creative risk and reward of design that draws from autobiographical truth rather than avoiding it.

**Combinatorial depth without content bloat.** Mewgenics launched with over 100 traits, multiple cat classes, and a full breeding system — but the depth this produces is not a function of raw content volume. It is a function of trait interactions, many of which were not explicitly designed but emerged from the combinatorial space. McMillen's description of the game as having an "endless design space" refers to this property: the trait interactions generate more possible gameplay situations than any content team could deliberately author. This is the systemic design philosophy he developed through The Binding of Isaac — more items interacting simply produces more gameplay than fewer items interacting complexly — applied to its fullest expression in a genre (turn-based tactics) where individual character capability is the primary strategic variable.
