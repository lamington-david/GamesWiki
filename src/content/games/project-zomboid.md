## Design Notes

**Mortality as a design pillar.** Project Zomboid opens with the words "This is how you died," and that premise is not rhetorical. Death is not a failure state to be avoided through skill; it is an expected outcome around which the entire game is designed. Characters accumulate skills, traits, and relationships that are lost on death, making every survivor feel genuinely individual and every death genuinely costly. This philosophy — sometimes called the "This Is How You Died" design position — produces a fundamentally different relationship between player and game than survival games that design death as a setback to be reversed. The cost of death is the point; survival is meaningful because it is precarious.

**Systemic depth over scripted scenarios.** Project Zomboid generates its drama entirely from systems rather than authored events. There are no story missions, no unique objectives, and no choreographed set pieces. The zombie horde behaves according to rules: they react to sound, cluster around light sources, and accumulate in areas of disturbance. Player characters have physical states — hunger, thirst, fatigue, illness, depression — that interact with each other and with the environment. The complexity comes from the intersection of these systems rather than from content authored on top of them. When something dramatic happens in Project Zomboid, it is a product of the simulation, not a developer's script.

**Parameterised difficulty as respect for player intent.** Before entering the game world, players configure an extraordinary range of variables: zombie density and aggression, starting conditions, resource availability, time progression, and character traits covering everything from athletic ability to psychological resilience. This is not a difficulty slider — it is a full expression of what game the player wants to play. The Indie Stone's design position is that a survival game should serve many different visions of what survival means, from accessible and story-driven to extreme and unforgiving. Parameterisation is how they honour those different visions without compromising the game's systemic integrity.

**Long-duration Early Access as community design.** Project Zomboid entered Early Access in 2013 and remained there for over a decade, receiving regular updates throughout. The development model produced an unusual relationship between game and player community: the game's systems were refined in public, with community feedback directly shaping feature priorities and design decisions. The modding ecosystem that emerged from this — enabled by the Indie Stone deliberately exposing their underlying systems — became one of the game's most significant features, with mods addressing everything from quality-of-life improvements to complete gameplay overhauls. The lesson is that building in public, over a long timeline, can produce games that are better calibrated to their audience than any internal playtesting program.

**Zombie film theory as design source.** The Indie Stone explicitly drew inspiration from zombie films rather than zombie games, citing George Romero's slow, shambling hordes as their primary reference rather than the fast-zombie action of contemporaries. This distinction carries design implications throughout the game: Zomboid's zombies are individually manageable but collectively lethal, dangerous through accumulation and circumstance rather than through offensive capability. The horror comes from the same place it does in Romero's films — from resource management, from bad decisions made under stress, from the gap between what the protagonist knows and what the situation requires. This fidelity to a filmic source rather than a genre convention produced a survival game with a distinct tonal register.
