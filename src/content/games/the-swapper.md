## Design Notes

**Clay-built world as philosophical metaphor.** The game's most immediately striking design decision is its handmade visual aesthetic. Facepalm Games — two Finnish university students, Olli Harjola and Otto Hantula — built environments from clay models and real-world found objects, then photographed and composited them. This was partly a practical solution to limited resources, but the effect is precisely right for the subject matter: a space station that feels ancient and organic, touched by human hands, gives the act of swapping between bodies a visceral weight it wouldn't carry in a cleaner digital world. The aesthetic choice and the thematic content reinforce each other in a way that feels designed, not accidental.

**Clone mechanics that foreground identity.** The core mechanic — a device that creates up to four clones of the protagonist, between which the player can swap control — is elegant because it immediately raises questions the game then uses as philosophical fuel. If you can create identical copies of yourself and switch between them at will, what makes "you" special? The puzzles are not just logic problems; each one asks the player to treat their copies as expendable objects, generating an uncomfortable cognitive dissonance that the narrative then examines directly. Mechanics that produce ethical discomfort as a byproduct of play — rather than through explicit moralising — are rare and worth studying.

**Metroidvania structure as expanding awareness.** The game's non-linear world design — a sprawling space station where new abilities unlock previously inaccessible areas — functions not just as a progression mechanic but as a metaphor for expanding understanding. As the player gains access to new regions, the station's mystery deepens rather than resolves. The open structure rewards curiosity and backtracking, mirroring the game's thematic investment in exploration as a mode of knowledge. The ability to pace revelations across a non-linear space — giving players the feeling of uncovering rather than being told — is one of the game's strongest structural achievements.

**Narrative integration by a dedicated writer.** The Swapper hired Tom Jubert (FTL, Driver: San Francisco) as a dedicated narrative designer — unusual for a two-person indie project. Jubert's approach was to embed philosophical questions about consciousness into environmental storytelling: monoliths scattered across the station pose questions without providing answers, and the plot's climax deliberately refuses easy resolution. His postmortem is candid about what worked (letting gameplay surface the themes) and what didn't (the density of written text relative to what players actually read). It remains a model case study in integrating narrative design with puzzle mechanics.

**Scope discipline as the enabling constraint.** One of The Swapper's clearest lessons is what focused scope enables. Two students, working part-time, produced a complete and polished six-hour experience by committing to a single elegant mechanic and building every element around it. The clay aesthetic eliminated the need for a dedicated artist; the puzzle design was deep rather than varied; the narrative worked with the gameplay constraints rather than against them. Games that succeed on small teams typically share this property — they find the one thing they do, and only that, rather than attempting to be many things at once.
