## Design Notes

**Digital-first design as liberation from physical constraints.** Eternal was built by people who had spent careers playing and designing paper card games, and who chose digital specifically because it removed constraints they found limiting. Cards in Eternal can do things physically impossible on a tabletop: count the exact number of cards in both players' decks in real time, shuffle themselves into a library, transform into different cards mid-game, or have abilities that trigger based on the game state in ways that would require a judge ruling at a paper table. The game is a thought experiment in what card game design looks like when the question "but how would you actually do this physically?" has no bearing. This produces some of Eternal's most interesting mechanics and some of its most cognitively demanding interactions.

**Factions as a design organisation system.** Eternal's five factions — Fire, Time, Justice, Primal, and Shadow — fill a role similar to Magic: The Gathering's five colours, giving each card a philosophical and mechanical identity that makes a large card set learnable by associating mechanical behaviour with faction identity. Fire cards are aggressive and direct; Time cards ramp resources and play large threats; Justice cards remove threats and buff units; Primal cards disrupt the opponent and draw cards; Shadow cards exchange life for power. New players can orient themselves by thinking about what kind of game they want to play and finding the faction that matches. The five-faction system is borrowed from Magic but extended to allow multi-faction cards more freely, which produces richer deck building decisions.

**The power system and avoiding mana frustration.** Eternal uses a resource system ("Power") derived from Magic's mana model but modified to reduce the game's most frustrating variance. Players can draft "Sigils" (basic power cards) at any point during deck building, and the game's mulligan system ensures players see power cards more reliably than in paper Magic. The design also removes "mana screw" scenarios through specific cards and mechanics that convert excess power into other resources. Dire Wolf's explicit design goal was to preserve the strategic interest of a mana-like resource curve while reducing the proportion of games lost to unplayable starting hands — a longstanding point of contention in Magic's community. The result is a resource system that trades some of Magic's strategic tension for reduced randomness in the early game.

**Combat designed around blocking as default.** In Magic: The Gathering, attacking players choose which creatures attack; defending players choose which creatures block. In Eternal, all units attack simultaneously during the attack step, and all units block unless the attacking player activates the "warcry" ability. This default-blocking design changes the combat calculus significantly: trading creatures is easier, board state matters more relative to cards in hand, and combat math is simpler because both players can immediately see what will die. The change is a deliberate design decision about where Eternal's strategic complexity should live — in deck construction and spell use rather than in combat step sequencing.

**A studio built by competitive players.** Dire Wolf Digital was founded specifically to make digital card games, staffed by people with competitive TCG backgrounds including professional Magic players. This heritage shapes Eternal's design in visible ways: the game's competitive format is taken seriously, the card design avoids obviously broken interactions in ways that suggest playtesting with tournament-level play in mind, and the team's communication with the community reflects an understanding of competitive card game culture. The lesson is that a design team's background produces a game in its own image — studios staffed by competitive players make games for competitive players, even when they intend otherwise.
