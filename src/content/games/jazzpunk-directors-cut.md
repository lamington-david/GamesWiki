## Design Notes

**Comedy as a primary design metric.** Jazzpunk was not designed to have a good story with jokes in it — it was designed to be funny, and everything else was subordinated to that goal. Luis Hernandez describes this realisation as transformative: once the team acknowledged that comedy was the game's actual purpose, the design process changed. They could evaluate decisions by asking whether they were funny rather than whether they were narratively coherent or mechanically satisfying. This gave the team a clear filter for cutting content and a clear criterion for prototyping new interactions. Comedy density — jokes per square metre of game space — became a practical design metric, and the game's relatively short runtime is a consequence of prioritising that density over length.

**The pivot from serious to absurd.** Jazzpunk's development began in 2007 as a straightforward cyberpunk espionage puzzle game with portal-style first-person mechanics and a noir aesthetic. The comedy elements were introduced as moments of relief within that serious framework. Hernandez has described the turning point: the team kept playing the funny parts rather than the serious parts, and eventually admitted to themselves where their actual interests lay. The pivot from serious game with comic relief to comedy game with a retrofuturist setting is a significant design lesson — understanding what a project actually is, rather than what it was intended to be, is often the most important creative decision a team can make.

**Art direction as tonal instrument.** Jazzpunk's visual language — flat graphic design influenced by mid-century designers like Saul Bass and Gerd Arntz, a limited palette of vivid solids, simplified geometry — creates a world that signals its own unreality. This is not stylisation for style's sake: the graphic flatness communicates that the world operates on comedy logic rather than simulation logic, preparing players to accept the absurd before they encounter it. When a character is replaced by an icon or an environment is sketched rather than rendered, the art is doing tonal work. The visual register makes the comedy structurally legible rather than structurally dissonant.

**Analog audio for a digital world.** Hernandez recorded Jazzpunk's music, voice, and sound design using analog hardware synthesisers and effects processors, deliberately replicating the audio production methods of the 1950s and 1960s. The result is a soundscape that feels materially of the game's retrofuturist setting rather than designed for it. This creates cohesion between the audio and visual registers — both communicate the same era, the same aesthetic logic, the same comedic tone. The lesson for audio design is that how sound is produced, not just what it sounds like, shapes the emotional register of a game space.

**Interaction as punchline delivery.** Many of Jazzpunk's jokes are delivered through interaction rather than dialogue or cutscene — touching an object produces an unexpected response, attempting a standard game action produces an unexpected outcome. This design strategy distributes the comedy throughout the environment rather than concentrating it in scripted sequences. Players who explore thoroughly find more jokes than players who move toward objectives; curiosity is rewarded with additional gags. The game treats first-person interaction mechanics — picking up, activating, examining — as a vocabulary for setting up and delivering comedy, which represents an extension of what first-person game space can communicate beyond information and challenge.
