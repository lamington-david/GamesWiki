## Design Notes

**The protagonist swap as a complete design rethink.** Season One placed the player as Lee, an adult man with physical competence and an explicit protective relationship with Clementine. Every system — combat, persuasion, resource gathering — was designed around a capable adult. When Season Two made Clementine the protagonist, it wasn't a simple skin swap. Director Dennis Lenart noted that Clementine deals with people and zombies "very, very differently" from Lee, which forced a redesign of every action encounter to foreground her vulnerabilities rather than her capabilities. The design lesson is that protagonist age and physical capacity aren't character details — they're systemic constraints that ripple into every mechanic.

**A different kind of agency.** In Season One, the player as Lee had a clear goal: protect Clementine. This gave every choice a moral anchor. In Season Two, with Clementine as protagonist, writer Mark Darin described the design goal as giving the player "a different sort of agency" — instead of deciding how to protect someone, the player now decides who to trust to protect them. This is a subtle but significant shift: the agency moves from actor to evaluator. The player's power is judgment rather than action, which reflects Clementine's position as a child in a world that doesn't accommodate children.

**Designing the illusion of consequence.** The Walking Dead's famous "Clementine will remember that" messages trained players to believe their choices had weight. Season Two revealed the design seam more clearly than Season One — characters didn't always honour player decisions, and story beats arrived regardless of prior choices. This gap between the game's promise and its delivery became a studied design failure: the interactive narrative form requires not just the appearance of consequence but mechanical systems that enforce it, otherwise the player's sense of agency collapses at the moments that matter most.

**Episodic structure as emotional tension.** Telltale's episodic model was, at its best, a delivery mechanism for cliffhangers and anticipation — the television logic of a season finale applied to games. Each episode was designed to end on an unresolved emotional state, creating the experience of waiting for a story to continue rather than stopping a game. This structure trades the game's control over pacing for a form of sustained investment unavailable to single-release titles. The practical design challenge was making each episode feel satisfying on its own terms while leaving enough unresolved to compel the next purchase.

**Moral ambiguity without judgment.** Across both seasons, Telltale's core design commitment was to avoid constructing a 'correct' path through the story. No choice is coded as objectively right; the game doesn't punish or reward based on a hidden moral score. This required writers to construct scenarios where multiple options were genuinely defensible and to resist the temptation to tilt the narrative toward one outcome. The result was a story that players argued about intensely after the fact — which is precisely what the design intended. The measure of a good moral choice in a narrative game is whether players still disagree about it years later.
