## Design Notes

**Kyogoku's formula transplanted to social media.** Urban Myth Dissolution Center draws its core structure from Natsuhiko Kyogoku's Hyakki Yagyō mystery novels, in which murders are framed through yōkai folklore and resolved through a form of exorcism — the expert who correctly identifies what myth a community has displaced onto a real event can dissolve it. Hakaba Bunko transplants this structure to contemporary Japan: instead of yōkai circulating through village rumour, urban myths spread on social media before Azami's agency is called in. This is a clean example of adapting a literary structure to a new setting while preserving the underlying logic that makes it work as mystery design. The formula survived the transfer because it is structural, not cosmetic.

**Social media as narrative delivery and game mechanic simultaneously.** The game uses social media feeds as the primary channel through which cases arrive — the agency learns about an urban legend from online posts and community discussion before investigating on the ground. This collapses three functions into a single interface: setting detail (information spreads this way in contemporary Japan), narrative delivery mechanism (cases unfold through posts, comments, and shared videos), and a game system (reading social media carefully is part of solving the mystery). Designers who want to integrate a contemporary technology into game design should study how Urban Myth Dissolution Center avoids making the social media element feel grafted on.

**The 'junk food' design philosophy.** In interviews, Hakaba Bunko described their goal as making something like junk food — a pleasurable, undemanding episodic experience rather than a prestige game with serious aspirations. This conscious calibration of tone kept the production scope manageable and gave the team a clear rejection criterion: anything that required the game to be weightier than its premise allowed was cut. The commercial result — over 300,000 units sold, awards recognition — suggests that honest scope-setting can compete with more ambitious productions. Designers often underestimate how much audiences value work that knows exactly what it is and delivers on that promise without apology.

**Pixel art as production strategy, not nostalgia.** The game's pixel art is not deployed for retro appeal — it is functional. Character portraits in pixel art can express emotional states clearly at low production cost. Background environments can be made distinct and readable without 3D asset pipelines. For a small studio working on an episodic structure with multiple cases and locations, pixel art is a scaling strategy: it allows more content at lower cost while maintaining a legible visual identity. The game's success with mainstream audiences demonstrates that pixel art, handled with care and confidence, reads as deliberate style rather than technical limitation.

**Episodic case structure as design architecture.** Urban Myth Dissolution Center structures itself as discrete cases rather than a single continuous narrative, following the Ace Attorney model of self-contained episodes building toward a larger arc. Each case has its own cast, setting, and urban legend at its center, which allows players to feel completion per episode and the team to design each unit independently with a clear scope. The episodic structure suits the social media theme: in the same way that online stories circulate, peak, and are forgotten, each case is a self-contained event that the agency resolves before the next legend surfaces. Form and content reinforce each other in a way that feels organic rather than designed.
