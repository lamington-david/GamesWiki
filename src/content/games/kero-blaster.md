## Design Notes

**Rebuilding rather than releasing.** Kero Blaster began as Gero Blaster, a different game Amaya announced at BitSummit 2013 and then quietly discarded because he wasn't satisfied with how it turned out. Rather than ship something he considered flawed, he started over and rebuilt it into Kero Blaster. This is a significant creative decision: Amaya consistently prioritises the quality of the player experience over momentum or sunk cost. The willingness to discard a year of work and rebuild is part of why his games feel considered rather than assembled.

**Staged skill development as structure.** Every element in Kero Blaster — bosses, stages, weapon upgrades — was designed with player skill development in mind. The order in which new mechanics appear, the spacing of upgrades, the placement of difficult encounters: all of it is sequenced to guide the player from uncertainty to competence without a tutorial. This is the same approach Amaya used in Cave Story. The game doesn't tell players what to do; it creates a sequence of situations in which the necessary skills can be discovered and then practised until they feel natural.

**Collaboration as motivation management.** Cave Story was made entirely by Amaya alone over five years. Kero Blaster involved Kiyoko Kawanaka contributing stage ideas — the clock boss and the car boss both originated from her sketches. Amaya has said that working with another person helped him stay motivated across the production. This is a practical insight about solo development: the isolation that enables creative control also enables stagnation, and structured external input, even limited, can provide the external friction needed to keep a project moving. The frog-driving-a-handcar image in the game came from a Kawanaka doodle Amaya found useful enough to include directly.

**Pixel art under constraint.** Amaya designed Kero Blaster's characters with a deliberately limited colour count, a choice that became more difficult to maintain as the game's later stages introduced more complex environments. This is an honest account of how constraints work in practice: they generate coherence and visual identity in the early going, but they have costs when the scope of the game demands more visual variety than the constraint allows. The limited palette is part of what gives Kero Blaster its distinctive warmth — the frog character reads as loveable rather than merely functional — but it required careful management to sustain across a longer game than Cave Story.

**Gameplay before narrative.** At GDC 2011, Amaya articulated a design principle he has held consistently: start with the game, not with the story. If the player is having fun, they will play; if they're playing, that is when they want to know the story. This ordering resists the instinct to front-load narrative, which is common in games that struggle with engagement. In Kero Blaster, the frog's workplace comedy premise is established quickly but lightly; the game earns its stranger later moments by first establishing that its mechanics are worth playing through. The story serves the game, not the reverse.
