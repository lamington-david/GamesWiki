## Design Notes

**Subjective perception as the visual design system.** Most games that deal with mental illness describe it in text or cutscenes. Milk outside a bag externalises it as the player's visual environment. Colours and shapes are distorted, environments shift unreliably, and perspective breaks in ways that reflect the protagonist's dissociation rather than any objective space. This is a distinct design approach: rather than representing mental illness as subject matter, the game makes the player experience distorted perception directly. The horror comes not from external threats but from the unreliability of the environment the player must navigate — a direct translation of dissociative experience into a player-facing problem.

**Deliberate ambiguity as structural design.** Kryukov has described his approach to storytelling as hiding the main plot behind multiple sub-layers, writing open endings, and stripping the story of direct explanations. This is not narrative laziness; it is a specific theory of how the game should function. The Milk series is designed to reward multiple playthroughs and interpretations, with meaning distributed across visual cues, word choice, and branching paths rather than concentrated in explicit revelations. The player constructs the story from fragments, and different players construct different stories. This demands writing precise enough that interpretations remain coherent, even without a single authoritative reading.

**Genre hybridisation to represent interiority.** The sequel's structure moves between visual novel and point-and-click adventure — the player reads the protagonist's internal narration, then explores her dream-space by clicking on objects that trigger memories. This hybrid form is not arbitrary: the visual novel segments represent conscious narration (the protagonist talking herself through getting milk), while the point-and-click sequences represent the unconscious (dreams, memories, things the protagonist isn't directly saying). The mechanic shift signals a shift in what layer of consciousness the player is accessing. Genre is used as a formal indicator of psychological register.

**Musical thinking applied to game writing.** Kryukov, who also composes the games' soundtracks, describes his approach to dialogue as treating word combinations like rhythmic or musical phrases. The cadence of how the protagonist speaks is as designed as what she says. This produces writing that reads unusually — the sentences are clipped, sometimes grammatically strange, with an obsessive quality that matches the protagonist's mental state. The dialogue's strangeness is doing character work that conventional well-formed prose cannot. The lesson for writers: the formal properties of language (rhythm, syntax, density) are expressive tools, not just delivery systems for meaning.

**Solo personal work as a distinct design mode.** The Milk series is autobiographical — Kryukov has spoken about the games' connection to personal experience of mental illness. This personal origin is legible in the games in a way that shapes how they work: because the subject matter is close, the design decisions about what to show and what to obscure have a weight that comes from lived knowledge rather than research. Games made from personal experience operate with a different authority than games that represent a condition from the outside. The specificity of the Milk games' distorted visuals — their precise wrongness — reflects the difference between imagining what dissociation looks like and knowing what it feels like.
