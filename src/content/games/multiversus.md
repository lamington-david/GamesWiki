## Design Notes

**2v2 as the structural core, not a mode.** The defining design decision in MultiVersus is that cooperative 2v2 play isn't a mode added to a 1v1 fighter — it's the structural premise from which everything else is derived. Every character's moveset includes abilities designed around having a partner: shields that protect allies, projectiles that teammates can interact with, movement tools that create space for a partner to exploit. This is distinct from how most platform fighters handle team modes, where 2v2 is typically 1v1 mechanics applied with an extra body on each side. Building from 2v2 upward changes what abilities are worth designing, what character archetypes make sense, and how balance functions.

**Cooperative ability design as character expression.** In most fighting games, a character's identity is defined by their solo moveset. In MultiVersus, character identity extends to how they interact with a partner. Jake the Dog can become a platform; Wonder Woman can project a shield that protects both fighters; Reindog has a tether that physically connects him to his partner for coordinated movement. These aren't gimmicks — they're the design expression of those characters' personalities translated into a cooperative context. The lesson is that licensed IP in a cooperative format gives designers a brief to think about a character's relational qualities, not just their individual power set.

**Free-to-play without barrier design.** Huynh's stated design principle for the business model was that the game should never prevent friends from playing together — characters should be accessible, unlocks should feel earned rather than gated, and the free-to-play model should lower friction rather than raise it. This is an explicit design constraint: the social premise of the game (play with your friends) must not be undermined by the monetisation model. In practice, the gap between the stated principle and the execution became a flashpoint of criticism, demonstrating how free-to-play design principles are easier to articulate than to maintain across a live-service lifespan.

**Authentic character representation as a design problem.** When the IP is pre-existing and beloved, getting the characters right is its own design discipline. The team watched original Looney Tunes cartoons to capture Tom and Jerry's animation timing. The team studied actor likenesses for human characters. The challenge is that authenticity and fighting-game viability can pull in opposite directions — a character who feels true to their source material may have a moveset that doesn't fit the game's meta. Navigating this tension, where the design answer must satisfy both the game's mechanical needs and the audience's expectations of the IP, is the distinctive challenge of licensed platform fighters.

**The shutdown as a design lesson.** MultiVersus permanently closed its servers in May 2025 after a rocky full launch failed to sustain the playerbase built during the open beta. The gap between the beta's reception (widely praised for its 2v2 design) and the full launch's performance (criticised for monetisation decisions and content rollout) offers a case study in how live-service execution can undermine genuine design merit. The game's core mechanical concepts — particularly its approach to cooperative character design — survived the discourse intact; the business execution did not.
