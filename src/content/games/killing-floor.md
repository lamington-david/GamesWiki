## Design Notes

**The wave structure as design infrastructure.** Killing Floor's wave-based design is not simply a difficulty delivery mechanism — it is the entire structural architecture of the game. Each wave has a defined enemy budget that scales with player count and current difficulty, creating a consistent difficulty ramp that lets players plan tactically rather than react randomly. The space between waves — the Trader period — is where the game's economy and strategy layer operates. This two-phase structure (combat wave then preparation phase) turns the game into something closer to a tower-defence rhythm than a continuous shooter, giving players agency over preparation and resource allocation in a way that continuous FPS games rarely achieve.

**The Trader mechanic as pacing and economy.** The Trader is one of Killing Floor's most distinctive design choices: a brief window between waves during which players must physically travel to a randomly-placed shop location and spend earned Dosh on weapons, armour, and ammo. This creates a secondary challenge layer — do you push for a better weapon at the risk of not reaching the Trader in time? The Trader location introduces a spatial negotiation into what would otherwise be pure combat. The mechanic also allows the game's economy to be player-controlled rather than algorithmic: spending too freely early means arriving to later waves underfunded, so resource management becomes a skill distinct from shooting.

**Perk design as layered specialisation.** Each character perk in Killing Floor specialises in a weapon category and grants passive bonuses that encourage players to commit to that weapon class. The perk system creates natural role differentiation within cooperative groups without mandating rigid team compositions — a Medic is more effective with healing tools but can still use standard weapons, meaning new players are not locked out of contributing. Perks level up within a session through specific actions (Berserker levels by dealing melee damage, Firebug by using incendiary weapons), creating a within-session progression arc that gives each wave a developmental quality rather than a flat difficulty climb.

**Cooperative pressure without mandatory communication.** Killing Floor supports cooperation without requiring it. Players who ignore their teammates will survive shorter runs, but the game never makes solo play mechanically impossible. The horde's movement patterns naturally drive players into shared defensive positions; the economy system naturally incentivises helping teammates survive so they keep generating Dosh; the perk system naturally rewards staying in lanes that complement one another. The cooperative pressure is structural rather than prescribed — the game doesn't tell you to cooperate, it just makes cooperation the locally optimal strategy at every decision point.

**Modding origins as design philosophy.** Killing Floor began as an Unreal Tournament 2004 mod in 2005, and this origin shaped both its technical foundations and its design sensibility. The development team came from the modding community and retained a modder's sensitivity to player feedback and community-driven iteration. Tripwire built tools to support the modding community around their retail games, and Killing Floor's long commercial lifespan (selling nearly three million copies across a decade) was supported by community maps, custom monsters, and player-created content. The lesson is that a game built by modders for modders, with modding in mind, creates a different and often more durable relationship with its player base than a game designed top-down for a mass audience.
