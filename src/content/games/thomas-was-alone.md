## Design Notes

**Narration as character creation.** Thomas Was Alone's most significant design decision was to give coloured rectangles inner lives through narration. Each shape has a distinct personality described by Danny Wallace's voiceover — Thomas is curious and lonely, Claire is confident but burdened by her abilities, John is smug, Chris is self-deprecating. These descriptions are brief and deliberately lightweight, but they're enough to make the player care about which shape gets where in each puzzle. The lesson is that character attachment is not produced by visual fidelity or narrative complexity but by the player receiving information about a character's inner state — even a rectangle's inner state — and finding it recognisable.

**Mechanics and personality as unified design.** Each character in Thomas Was Alone has different physical properties — different heights, speeds, and jump arcs — and these mechanical differences are the same things that define their personalities. Claire is capable of floating; her personality trait is that others expect too much of her. John is tall and slow; his trait is arrogance. The shapes' mechanics are their identities, which means learning how to use each character mechanically is simultaneously developing a relationship with them. This alignment of mechanical role and narrative identity is elegant game design: the player understands each character as a person at the same moment they understand them as a tool.

**Prototyping as creative commitment.** Bithell made the original Thomas Was Alone Flash game in 24 hours. The prototype was not a proof of concept — it was the concept, made in its simplest possible form. The decision to expand it into a commercial release came only after the prototype demonstrated that the core experience worked: that moving coloured rectangles around was satisfying even before narration or music. Rapid prototyping at this level does not just save time; it forces creative commitment to the actual experience rather than a description of the experience. If something works in its most stripped-down form, adding production value serves the core rather than compensating for its absence.

**Accessibility through abstraction.** The game's visual simplicity — coloured rectangles on plain backgrounds — makes it broadly accessible to players who find stylised art intimidating or who play on modest hardware. But the accessibility is also structural: because the characters are shapes rather than humanoid figures, their properties are immediately legible. Thomas is a small red square — his size and jump height are visible at a glance. This transparency of design is harder to achieve with complex character models where mechanical properties are hidden beneath visual detail. Abstraction produces readable games.

**Solo development and the power of yes.** Thomas Was Alone was developed primarily by Bithell in spare time while working a day job. This constraint made him say yes to cheap solutions that turned out to be effective ones: narration instead of cutscenes (much cheaper to produce), coloured shapes instead of character models (much cheaper to animate), simple music instead of dynamic audio systems. The game's most beloved features emerged from limitations that forced non-standard approaches. Constraints are most creatively productive when a developer responds to them with 'yes, but differently' rather than 'no, I can't afford the standard solution.'
