## Design Notes

**Interface as thematic content.** Nyalra's decision to present the game through a Windows 95-style desktop interface was not aesthetic nostalgia but thematic argument. The player sees the protagonist Ame through a manager's view — her emotional state displayed as numerical values in a task-manager-style panel, her communication reduced to instant messenger exchanges. This interface replicates the distance between how people present themselves online and how they actually feel. The design lesson is that UI is not neutral plumbing: the choice of interface encodes assumptions about what the player's relationship to the subject is, and those assumptions can themselves become meaning.

**Player investment through ownership of decisions.** Nyalra's game design theory, drawn from Monster Rancher 2, is that players develop emotional attachment proportional to the decisions they make on a character's behalf. By forcing players to make hundreds of small daily decisions about Ame's schedule, activities, and interactions over 30 in-game days, the game creates the sense that the player has built this version of Ame — that their choices have authored the relationship now endangered by declining mental health statistics. The emotional weight of the endings depends on the investment accumulated through routine management. Emotional stakes are a function of sunk decision-making, not just narrative declaration.

**Multiple endings as design argument.** NEEDY GIRL OVERDOSE has 26 endings, the majority of which are forms of Ame dying, losing sanity, or the relationship collapsing. This is not a conventional fail-state model — it is the game's thematic argument that there is no sustainable way to manage another person's mental health through optimisation alone. Players who try to maximise Ame's statistics discover that the game's systems resist this: too much streamer content depletes her mentally, too little costs followers, and most players eventually reach an ending they did not want. The failure modes are the lesson, not the consequence of failing to find the correct path.

**Denpa aesthetics as design language.** The game uses the visual and tonal register of 90s Japanese eroge — garish pastels, deliberately lo-fi pixel art, emotionally volatile character presentation — not as nostalgia but to evoke a specific genre's relationship to psychological instability and the destabilising intimacy of visual novels. Denpa culture in Japanese games is associated with characters who are psychologically abnormal in ways that undermine the player's comfortable distance from them. Nyalra used these aesthetics deliberately to signal to players who recognised them that Ame is not a stable subject to be managed from safety.

**Loneliness and internet community as primary subject.** Nyalra has stated that NEEDY GIRL OVERDOSE is fundamentally about loneliness — specifically, about how communities form online and whether they can substitute for human connection. Ame's relationship with her followers and with the player-manager is a study in the gap between the performance of connection and the experience of it. This is unusual in game design: a game that uses its core mechanics — management, optimisation, statistical monitoring — to argue that those tools are the wrong ones for the problem they are meant to solve. The player manages Ame toward disconnection while believing they are helping her.
