## Design Notes

**Design under successor constraints.** Dark Souls II is a significant case study in what happens when a franchise sequel is helmed by a different director. While Miyazaki supervised, day-to-day creative direction fell to Tomohiro Shibuya and Yui Tanimura, who had to balance an established franchise identity against their own instincts. The result clarifies which elements of the original were Miyazaki's specific authorial choices versus broader Souls formula — changes like the hub-and-spoke world structure, the more theatrical boss encounters, and a greater emphasis on lore through environmental placement are distinctly Tanimura's voice, and studying them alongside the original reveals how much authorial identity shapes the feel of a consistent-seeming formula.

**The Adaptability controversy as a lesson in invisible contracts.** One of Dark Souls II's most discussed decisions was tying roll invincibility frames to the Adaptability stat — a variable new to the series. In previous games, rolling felt universally reliable; in DS2, underpowered characters rolled and still took damage, confusing players who expected consistent behaviour. The lesson is about implicit contracts: Souls games had trained players to interpret rolling as a reliable evasion tool. Changing that behaviour through a hidden stat, without clearly surfacing the change, generated confusion that damaged the game's difficulty reputation. Transparent trade-off systems work; opaque ones produce frustration that players misattribute to the core design.

**Enemy respawn limits as a corrective experiment.** Dark Souls II introduced a cap on enemy respawning — enemies stop returning after being killed roughly twelve times, leaving areas permanently depopulated. This directly targeted soul-farming by removing the incentive to grind the same area. The experiment had unintended consequences: players who struggled and died repeatedly found areas became easier not through skill improvement but through attrition, removing challenge without the player earning the removal. The design reveals the difficulty of using economy as a behavioural lever — removing farming rewards pushed players toward exploration but inadvertently created a second degenerate strategy in its place.

**Power Stance and the value of additive mechanical depth.** The Power Stance system — unique to Dark Souls II — allowed players wielding two weapons of sufficient size to enter an aggressive dual-wield stance with entirely new move sets. This was optional, discoverable depth layered on top of the existing weapon system, enabling playstyle archetypes that could not exist otherwise. Its legacy was confirmed when the mechanic was reintroduced in Elden Ring. It demonstrates how optional expert-level systems — depth that never impedes casual progression — enrich long-term appeal without alienating newer players. The best mechanical additions in sequels are those that expand the design space rather than replace parts of it.

**Scholar of the First Sin as revisionist design.** The Scholar edition is unusual: a studio systematically revisiting every area of a shipped game to revise enemy placement, introduce new NPC invaders, and recontextualise the narrative. Producer Yoshimura confirmed that enemy placement was comprehensively reworked across all areas, not merely adjusted in a few. For returning players, this creates a game that is simultaneously familiar and new, requiring spatial relearning of areas they already know. It demonstrates that iterative design does not end at launch, and that a second-edition release can function as genuine creative revision rather than technical polish — a model for how studios can revisit released work meaningfully.
