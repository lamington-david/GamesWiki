## Design Notes

**Autobiographical source material as design foundation.** Jenny Jiao Hsia and AP Thomson built Consume Me directly from Hsia's personal experience with disordered eating, using that material not as metaphorical content but as structural logic: the game's minigames are designed to replicate specific emotional experiences of dieting — tracking, restricting, bingeing, compensating — in interactive form. This grounds the design in a kind of formal integrity that games about personal themes sometimes lack: the mechanics do not just illustrate the subject, they enact it. The design lesson is that autobiographical material, rigorously translated into systemic terms, can produce game experiences that cannot be achieved through research or imagination alone.

**Dark humour as accessibility tool.** A game about disordered eating could easily become oppressively heavy, alienating players who need emotional distance to process the subject. Hsia and Thomson's decision to treat the subject with dark humour was both an artistic choice and an accessibility decision: comedy creates space between the player and the material without requiring the player to be detached from it. The humour in Consume Me is not at the subject's expense — it is a way of making proximity to difficult content survivable. This is a transferable principle for designers working with genuinely hard subject matter: tone is not just aesthetic preference but a design tool that regulates the player's ability to stay present.

**Minigames as emotional metaphor.** The game's minigames are designed to feel like what the activities feel like: food-tracking sequences carry compulsive, number-fixated energy; the pacing of restriction sequences differs from the pacing of bingeing ones. This is minigame design used as emotional representation, not just as variety or respite from a main loop. The formal lesson is that minigame design choices — pacing, control scheme, visual presentation, feedback style — can convey emotional qualities that dialogue and image alone cannot, because game mechanics create a felt experience of doing rather than observing.

**Trigger-conscious design as responsibility.** Hsia and Thomson were conscious that a game about disordered eating could harm players with those experiences and built safeguards into the design — content warnings, design choices that avoid providing actionable "tips" usable by players in recovery, and an approach Hsia described as intending to be cathartic rather than instructive. This represents a design responsibility that games about mental health subjects rarely take seriously: when a game's subject matter overlaps with experiences that can be clinically dangerous, the designer must think about harm as carefully as they think about meaning.

**Personal specificity and universal resonance.** Consume Me won the 2025 IGF Grand Prize, Nuovo Award, and Wings Award — three of the most prestigious recognitions available to independent game designers. Its success demonstrates that games built from intensely personal experience do not sacrifice universality: the more precisely Hsia rendered her specific experience, the more broadly recognisable the emotional territory became. This challenges the assumption that personal material is limiting material, and suggests that the specificity of an experience is often what makes it emotionally legible to others who have not shared it. Consume Me is the most recent major example of a consistent pattern in independent games: the most particular games are often the most universal.
