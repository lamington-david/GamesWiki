## Design Notes

**Designing for the story after the plan fails.** Monaco's core design insight is that heist stories — in cinema and in games — are most interesting not when the plan works but when it doesn't. The game's systems are calibrated to produce failure at a rate that keeps tension constant: guards move unpredictably, alarms alert the whole building, and co-op players inevitably make mistakes. Rather than fighting this tendency, Schatz designed Monaco to make those failures funny, memorable, and survivable. The chaos isn't a flaw in the stealth system; it's the emergent narrative engine the game was built to run.

**Character asymmetry as co-op vocabulary.** Monaco's eight playable characters have radically different abilities — the Lookout sees through walls, the Locksmith opens doors instantly, the Hacker disables electronics, the Mole tunnels through walls. These aren't cosmetic variations; they're design lenses through which players experience the same level differently. In co-op, character selection becomes a planning conversation: which abilities are needed for this level's specific layout? The asymmetry gives every co-op session a distinct tactical vocabulary determined by who brought whom.

**Feedback discipline over feedback compliance.** At GDC 2014, Schatz explained that the three most consistent pieces of negative feedback Monaco received during development were about its random guard AI, its punishing line-of-sight system, and its unpredictable alarms — exactly the systems he chose not to change. His reasoning was that these elements served the game's core design goal of producing unpredictable, improvised heists. He kept them because feedback tells you what players felt, not what the game should be. This is one of the clearest articulations in indie development of how to use feedback diagnostically without treating it as a design brief.

**The IGF win as a development burden.** Monaco won the IGF Grand Prize and Excellence in Design award as a prototype in 2010, three years before it shipped. Schatz has discussed how this early validation created pressure — commercial expectations, press attention, a player base waiting — that complicated the long iteration required to turn a winning concept into a finished game. Monaco's development history is a useful case study in the double-edged nature of early recognition: it proved the concept was sound but made the iterative work harder to sustain emotionally.

**Line of sight as commitment mechanic.** Monaco's cone-of-vision guard system makes every player action a calculation of cover: move now and risk detection, or wait and let the cone pass. Unlike games that use fog-of-war or opacity to hide guards, Monaco shows you exactly what each guard can see in real time. This information completeness means every detection is the player's fault — they had the data and misjudged or mistimed. The system creates a distinct co-op communication loop: one player watching sightlines calls the window for another player to move through a gap.
