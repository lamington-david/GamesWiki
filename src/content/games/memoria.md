## Design Notes

**Dual narrative as a structural problem with structural solutions.** Memoria's central design challenge is running two completely separate stories — Geron's in the present, Sadja's five hundred years prior — with different visual tones, different gameplay registers, and different emotional registers, while making the dual structure feel like a deliberate choice rather than two half-games bundled together. The connection between the timelines is revealed gradually as each character's story sheds light on the other's. The lesson is that dual narrative in adventure games only works when the timelines are genuinely in dialogue, not merely parallel — the player must feel that understanding Sadja's story changes how they read Geron's, and vice versa.

**Narrative-first puzzle design as a studio doctrine.** Daedalic's stated design principle, articulated consistently across interviews, is that puzzles must serve the story. In practice this means that the design process for Memoria started with story moments and then asked what puzzle mechanic could deliver the player to that moment, rather than designing interesting puzzle mechanics and then constructing story around them. This is the inverse of how many adventure games are designed, and it has a practical consequence: puzzles in Memoria are rarely clever for their own sake, but they consistently feel motivated by the situation. The risk of this approach — that puzzles can feel underpowered compared to story-first designs — is visible in some of Memoria's more mechanical stretches.

**Chapter-specific puzzle vocabularies.** Kevin Mentz describes designing Memoria's puzzles in distinct categories mapped to different chapters — Myst-style environmental puzzle-solving in some sections, Loom-style magic-based interactions in others, inventory combination logic in others. Each chapter teaches and then operates within a specific puzzle vocabulary rather than mixing all mechanics simultaneously. This is a structural decision that reduces cognitive load (players learn the rules of each section) at the cost of variety within sections. It reflects a philosophy about adventure game difficulty: managing the player's mental model of what kind of thinking is required is as important as the difficulty of the individual puzzles.

**Oil-painted backgrounds and 2D character expression.** Daedalic's art direction consistently champions hand-drawn 2D backgrounds paired with 3D character models — a hybrid approach that preserves the expressive, atmospheric quality of painted environments while enabling fluid character animation. The studio's creative director argues that emotional storytelling in adventure games requires visual expressiveness that detailed 3D environments often sacrifice to realism. Memoria's dual timelines reinforce this through distinct visual tones: Geron's world is muted and intimate; Sadja's is dramatically lit and grand. The visual design does narrative work before dialogue begins.

**The Dark Eye licence as a world-building asset.** Memoria is set in Aventuria, the world of the German tabletop RPG Das Schwarze Auge (The Dark Eye), which has been in continuous publication since 1984. Adapting an established world with extensive lore for a game aimed at players who may have no prior knowledge of the setting requires careful calibration — enough context that newcomers are not lost, enough depth that existing fans feel the world is respected. Daedalic's approach is to treat the setting as texture rather than curriculum: the world's history enriches the story without demanding knowledge to follow it. This is a transferable model for any licensed adaptation of a deep-lore IP.
