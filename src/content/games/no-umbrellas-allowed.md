## Design Notes

**Value as an empathy system.** The core mechanic of No Umbrellas Allowed is pricing second-hand items — but unlike most shop sims where prices are set objectively by market value, here value is determined by listening to customers' stories about why they're parting with their belongings. The emotional significance of an object to its previous owner is material to its price. This design externalises value determination as a dialogue between player and NPC, making pricing an act of interpretation and empathy rather than calculation. The result is a shop sim where paying attention to people is the skill being tested.

**The pawnshop as political window.** Every item that passes through Darcy's shop carries a story that reveals the structure of Ajik City's surveillance state. Through the objects people bring in — and what they're willing to surrender — players assemble a picture of how Fixerain, the government-mandated mood suppressant, shapes daily life, who holds power, and what people are forced to commodify. Material objects become compressed social evidence. The game uses retail as a site of political disclosure: you learn more about the city through what its inhabitants sell than through any official exposition.

**Emotion as contraband.** The world of Ajik City criminalises emotional expression through Fixerain — a suppressant drug administered via rainfall. This is why umbrellas are prohibited: they represent resistance to state-mandated emotional neutrality. This central metaphor, in which prohibition of feeling is made physically literal through weather, is a strong model of science-fiction world-building where the fantastical premise illuminates a concrete social concern. The game's speculative conceit makes visible the real mechanics of systems that operate on interiority rather than just behaviour.

**Korean cyberpunk aesthetics as semantic design.** Hoochoo Game Studios' debut brings a distinct visual sensibility drawn from Korean cyberpunk tradition and webtoon art culture — neon commercial signage, concrete urban density, grey anonymity interrupted by saturated colour. The visual language is not decorative but meaningful: the contrast between the vibrancy of suppressed feeling and the uniform grey of conformity is the game's primary visual argument. NPCs are rendered as colour-blocked silhouettes, making the few individualised characters stand out immediately. Aesthetics and politics are unified rather than separated.

**Multiple endings as structural refusal.** With 16 different endings, No Umbrellas Allowed resists the dominant tendency to designate a single correct narrative path. The endings don't merely vary in outcome — they differ in what they say about the moral position the player occupied throughout: whether you helped customers resist the system, exploited their vulnerabilities, or simply did your job efficiently. This plurality treats the full possibility space as the work's meaning rather than routing players toward a sanctioned reading. It's a structural commitment to moral ambiguity that is rarer in narrative games than it should be.
