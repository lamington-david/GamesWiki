## Design Notes

**Starting from a sound.** Tormentor X Punisher was built backward from an audio idea: Joonas Turner, a professional sound designer, had a specific machine gun sound in his head and built a game to justify hearing it. This is an unusual design origin but a revealing one. Starting from a sensory detail rather than a mechanic or story forces every subsequent decision to serve that initial feeling. The game's one-hit kill systems, its relentless pace, its minimalist visuals — all of them exist to get out of the way of the sound and to make the sound matter as much as possible in every moment.

**The polyrhythm mechanic.** Turner designed the machine gun fire to fall in rhythmic sync with the music's underlying beat. As a result, when players shoot, they are producing polyrhythms — secondary rhythmic layers played against the existing soundtrack — without knowing they are doing so. Players feel like they are performing alongside the music rather than playing over it. This is the game's central ludomusical innovation: player action becomes musical participation, and the violence is inseparable from the groove. The lesson is that synchronizing player input timing to musical structure can produce a feeling of flow that is distinct from the conventional game-feel tool of animation feedback.

**Everything dies in one hit.** The decision to make all enemies and the player die in a single hit strips away the conventional health-management layer of the top-down shooter genre. There are no damage sponges, no resource decisions, no health pickups to collect. This extreme simplification forces the game to find its depth elsewhere — in movement, positioning, and the specific mechanic of the boss-as-lives-counter. It also produces extreme intensity: because any enemy can kill the player instantly, there is no comfortable margin, and attention cannot wander. Minimalism here is not a resource constraint but a deliberate tonal choice.

**The boss-as-lives-counter.** In Tormentor X Punisher, bosses are permanently present in each arena and function as the player's life counter: every time the player dies, a boss is damaged; every time the player kills a regular enemy, a boss is also damaged. If a boss dies from enemy hits before the player kills it, the run ends. This creates a ticking-clock relationship with the bosses that is invisible in most moments but becomes catastrophically present when the player realizes a boss is nearly dead and not yet killed. It is an elegant mechanical twist that makes the cost of dying visible in an unusual place — on the enemy rather than on the player.

**Sound design as game design.** Turner's background in game audio (he contributed sounds to Nuclear Throne and Downwell) produced a game where the audio-visual relationship is tighter than in most action games. Screen shake, music tempo, and gunshot rhythms are calibrated together rather than separately. This produces a unified sensory experience where sound and image reinforce each other at every moment rather than running in parallel. The transferable principle is that audio designers and game designers should be in conversation from the earliest prototype: the more deeply the audio is embedded in the mechanical design, the more difficult it is to add later, and the more the final result suffers if it's treated as a finishing layer.
