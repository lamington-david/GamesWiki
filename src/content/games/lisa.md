## Design Notes

**Choices with gameplay consequences.** Jorgensen's central design principle in LISA was that choices must affect gameplay, not just narrative. In most RPGs with choice systems, choosing option A or option B changes the next scene of dialogue; the player's capabilities remain intact. In LISA, making a choice to save a party member might cost Brad a limb — permanently reducing his attack options for the rest of the game. This means the weight of a decision is felt mechanically, not just emotionally. The interactive medium's specific power is consequence through system; LISA insists on it in a way few games do.

**RPG Maker as constraint and opportunity.** LISA was built in RPG Maker VX Ace, an engine with obvious limitations: fixed grid movement, a constrained visual vocabulary, limited audio mixing. Jorgensen's response was to push against these limits rather than accept them. The turn-based combat was extended to include permanent party member loss. The visual style was made to evoke Earthbound while adding grotesque art that the engine could support. Critics have noted that the game pushes the engine to its limits; from a design perspective, what's notable is that engine constraints focused Jorgensen's decisions — he couldn't build anything technically complex, so everything simple had to carry weight.

**Tonal dissonance as design argument.** LISA is funny. Party member names are absurdist, many enemy designs are comic, and the world's backstory is described with deadpan understatement. This comedy operates alongside genuine horror — party members die permanently, violence is specific and cruel, and the protagonist's emotional damage is presented without irony. This dissonance is not accidental. Jorgensen explicitly cited Earthbound as the model for using comedy to make darkness land harder — the tonal safety of the joke allows the player to lower their defenses before the game makes its actual point. The RPG format's conventional cheerfulness becomes part of what makes the game disturbing.

**Post-apocalypse as setting for masculine psychology.** The world of LISA has a specific premise: women are gone, men have formed gangs with their own codes and hierarchies, and Brad is trying to protect the last girl in the world. This is not random worldbuilding; it is a situation designed to surface a specific set of questions about how men define themselves, relate to each other, and exercise or abdicate protection. The post-apocalyptic RPG genre typically uses destruction as a backdrop for heroism; LISA uses it to examine what kind of people survive and what the survival costs them. The game's themes arrive through the world design, not through cutscenes.

**Permanence as emotional investment.** In most RPGs, party members who die in battle can be revived. LISA's permanent party member loss creates a different relationship between player and roster. Characters who travel with Brad become investments — not just in their stats but in their established personalities and roles. When they die, their absence reshapes both the emotional texture of the game and the player's tactical options. This is a design commitment: to make loss mean something, you have to make loss irreversible. Many games hesitate at this point. LISA does not.
