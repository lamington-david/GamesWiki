## Design Notes

**Art direction as tone: low-poly with high-detail textures.** Sorry We're Closed uses a specific visual technique — low-polygon character models with unusually detailed surface textures — to produce an uncanny aesthetic that sits between PS1 nostalgia and contemporary production values. This is not simply a stylistic choice but a tonal one: the combination produces a sense of wrongness, of something almost-real that cannot quite resolve into familiarity. The artist CB has cited non-gaming creative influences — musicians like VHS Head and FKA Twigs, fashion aesthetics from bubblegum pop and luxury streetwear — as more formative than game precedents. This outside-the-games-canon reference pool produces character designs that feel genuinely novel rather than derived from genre convention, which is part of why the game's visual identity is immediately recognisable.

**Zone separation as a design of two emotional registers.** À la mode games deliberately structured Sorry We're Closed around alternating safe and dangerous zones: Michelle spends time in town areas talking to characters without combat, then enters dungeons with combat and reduced social interaction. This separation creates a double emotional register — the town sequences establish character relationships and narrative stakes, while the dungeon sequences impose isolation and threat. The horror of the dungeon is amplified by the contrast with the warmth of the town; the player cares more about surviving because they have been made to care about what they return to. The designers describe this as managing a "feeling of isolation" in the dungeon sections — achieved not by making the dungeons more threatening in isolation, but by making the social spaces more alive.

**Fixed-camera combat as an honest design commitment.** Sorry We're Closed uses fixed-camera third-person combat in which Michelle cannot move and shoot simultaneously — a design inherited from classic Resident Evil. This choice was deliberate and somewhat counter-cultural: fixed-camera combat has been out of fashion since the mid-2000s. À la mode games chose it because it produces a specific kind of tension — the player must fully commit to attack or evasion, not combine them — and because the fixed-camera perspective allows the designers to control exactly what the player sees and how threats enter the frame. The Third Eye ability, which lets Michelle stun enemies and see their hearts, is designed to work within these constraints rather than work around them, adding a layer of player agency that does not break the fundamental tension of the combat design.

**Designing for emotional horror, not just mechanical threat.** Sorry We're Closed's horror is fundamentally about relationships — specifically about the emotional cost of queer relationships with angels and demons in a world that punishes vulnerability. The game's narrative involves Michelle's curse and its origins in a bad breakup; the progression involves confronting figures from that relationship; the endings depend on the player's choices about forgiveness, agency, and self-knowledge. This is a different design premise from most survival horror, which locates horror in physical threat. À la mode games places it in emotional exposure — in being known, in past damage, in the cost of attachment. The combat system exists in service of this emotional logic rather than as the game's primary statement.

**Debut studio scope management and genre ambition.** Sorry We're Closed is à la mode games' first commercial release, and its scope — a full narrative survival horror RPG with a robust combat system, multiple endings, and fully realised character designs — represents significant ambition for a first game. The studio managed this ambition by constraining the runtime (approximately eight hours) and by building on Unity with Akupara Games as publisher, gaining production support without surrendering creative direction. The game's Best Debut recognition at the 2025 Indie Game Awards reflects a specific achievement: delivering a complete, polished, thematically coherent first game in a genre where scope management is historically a serious challenge, while maintaining a visual and narrative identity distinctive enough to sustain critical and commercial attention.
