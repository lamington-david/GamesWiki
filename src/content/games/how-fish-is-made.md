## Design Notes

**The illusion of choice as the entire game.** How Fish Is Made is built around a single design premise: a binary choice — UP or DOWN — that produces the same outcome regardless of what the player picks. This is not a hidden fact revealed at the end; it is the game's consistent logic from the first fish to the last. What changes is not the result but the player's relationship to choosing. By the time the game ends, it has articulated through play rather than dialogue that the act of choosing may matter less than the frameworks people construct around their choices. This is a thesis built into the mechanics, not explained in text.

**Fish as unreliable narrators.** The sardines the player encounters each have a fully formed, internally consistent reason for their upward or downward choice. Some are logical; some are mystical; some are frightened; some are aggressive; all are certain. The game uses this cast of minor characters to model how decision-making works in practice — not through rational comparison of outcomes, but through social influence, fear, and post-hoc rationalization. Designing a character roster that illustrates a philosophical point without any of them being overtly didactic is precise craft, achieved here in a student project.

**Industrial horror without monsters.** The game's visual register — metallic chutes, grinding machinery, low-poly sardines — produces unease through setting rather than threat. There is nothing to run from. The horror is environmental and conceptual: the factory exists to process the player, and the game makes no attempt to hide this. This is a use of environmental horror design that strips out conventional horror mechanics (darkness, enemies, jump scares) and replaces them with the cognitive discomfort of an industrial system that is entirely indifferent to the choices being made within it.

**Student-project brevity as artistic discipline.** At fifteen to twenty minutes, How Fish Is Made is exactly as long as its idea requires. There is no padding, no tutorial, no escalating difficulty — the game makes its point and ends. This brevity was partly a student project constraint but is also what makes the game work: extending it would dilute it. For designers exploring how small scope forces economy of expression, this is a case study in ideas that would not survive at twice the length, and which are perfectly served by the constraint.

**Free-to-play as ethical design.** The game is free on Steam and itch.io, which is consistent with what it is arguing. A game about the meaninglessness of individual choice in systemic contexts, distributed freely without any monetization, creates a coherent relationship between its form and its content. The team did not monetize the experience because monetization would have introduced stakes — price signals value, and a game about meaninglessness probably should not cost money to access. Whether intentional or not, this is a design decision that strengthens the work.
