## Design Notes

**Time as the primary resource.** Cart Life's central design pressure is time — not money, not inventory, not customer relationships, but time. Every action takes real game-minutes: commuting to the stand, preparing coffee, visiting a government office, collecting a child from school. These time costs are calibrated to mirror actual precarity: when you are barely surviving, time spent on one thing is necessarily time not spent on another. The simulation's design decisions — how long each action takes, when children need to be collected, when business licences expire — are not arbitrary but precisely calibrated to produce the feeling of never having enough time for everything that needs doing. Time pressure is the design language for economic constraint.

**Pixel art as emotional distance.** Hofmeier designed Cart Life on a monochrome pixel grid not for nostalgia but because he wanted players to project emotional nuance into minimal visual information. The characters' faces and expressions are suggested rather than rendered; the spaces between pixels are filled by the player's imagination. This technique uses restraint to generate investment — what the player imagines may be more affecting than what could be depicted. The choice also made the game viable as a solo project, but its emotional effect is a consequence of the aesthetic decision, not a workaround for it. The form serves the content: these characters occupy visual space proportional to their social visibility.

**Research as design process.** Hofmeier interviewed real street vendors during development, with research informing the economic and social structures of the simulation. The depiction of how vendor licences work, what margins look like on coffee, and how vendors relate to one another draws on actual observation. This research-as-design methodology is common in documentary filmmaking but rare in game development. It produces a quality of specificity that distinguishes Cart Life from abstract economic simulations — players sense that the pressures are not invented but observed, which changes the moral weight of the choices being modelled. The game's argument is persuasive because it is grounded.

**Failure as narrative.** Cart Life can be failed permanently. Characters can lose their businesses, lose custody of children, or simply run out of time and money. Unlike most games, which treat failure as a blocking condition that returns the player to a prior state, Cart Life allows permanent narrative failure and continues past it. This design decision — treating failure as a story development rather than a mechanical reset — follows literary rather than game conventions. It is also a design statement about the lives being modelled: for real street vendors, failure is not a reset condition. The willingness to let players fail and live with the consequences is the most radical design choice in the game.

**Genre as advocacy.** Cart Life's characters are a single mother fighting for custody, an Egyptian émigré building a business in an unfamiliar city, and an older widower keeping a cat and a newsstand alive. These are people for whom the normal operations of society are difficult. Building a simulation around their circumstances is a political design choice: the mechanics — time pressure, financial precarity, bureaucratic friction — are arguments about what those lives are actually like. Cart Life treats economic simulation not as a management exercise but as a form of advocacy, making the design of the mechanics themselves the political statement. This is the strongest version of the claim that games can make arguments.
