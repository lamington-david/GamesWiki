## Design Notes

**Tool-based puzzle design as a genre unto itself.** Toki Tori gives players a limited toolkit — Bridge Builder, Freeze-o-Matic, Slug Sucker, Telewarp — in limited supply per level. The entire design of each puzzle hinges on identifying which tool to apply where, and in what order. This creates a distinctive design space: unlike action games where player skill determines outcome, Toki Tori puzzles have specific correct solutions that require planning rather than execution. The limited tool supply makes each placement feel weighty; using the last Bridge Builder incorrectly means restarting the level. This is a design philosophy where "thinking about the game" is the primary activity, not playing it.

**Progressive introduction of mechanics.** New tools are introduced one at a time, allowing players to understand each in isolation before combinations are required. Early levels function almost as single-tool tutorials, giving players space to discover what each item does and doesn't do before the game starts combining them. This structure — drip-feeding mechanics across a long level progression — ensures players never face a puzzle requiring knowledge they don't have. Two Tribes understood that teaching a tool's properties must precede using those properties as puzzle elements, and designed their level order around this principle.

**The egg collection constraint.** The requirement to collect every egg in a level introduces a routing constraint that fundamentally changes what puzzles look like. The question isn't "how do I reach the exit" but "how do I visit every egg optimally using the tools I have." A single secondary rule transforms spatial navigation into a route-planning problem. This is a lesson in how constraints multiply puzzle depth: the egg collection rule doesn't add mechanics but dramatically increases the number of things a player must consider in any given chamber. Secondary objectives that interact with core mechanics are often more generative than additional mechanics would be.

**Remake as design documentation.** Rebuilding Toki Tori for WiiWare and PC in 2008 required Two Tribes to re-examine every original design decision. The level layouts were redesigned, controls modernised, and some puzzles reconsidered — but the core tool-based philosophy survived intact. This process of selective preservation reveals which design elements are truly fundamental to a game's identity. The mechanics that couldn't be changed without making a different game are the game's essential design. Developers working with legacy properties — or deciding which elements of a prototype to scale — can use this lens to identify what they're actually building.

**The sequel as design critique.** Toki Tori 2 made the most radical possible choice: removing all of Toki Tori's tools and replacing them with two simple abilities (whistle and stomp). Van Ginkel described this as returning to the core of what makes puzzle games work — observation and understanding of a system rather than resource management. The sequel's existence as an explicit rejection of the original's systems illuminates something about both games: it reveals that Two Tribes themselves were uncertain whether the tools were the game's strength or its limitation. Most developers never make this critique explicit. Reading Toki Tori 2's design against the original's is a model for how sequels can productively interrogate their predecessors.
