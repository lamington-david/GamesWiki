## Design Notes

**Blood as health: aggression as the survival strategy.** ULTRAKILL's most distinctive mechanic is its health system — enemies drop blood when damaged, and blood regenerates the player's health when collected. Collection is not passive: you must be close to enemies for blood to reach you. This single rule restructures the entire game's incentives. Defensive play, the default strategy in almost every shooter, is self-defeating in ULTRAKILL. The only way to stay healthy is to stay aggressive, stay close, and keep killing. The mechanic is not just clever — it produces play that looks and feels fundamentally different from the genre's defaults.

**Style meter as expressive feedback.** Borrowed and adapted from Devil May Cry, ULTRAKILL's style meter rewards combat diversity. Repeating the same weapon or tactic causes the style grade to decay; switching weapons, combining movement with attacks, and using different tools against the same enemy drives the grade higher. The meter does not gatekeep progression — you can finish the game at D rank — but it creates a second layer of engagement for players who choose to engage with it. Crucially, the style meter functions as real-time feedback about play quality: when the grade is climbing, you are doing something interesting.

**Movement as a language, not a feature.** Hakita describes ULTRAKILL's movement system as designed to be a vocabulary rather than a checklist of abilities. Dashing, sliding, and air-strafing are not power-ups acquired through progression — they are available from the start and designed to combine. This means ULTRAKILL does not have an early game and a late game in the conventional sense; it has a period of learning the language and a period of speaking it fluently. Players who invest in learning movement find that the game becomes more expressive over time, not because new tools were added but because existing tools were internalised.

**Feedback at every layer.** Hakita credits God Hand as an influence specifically for its sound design, and ULTRAKILL carries that influence into its feedback philosophy. Every action in the game produces clear, distinct, satisfying feedback: hits register with sharp audio cues, style grade changes are announced with screen text, movement has weight and physical presence. This density of feedback is what makes ULTRAKILL feel kinetic rather than chaotic — the player always knows what they did, what it achieved, and how well they did it. Feedback clarity is what allows the game's high difficulty to feel fair rather than arbitrary.

**Solo development through Early Access as a design loop.** ULTRAKILL launched in Early Access in 2020 with several levels and has grown layer by layer over years of development. Hakita has described the Early Access process as a sustained dialogue with a community that grew to understand the game as deeply as he did. This created unusual pressure — the community's expectations were specific and sophisticated — but also unusual value: highly engaged players surface edge cases, difficulty spikes, and feel problems that internal testing would have missed. The game that shipped in full release is substantially shaped by this sustained feedback loop.
