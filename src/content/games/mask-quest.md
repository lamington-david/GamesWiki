## Design Notes

**Breath as a resource, not a gimmick.** Mask Quest's central innovation is treating breath-holding as a first-class resource with direct mechanical stakes. The player must navigate hazardous environments while managing how long they can hold their breath — and the game designs its levels around moments where the urge to rush, to take a shortcut, or to fight through a dangerous section is in direct tension with the need to conserve breath. This creates a layer of biological urgency that sits underneath the platforming challenge: not "can I make this jump" but "can I make this jump now, with this much breath remaining." The mechanic works because it taps into a real physiological discomfort, making the in-game resource feel physically legible in a way that abstract stamina bars rarely achieve.

**increpare's single-constraint design philosophy.** Stephen Lavelle has released over five hundred games and is recognised as one of the most prolific independent game developers in history. Across this output, a consistent design method emerges: take one constraint or mechanic that produces interesting decisions, build levels that explore every dimension of that constraint, and stop before adding a second mechanic that would dilute the clarity of the first. Mask Quest applies this to breath: the game does not also add stamina, or health, or a limited jump count. The breath is the resource, and the level design is an exhaustive investigation of what that one resource implies when placed in different spatial and threat configurations.

**Old-school platforming as a container for new ideas.** Mask Quest uses the visual and structural language of classic 2D platformers — grid-based levels, pixel art, identifiable enemy types, clear hazards — as a foundation that players can read without instruction. This is a deliberate design move: by grounding the game in familiar genre conventions, Lavelle creates a context in which the breath mechanic stands out with maximum clarity. Players know how platformers work; the breath rule is the only thing that is different; the experience of learning the game is therefore an experience of understanding one specific thing. The classic aesthetic also allows the game to be short — players do not need to be taught the platformer basics — while still delivering fifty-plus levels of escalating challenge.

**Collaboration and scope in the increpare practice.** Mask Quest was made in collaboration with undef, a recurring practice in Lavelle's work — bringing in a co-designer or collaborator to develop a specific project rather than working solo on everything. This collaborative mode allows increpare to work at higher production quality on selected titles while maintaining the prolific output that defines his practice. The game's scope — a complete campaign at roughly ten hours of play, with a full original soundtrack — is more developed than many increpare releases, reflecting the focused investment that a collaboration enables. The game demonstrates that constraint-based design and polished production are not in tension.

**Difficulty as a form of respect.** Lavelle's games are consistently more difficult than the mainstream expects, and this is understood as a design position rather than a quality control failure. The difficulty of Mask Quest is calibrated so that every challenge has a legible solution — the breath mechanic always explains why a given section is hard, and the solution always involves understanding the breath's duration and the level's timing rather than pure reflex optimisation. This is the design principle that critics identify as "fair" difficulty: the player can always understand why they failed, which means failure is always a lesson rather than an arbitrary punishment. The game's Steam rating reflects the tension between this demanding design position and player expectations calibrated by more forgiving platformers.
