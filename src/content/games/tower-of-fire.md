## Design Notes

**Solo authorship as creative coherence.** Tower of Fire was designed, programmed, and scored by a single developer. This kind of solo authorship produces a game where every element — the look, the sound, the puzzle logic, the tone, the combat — originates from the same creative perspective. There are no committee compromises or feature trades. The result is a game with unusual coherence of vision: the comedy, the chicken fighting, the crate-pushing puzzles, and the Heavy Metal soundtrack feel unified not despite being eclectic, but because they were all filtered through one sensibility. Solo development at this scope is less a production model than a personal statement about what the game needed to be.

**Custom engine as creative control.** Rather than building on an established engine, the developer wrote a custom one from scratch — explicitly to support the chicken-slaughtering physics the game required. This is a significant commitment that carries enormous maintenance overhead, but it reflects a priority often compromised in commercial development: making tools fit the vision rather than fitting the vision to the tools. That the custom engine is called out in the game's own description signals a design philosophy where technical decisions are inseparable from design intent. The engine exists because the game demanded physics that no available tool provided correctly.

**Retro aesthetics as creative constraint.** Tower of Fire aims to feel like "a missing gem from the early 00's" — not a game that imitates 2003 due to limited resources, but one that deliberately works inside the aesthetic rules of that era. Retrograde aesthetics function as constraint: they narrow the visual vocabulary in ways that force creative solutions within those limits. Games that deliberately commit to an era's constraints develop a distinctive personality that visually maximalist games cannot achieve. The game doesn't look like it's making the best of what it has; it looks like it chose its limits on purpose.

**Comedy through mechanical commitment.** The game describes its chicken combat as more "realistic, fluid and satisfying than ever" — a joke that only works if the system actually delivers. This is comedy through mechanical seriousness: the humour comes not from the absurdity of the premise alone but from the absurdity of taking that premise seriously and implementing it with craft. Games that label themselves funny without investing in mechanical execution tend not to be. Crate-pushing and boulder-rolling puzzles borrowed from earnest puzzle games are deployed here in a comedy context, and they work because the systems are genuinely functional rather than comedically broken.

**Genre fusion as design identity.** The "Frankenstein of Monkey Island, Chip's Challenge, Zelda and Power Metal" description is marketing, but it is also accurate design archaeology — each influence contributes a distinct functional layer. The adventure game comedic structure, block-pushing puzzle mechanics, top-down action-adventure exploration, and Heavy Metal musical aesthetic are each identifiable and load-bearing. Deliberate genre fusion where each ingredient remains recognisable produces games with personality that homogenised design cannot achieve. The fusion works when each source is chosen for what it contributes functionally, not assembled randomly for eclecticism's sake.
