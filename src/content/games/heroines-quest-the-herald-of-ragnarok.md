## Design Notes

**Adventure-RPG integration as a design problem.** The Quest for Glory series established the adventure-RPG hybrid as a genre, but individual games in that series often handled the integration unevenly — puzzles that could be bypassed with the right stat, or RPG elements that felt tacked onto a fundamentally adventure-game structure. Heroine's Quest was designed to solve this problem deliberately, ensuring that each character class (Warrior, Sorceress, Rogue) has distinct puzzle solutions throughout the game, making class choice feel genuinely meaningful rather than cosmetic.

**Three classes, three games.** Pieter Simoons designed Heroine's Quest so that the three classes encounter the same events through fundamentally different means. A Warrior might break through an obstacle directly; a Sorceress might use magic to bypass it; a Rogue might find a deceptive route around it. This multiplies the design surface significantly — every puzzle that allows class-specific solutions requires three implementations — but produces a replayability that makes the game feel like a different experience depending on your class, rather than the same experience with different numbers.

**Norse mythology as puzzle logic.** Setting the game in the world of the Poetic Edda was not merely an aesthetic choice. Norse mythology's specific characters, objects, and narrative rules become puzzle elements: knowing the lore informs player decisions, and game puzzles are built around the logic of the myths rather than arbitrary adventure-game conventions. This grounds the design in a consistent internal world where solutions feel earned rather than arbitrary, and rewards players who engage with the setting's lore.

**Freeware as design statement.** Crystal Shard released Heroine's Quest free on Steam, a decision that reflected both practical reasoning and philosophical stance. For a game built as a tribute to a beloved series, asking players to pay created friction that a free release removed. The freeware model also placed the game in front of audiences who might not have risked money on an indie retro-style hybrid, generating the word-of-mouth that brought it to the attention of the original Quest for Glory creators.

**Small team, deep simulation.** Crystal Shard built Heroine's Quest with a tiny distributed team — designer/programmer Pieter Simoons in the Netherlands, artists and composers in different countries. The game's depth of systemic integration (each class's skill progression, multiple puzzle solutions, voiced dialogue, a full Norse mythology narrative) represents an unusual achievement in scope relative to team size, demonstrating that disciplined design scope — committing to hybrid genre integration rather than expanding content breadth — can produce work that punches above its means.
