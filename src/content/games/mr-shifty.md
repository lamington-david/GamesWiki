## Design Notes

**Teleportation as a spatial reading mechanic.** Mr. Shifty's core ability — phasing through walls and across short distances — transforms each combat room into a spatial puzzle before it becomes an action problem. Players must read geometry for thin walls that permit teleport traversal, identify sightlines to exploit, and plan sequences of shifts that thread through gunfire. The mechanic requires players to think in three dimensions about a two-dimensional space: what looks like a blocked path in top-down view is often a wall thin enough to pass through. This rewards spatial literacy in a genre that usually rewards only reaction time.

**The five-shift limit as rhythmic design.** Shifting is capped at five consecutive uses before a cooldown triggers. This constraint is the game's central tension generator: with five shifts available, players can teleport aggressively and displace enemies before they react; exhaust the meter, and they're immobile for several seconds while gunmen close in. The rhythm of shift-burst then vulnerability mirrors the tension loop of Hotline Miami's run-and-react cycle, but with a meter players can consciously manage. The design teaches players to read when to spend shifts and when to conserve them — a resource economy expressed through movement.

**One-hit lethality at speed.** Like Hotline Miami, Mr. Shifty kills and dies in a single hit. This forces players to treat combat as a choreography problem — deaths reveal what the player failed to anticipate, and restarts are immediate, sending them back to treat the same room as a new puzzle. The feedback loop is tight: die, identify the mistake, replay the encounter with adjusted positioning or shift sequencing. At its best this produces the Hotline Miami feeling of finally threading a perfect run through a room that killed you a dozen times.

**Level design as escalating constraint.** The game introduces new room configurations and enemy types that progressively complicate the teleport mechanic — walls that can't be phased through, enemies with shotguns who punish close-range play, floors with hazards that redirect teleport targets. Each new element is essentially a new constraint on the shift mechanic, requiring players to revise their mental model of how the ability works in context. This is competent mechanic-based level design: the levels don't get harder by adding more enemies but by narrowing the space of viable strategies.

**The ex-Halfbrick heritage.** Team Shifty formed from former Halfbrick developers who built mobile action games, and their instinct for distilling mechanics to their purest expression is evident throughout Mr. Shifty. The game has essentially one verb — shift — expressed across dozens of variations. This single-mechanic focus, common in mobile design where screen space limits input complexity, gives the game its coherence. It's a useful reminder that disciplined scope is often a design virtue: a game that does one thing well is usually more satisfying than a game that does many things adequately.
