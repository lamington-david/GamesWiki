## Design Notes

**Brutalist architecture as puzzle language.** Kairo's structures are not decorative — they are the puzzles. Richard Perrin chose brutalism's vocabulary of exposed function, massive scale, and geometric severity because it communicates purpose visually without requiring text or icons. A lever integrated into a concrete pillar reads as a lever because brutalism makes function visible. A monolith with a switch at its base is inherently legible within the logic of brutalist design. This is architecture-as-tutorial: the built environment teaches the player how to read it by being rigorously consistent about what each visual language element means.

**Wordless design at scale.** Kairo has no dialogue, no tutorial text, no inventory, and no HUD. The player navigates an enormous alien world using only environmental cues — lighting, geometry, scale, and audio. This is an extreme example of what some designers call "showing not telling" taken to its logical conclusion: the game simply refuses to explain itself in language and trusts that a designed environment can carry all necessary information. The gaps this creates — places where the player must stop, look, and interpret — are where the atmosphere lives. Difficulty in Kairo is primarily perceptual rather than mechanical.

**Solo development as aesthetic constraint.** Perrin built Kairo over multiple years as a single developer with limited 3D art skills, and the result is a game whose aesthetic was shaped directly by what one person could produce. The brutalist style, which relies on simple geometric volumes rather than detailed organic modeling, was something Perrin could execute to a high standard alone. The minimalism was not a philosophy imposed on the project from outside but the natural result of a solo developer choosing a visual language within their capabilities. This is a case study in how constraints shape aesthetics in productive rather than limiting ways.

**Scale and proportion as emotional tools.** The structures of Kairo are designed to dwarf the player — doors thirty meters high, open plazas of bare concrete measured in hundreds of meters. This is a deliberate emotional choice: the architecture communicates that whatever civilization built these things was not human in scale or purpose. The player's smallness within these spaces produces a specific variety of existential unease that would be impossible if the spaces were human-sized. Proportion is doing work here that no amount of horror lighting or monster design could replicate.

**Atmospheric exploration as a complete genre proposition.** Kairo arrived around the same time as several other first-person atmospheric explorers, and its design is interesting partly for what it refuses. There is no combat, no inventory management, no quest log, and no fail state. The player simply moves through a world and solves what it presents. This stripping-back raises a question useful for designers: what is actually required for a game to constitute a satisfying experience? Kairo's answer is rigorous enough to be generative.
