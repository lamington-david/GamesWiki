## Design Notes

**Physics-based movement as the core design proposition.** Capsized gives the player a grappling hook and jetpack simultaneously, creating a movement system where vertical and horizontal traversal blend into a single fluid capability. The design intention was to bring the spatial freedom of first-person shooters — the sense of moving through three-dimensional space on your own terms — into a 2D environment. Jesse McGibney and Lee Vermeulen explicitly cited FPS games as influences on how they thought about player locomotion, which produced something unusual: a 2D platformer where jumping and running are the least interesting movement options available.

**Hand-drawn worlds as a response to movement fidelity.** McGibney hand-drew all of Capsized's alien environments at a level of detail that was unusually high for indie 2D games in 2011. This decision was not purely aesthetic — a movement system as fluid as Capsized's requires an art style that rewards close inspection, because players will be swinging through the same areas multiple times at speed. Tiled or procedurally generated environments would feel repetitive against the movement; hand-crafted ones reward revisitation. The art style and the movement system are in a design conversation with each other.

**Thesis project as commercial game.** Capsized began as a final undergraduate project at the University of Saskatchewan, which both developers completed before continuing development full-time. The direct transition from student project to commercial product without an intermediate prototype phase meant the game shipped with the design priorities of students — emphasizing technical novelty and art quality over genre conventions or market positioning. This is both the game's strength (it feels genuinely experimental) and an explanation for its mixed commercial reception: it did not design to expectations it did not know existed.

**Level design in service of movement.** Designing levels for a grappling hook and jetpack requires thinking about level structure differently than conventional platformers. Empty space is valuable because it allows the grappling hook to work; enclosed spaces create friction and intensity by limiting it. Capsized's levels use this systematically, alternating open traversal sections where the player can express the full velocity of the movement system with tighter combat spaces that constrain it. This is a specific level design skill — building for a movement system rather than against it — that the game demonstrates throughout.

**Indie origins and the legacy problem.** Capsized was released the same year as Bastion, Terraria, and Minecraft's commercial launch — a competitive context that made visibility difficult for a 2D shooter with unconventional movement design. Alientrap's subsequent work (particularly Apotheon) received more critical attention, and Capsized has been partially overshadowed by its successors. For designers, the game is a useful object lesson in how movement system design can be technically sophisticated and genuinely novel without producing commercial or critical success — and in what that gap reveals about what players were actually looking for in 2011.
