## Design Notes

**Diegetic UI as first principle.** 4A Games removed nearly all HUD elements from Metro: Last Light, integrating player-critical information directly into the game world. Your wristwatch shows elapsed time on gas mask filters; bullet count is visible on the physical ammo pouch; health is conveyed through Artyom's breathing and posture changes. Every piece of information you need exists inside the fiction rather than floating above it as interface. This design decision cost significant technical effort but achieved what HUD-based systems cannot: information-gathering becomes an act of inhabiting the character rather than reading a dashboard, sustaining immersion through moment-to-moment play rather than just in cutscenes.

**Bullets as currency and permanent tension.** The Metro universe uses pre-war military-grade ammunition as currency alongside conventional scavenged bullets. This dual-use creates a constant economic tension: high-grade ammo is both the most effective weapon and the most valuable trade good. Every firefight forces a choice between combat effectiveness and economic survival, giving routine encounters multiple simultaneous stakes. This single design decision — making your best weapon also your primary currency — transforms the entire combat economy into a resource management layer that persists through the game's systems.

**Moral design without moral legibility.** The karma system in Last Light tracks player behaviour across the run — how often you resort to violence, whether you show mercy to incapacitated enemies, what you choose in ambiguous situations — and uses this to determine the ending. But the system never labels choices, signals consequences, or tells players they're being evaluated. This covert moral design elicits authentic ethical behaviour rather than performed virtue: players who show mercy do so because it felt right, not because they saw a karma notification. The resulting variation in endings reflects who the player actually was, not who they were trying to be for the system.

**Atmosphere through inhabited space.** The Moscow Metro is populated with characters whose lives extend beyond the player's story. Overheard conversations reveal faction politics, personal histories, and the daily texture of surviving underground without ever becoming explicit exposition. Side areas reward exploration with scenes of ordinary post-apocalyptic life — a puppet show, a family's bunker, children playing — that the main quest never requires you to find. This environmental storytelling creates the sense that the world exists independently of Artyom's presence, making the setting feel geographically and socially real rather than a backdrop constructed for a shooter level.

**Ukrainian development against resource constraints.** 4A Games built Metro: Last Light in Kyiv with a budget that, according to creative director Andrey Prokhorov, represented less than what competing AAA studios spent on cutscenes alone. The game was acclaimed for its atmosphere, narrative depth, and technical craft — achieving in constrained circumstances what better-resourced studios failed to produce. The team drew on direct Eastern European experience of Soviet-era infrastructure, post-industrial aesthetics, and political fatalism to write a world they understood from the inside rather than reconstructed from research. Proximity to source material is a competitive advantage that money cannot replicate.
