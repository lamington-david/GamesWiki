## Design Notes

**Non-linear time as a horror instrument.** Mouthwashing doesn't tell its story in order. The game cuts between a catastrophic present and the events leading up to it, giving the player fragments that must be assembled into a complete picture. This technique has a specific effect: you spend the entire game dreading something you already partially know is coming. The horror comes not from surprise but from approaching inevitability with full understanding of what it means. By structuring revelation backwards, Wrong Organ extracted dread from information rather than from its absence — the opposite of most horror design.

**PSX aesthetics as emotional filter.** The game's low-poly, low-resolution visual style is not nostalgia bait. Art director Johanna Kasurinen explicitly chose it to create emotional distance — the roughness of the presentation prevents players from clearly seeing the worst things that happen, which makes those things more disturbing, not less. The limited fidelity works like a censored photograph: the mind fills in what the pixels don't show. It also evokes the specific visual register of 1990s Japanese horror games, carrying cultural associations of dread that high-fidelity rendering could not reproduce.

**Horror through implication, not mechanics.** Mouthwashing is, mechanically, a walking simulator with minimal interaction. There are no monsters to run from, no combat, no jump scares. The horror is entirely constructed through writing, sound design, and the deliberate framing of what the camera does and doesn't show. This was a principled choice, not a constraint: the team believed that agency creates hope, and they wanted to remove hope. When players can act on the world, they develop the feeling that outcomes are changeable. In Mouthwashing, they cannot, and the design uses that passivity to produce a specific variety of existential horror unavailable to games with more interactive loops.

**Sound as the primary narrative channel.** Audio director Martin Halldin designed the sound environment of the cargo ship Tulpar as a character in its own right — the hums, clanks, and silences communicate the ship's state and the emotional tenor of each scene independently of what is happening on screen. Several of the game's most affecting moments derive their power almost entirely from audio, with visuals serving a secondary function. This inverts the typical hierarchy in game design, where visual information dominates and audio confirms it. For games with limited graphical budgets, this suggests a path to emotional depth that doesn't require visual fidelity.

**Small scope as creative freedom.** Mouthwashing is completable in under two hours. This was not a limitation but a design constraint the team embraced: a short experience can be controlled with precision that longer games cannot achieve. Every scene in the game was iterated until it earned its place. The brevity also changes the player's relationship to difficulty — there is no save-before-the-hard-part, no grinding, no padding — just a single dense object the player must move through completely. The game's commercial success despite its length (500,000 copies sold) challenged assumptions about the minimum playtime required for premium pricing to succeed.
