## Design Notes

**Constraint as aesthetic identity.** panstasz made all the art in WORLD OF HORROR in MS Paint using a mouse, working within a strict 1-bit (black and white only) palette. This was not chosen for speed or ease — MS Paint is harder to work in than purpose-built pixel art tools. It was chosen because the resulting visual style, with its high-contrast stark forms, matched the aesthetic of the game's primary influence: Junji Ito's manga. The constraint produced an identity so distinctive that the game's visual language is immediately recognisable. This is the strongest version of the constraints-as-creativity argument: not that limitations force clever solutions, but that specific limitations can produce specific aesthetics that would not emerge from freedom.

**Solo development over extended time.** panstasz developed WORLD OF HORROR over approximately five years while maintaining a dental practice, releasing in Early Access in 2019 and completing it in 2023. This development model — a primary career funding an ambitious side project developed "low and slow" — produced a game of commercial-release quality that a part-time developer could not have finished on a conventional game-jam timeline. The lesson is that the time dimension of development is a variable that can be extended rather than compressed. A solo developer with unlimited time can achieve what a small team achieves in condensed time, at the cost of a longer wait and the challenge of sustaining creative direction over years.

**Roguelite structure as horror amplifier.** The game's procedural mystery system generates cases with randomised elements — location, antagonist, victims, required resolution — combined from a pool of content designed with horror logic. The randomness functions as a horror tool because it makes the world feel ungovernable and unpredictable, which is the emotional core of cosmic horror. Players cannot optimise their way to safety because the threats are not consistent enough to fully learn. This is an insight about horror game design specifically: roguelite randomness, often used for replayability in other genres, has particular horror utility because it prevents the player from ever feeling fully in control.

**Translating manga aesthetics into interactive form.** Junji Ito's horror works in print through the relationship between detailed, realistic figure-work and grotesque, impossible content — the ordinary rendered precisely, the extraordinary rendered with the same precision. WORLD OF HORROR achieves a similar effect through its 1-bit aesthetic: the pixel-art portraits of characters and monsters share the same visual treatment as menus and status bars, normalising the monstrous through presentation. The game doesn't look like it's trying to be scary — it looks like any other 1-bit interface game — and this flatness of presentation is where its horror lives. Adapting a medium means finding what produces the same emotional effect, not copying the surface.

**Horror RPG mechanics as thematic design.** The game uses traditional JRPG stat systems — health, reason (sanity), resources — that map directly onto the cosmic horror themes. Reason depletes as you investigate horrors, reflecting Lovecraftian sanity mechanics. The management of reason and health creates the sensation of a fragile human consciousness probing forces it cannot survive understanding. These mechanical systems are not decorative overlays on a horror setting but structural expressions of the horror's underlying logic: knowledge is dangerous, human capability is limited, and the best outcome is temporary survival. When a game's mechanics and its themes say the same thing, the result is a more coherent and affecting experience.
