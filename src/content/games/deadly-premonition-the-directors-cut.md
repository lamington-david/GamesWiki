## Design Notes

**Real time, real scale, real life.** SWERY built Deadly Premonition around three design pillars he called "the three reals." Every resident of Greenvale has a daily schedule — they sleep, eat, go to work, and return home on a clock that runs independently of the player's actions. The town square genuinely empties at night. The diner only serves breakfast in the morning. This simulation operates regardless of whether it's convenient for the player and regardless of whether the player ever notices it. The design argument is that a world that operates according to its own logic — not the player's agenda — is more convincing and more affecting than one that exists purely to serve the player. Players who engage with the simulation discover a Greenvale that feels genuinely inhabited.

**Character resumes as design infrastructure.** Before writing a single line of dialogue for any NPC, SWERY wrote what he called "resumes" for each character — detailed biographical documents covering childhood, first loves, recurring habits, catchphrases, and personal quirks. Most of this material never appears explicitly in the game. It exists so that SWERY could write each character's dialogue and actions from an internally consistent character rather than from narrative convenience. The result is a cast where every resident of Greenvale behaves in ways that feel psychologically coherent even when bizarre — their strangeness is characterological rather than arbitrary. This methodology (over-document characters so you can write them authentically) produces NPCs that players find memorable long after other mechanical details fade.

**Reward as character knowledge.** Deadly Premonition's reward structure is the inverse of most open-world games. Completing quests and side objectives does not primarily yield equipment, experience points, or upgrades — it yields deeper knowledge of the characters involved. The game's design bet is that players will invest in Greenvale's residents enough to find that relationship deepening genuinely satisfying. SWERY's explicit goal was for players to think about the game's characters when they weren't playing — to carry Francis York Morgan's psychology around in their heads. This is the design philosophy of serialised television translated into an interactive medium: the real reward is caring about the people.

**TV serial structure.** The game's main narrative is structured as a television mystery serial, complete with episodic chapters, cliffhanger endings, and a protagonist who talks to an imaginary friend named Zach throughout. This structure — unusual for video games in 2010 — was a deliberate creative choice. Each episode is meant to leave the player with a question demanding resolution, creating the compulsive viewing rhythm of a mystery drama. SWERY was drawing directly on American prestige television (particularly Twin Peaks, which he cites as an influence), and the design lesson is that pacing structures from other media can be meaningfully translated into games when they serve the genre's specific strengths.

**The design value of committed weirdness.** Deadly Premonition's production quality is genuinely inconsistent — its combat is mechanical, its graphics dated even for 2010, and many of its systems underdeveloped. Its cult status is not despite these limitations but alongside them; the game's passionate following is built on SWERY's extraordinary commitment to his own bizarre vision. The character writing, world simulation, and tonal contrasts (a serious murder mystery containing extended sequences of card games and fishing) are so specifically strange and so fully committed that the game creates an experience available nowhere else. The design lesson — that a distinctive, fully-committed vision at the centre of a game can make players forgive or even cherish its failures elsewhere — is one of the most counterintuitive but consistent findings in cult game history.
