## Design Notes

**A complete game as camouflage.** Glittermitten Grove's defining design decision was to make the decoy fully real. It isn't a thin wrapper or a loading screen — it's a functioning fairy-village resource-management sim with its own economy, seasonal cycles, and building constraints. That completeness is what makes the misdirection work: players have no reason to suspect a "finished, released, reviewed" game is secretly a shell, which is precisely what let Frog Fractions 2 stay hidden inside it for years. The lesson for any ARG or hidden-content design is that the cover needs to survive scrutiny as a standalone product, not just look plausible at a glance.

**Mystery as structure, not marketing.** Jim Crawford's talks make clear that hiding Frog Fractions 2 wasn't a stunt bolted onto a finished game — it was the organizing principle from the start. Modern spoiler culture treats secrets as fragile and temporary; Crawford's response was to build a secret robust enough to survive being spoiled, because the *act* of discovery (the speculation, the collective puzzle-solving, the sense of an in-crowd) generates more value than the secret's content alone. Designers chasing "no spoilers" should consider instead designing for spoiler-resilience: structure the discovery process itself to be worth experiencing even once the destination is known.

**Resource management as cover for tonal whiplash.** Underneath its sugary aesthetic, Glittermitten Grove has a real systemic backbone — trees compete for sunlight, houses can only be placed in specific spots, resources are seasonal and finite. That genuine mechanical depth, paired with a deliberately twee art style, means the game doesn't feel like a placeholder while you're inside it. When the tone eventually ruptures into the ASCII-text, surreal world of Frog Fractions 2, the contrast lands harder precisely because the first game was never coasting on being "just a shell."

**Constraint-driven development.** Twinbeard's small team effectively built two games in the budget and time usually allotted to one, and Justin Bortnick's account of "everybody has 10 jobs" reads less as a complaint than as an explanation for why the game ended up so modular: discrete vignettes and systems were proven individually before being assembled into the whole. Teams operating under similar resource constraints can take from this that splitting ambitious scope into smaller, independently functional pieces — rather than attempting one unified production — can be the only realistic path to delivering something this structurally audacious.

**Letting the audience's imagination outpace the work.** One of the most striking admissions from the Twinbeard team is the acknowledgment that the community's speculation about what might be hidden in Glittermitten Grove often grew more elaborate than anything they could realistically build. Rather than treating that as a failure, the team leaned into managing expectation as part of the design: the goal wasn't to "win" against player theorizing but to give it room to run, trusting that the mystery itself — sustained over years — was the real deliverable, with the eventual reveal serving as a satisfying punctuation rather than the entire point.
