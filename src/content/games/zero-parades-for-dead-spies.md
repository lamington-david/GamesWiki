## Design Notes

**The literary RPG voice as a transferable design system.** ZA/UM's most distinctive invention in Disco Elysium was using skill checks not as gates but as voices — the player's psychology externalised as competing internal commentators. ZERO PARADES keeps this system while transplanting it into espionage. The operative Hershel Wilk operates under an alias, which means the skill voice system now speaks to both who the player character is and who they are pretending to be. This layering of identity is a natural extension of ZA/UM's design: if Disco Elysium's skill checks revealed the detective's fractures, ZERO PARADES's skill checks negotiate between a spy's cover and their actual judgement.

**Genre as a lens for systemic design.** The espionage genre provides ZERO PARADES with a set of pre-existing reader expectations — tradecraft, covert missions, the city-state as a closed space with information asymmetry — that ZA/UM uses to structure what would otherwise be purely open narrative RPG. The city-state of Portofiro is not just a setting but a mechanical argument: a bounded political geography where every NPC is a potential asset, informant, or threat makes the game's social skill systems feel immediately purposeful. Choosing espionage as a genre is itself a design decision about what kinds of player behaviour feel meaningful.

**Maintaining voice through personnel change.** ZERO PARADES was developed after significant changes to ZA/UM's creative team, with several of Disco Elysium's original writers departing before the sequel entered production. The studio's public writing about Zero Parades consistently addresses how new writers approached the task of writing in ZA/UM's voice — described as a kind of institutional aesthetic defined by literary density, dark humour, and political philosophy — rather than replicating specific stylistic tics. This is a practical design challenge with broad relevance: how does a studio maintain its distinctive register when the people who defined it have left? Zero Parades is an ongoing case study in answering that question.

**Portofiro as a worldbuilding strategy.** Rather than continuing in Revachol, Disco Elysium's fictional city, ZERO PARADES invents Portofiro, a city-state designed from the ground up around the espionage narrative. This is a significant design choice: a new city can be built around its story requirements rather than adapted to them. Portofiro's geography, political factions, and cultural atmosphere can all serve the spy narrative directly, whereas extending Revachol would require honouring prior canon. New settings are more work but give writers full control over what the world contains and what it implies — a freedom that ZERO PARADES appears to use deliberately, with Portofiro's design reflecting the game's themes of loyalty, concealment, and political compromise.

**Isometric RPG as a camera philosophy.** ZA/UM's use of isometric presentation is not simply aesthetic — it is an argument about the player's relationship to their character. The overhead perspective creates distance and observation: you watch your character navigate the world rather than inhabiting them directly. This detachment is thematically useful for both games: in Disco Elysium, you observe a broken man; in ZERO PARADES, you observe an operative whose inner life is partially concealed even from the player. The camera is an implicit design statement about how much you should identify with versus analyse the character you are playing.
