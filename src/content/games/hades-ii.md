## Design Notes

**The sequel as design expansion, not repetition.** Supergiant's approach to Hades II explicitly avoided remaking the first game with more content. Melinoë — a witch rather than a warrior, moving through the surface world above the underworld rather than through it — requires a different movement vocabulary, different boon logic, and a different emotional register in dialogue. Where Zagreus's arc is familial and defiant, Melinoë's is scholarly and purposeful. These differences are not cosmetic: they cascade into combat feel, hub world dynamics, and the game's narrative structure. The lesson is that a sequel can serve as a genuine creative expansion by treating the new protagonist as a design brief that necessitates different systems rather than identical ones with reskins.

**The game as its own design document.** Studio director Amir Rao describes Supergiant as a "heavily iterative studio" that doesn't use long, elaborate design documents. The design documents that exist are loose, and the actual design emerges through building, playtesting, and responding to what the playable game reveals. For Hades II's Early Access period, this meant that player feedback could immediately influence whether a system survived or was replaced — not because players were given editorial control, but because observable player behaviour provided better data than pre-planned assumptions. This approach requires a studio culture that tolerates systemic uncertainty in exchange for systemic responsiveness, and the team's long history of working together made that culture possible.

**Expanded boon systems and build complexity.** Hades II substantially deepens the boon selection systems from the original game, introducing status conditions, upgrade paths between boon tiers, and interactions across Olympian god offerings that reward players who understand the full system. This expansion of complexity is managed through progressive disclosure: early runs present a more limited selection, and the full depth of the system becomes legible gradually as players develop familiarity. The challenge of adding complexity without overwhelming new players is one of the core sequel design problems, and the game addresses it by trusting players to discover depth through play rather than documenting it upfront.

**Melinoë's character as a design constraint.** Designing around a specific protagonist personality rather than a blank player-character avatar imposes design constraints that pay off in narrative coherence. Melinoë's academic, purposeful disposition means certain dialogue options and weapon types feel more natural within her voice than they would for a different protagonist. These self-imposed constraints prevent the design from sprawling into incoherence — the protagonist serves as a filter on what content belongs in the game and what doesn't. This is a design technique familiar from linear games but less common in the roguelike genre, where player-character identity is often deliberately minimal.

**Early Access as sequel design research.** Hades II's Early Access period served a different function than the original game's did. For Hades, Early Access was where the game proved that the narrative-roguelike concept worked at all. For Hades II, Early Access was where the team could test whether expanded systems — new weapon types, new companion mechanics, new mythological territory — justified their design cost against player engagement. The Early Access community for a sequel arrives with established expectations, which makes negative signal (when players bounce off a new system) more meaningful and faster to diagnose than it would be for a novel concept. The sequel's iterative process benefits from the first game having already established what the audience expects and values.
