## Design Notes

**Autobiography as design methodology.** Nina Freeman's background is in poetry, and she approached Cibele the way a poet approaches personal material — by treating her own experience as the source text and game design as the medium for rendering it. The story is taken directly from a real relationship Freeman had in 2009, conducted through the MMO Final Fantasy XI, and the game includes actual photographs, drawings, and documents from her real files. This is not just thematic inspiration; the game's assets are the autobiography. The design challenge was not inventing a story but selecting from real material and constructing a system that let players inhabit it.

**Desktop fiction as a narrative form.** Cibele places the player inside a simulated computer desktop belonging to the protagonist. Between chapters, the player can explore the desktop freely — reading emails, looking through a photo library, opening browser tabs. This desktop space is the primary space of the game, and the fictional MMO Valtameri runs inside it. The desktop fiction format creates intimacy through access: the player is inside someone's private machine, looking through their actual files, in a way that produces the feeling of closeness without a single line of direct characterisation. The form fits the subject — a relationship that existed almost entirely in digital space is told through a digital environment.

**The game within a game.** Cibele's structure alternates between the desktop exploration layer and sessions inside Valtameri, where the protagonist and her love interest Blake form their relationship. The Valtameri sequences are stylised and playful — an actual working (minimal) MMO — while the desktop sequences are naturalistic and emotionally denser. The two registers play against each other: the MMO is where the romance feels light and possible, the desktop is where its complications accumulate. This layered structure lets Freeman show both what online relationships feel like from inside them and what they look like from outside — simultaneously.

**Designed for a single playthrough.** Cibele is a short game — under an hour — with no branching choices, no replay value, and no mechanical challenge. It is designed to be experienced once, in sequence, the way a short story is. This is a deliberate design position: the game doesn't want players to optimise or replay; it wants them to experience the arc once and then think about it. For games discourse in 2015, this was a rejection of almost every convention of what games were supposed to offer — replayability, difficulty, player agency — in favour of treating the medium as a vehicle for a specific emotional journey that should happen once.

**Personal games as expanded design vocabulary.** Freeman's work on Cibele (alongside her earlier short games like Freshman Year and How Do You Do It?) established a practice of personal game-making that influenced a generation of indie developers. The key design move is using the game's authorial position as a feature rather than an abstraction: when players know the game is autobiographical, it changes how they receive every detail — the real photos carry more weight, the mundane desktop contents feel more intimate. This is a design technique unavailable to purely fictional games, and Cibele demonstrates how autobiography can be a formal advantage rather than just a content category.
