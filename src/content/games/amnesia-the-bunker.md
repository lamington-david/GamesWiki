## Design Notes

**From scripted to emergent horror.** Thomas Grip and Fredrik Olsson's key design shift for The Bunker was abandoning the scripted, linear horror Frictional Games had used across four games in favour of systemic design. Where previous Amnesia titles placed monsters in authored locations and triggered scares at predetermined moments, The Bunker's monster — the Beast — emerges from player actions and responds dynamically to light and sound. This changes the nature of fear from anticipation of authored events to uncertainty about emergent ones: the player cannot know when the Beast will appear, only what conditions make it more or less likely. The design insight is that player-generated threat is more frightening than designer-placed threat.

**The generator as tension engine.** The Bunker's central mechanic is a generator that must be periodically refuelled to maintain light, creates noise when started, and attracts the Beast. The flashlight also emits sound when charged, creating a layered resource problem where maintaining visibility is itself a risk. This is an example of how a single mechanic — the generator — can serve multiple design functions simultaneously: pacing, resource management, risk assessment, and audio atmosphere. The best systemic horror design gives the player constant decisions with no risk-free option, and the generator achieves this with minimal complexity.

**Non-linear level design in a horror context.** Previous Amnesia games used linear corridors that players traversed once. The Bunker gives the player a single large interconnected map, traversed freely with objectives tackled in any order. This changes horror's relationship to space: the player must learn the map, not just follow it, creating a different kind of dread from returning to known locations that are now dangerous in new ways. Frictional's insight was that knowing a space and still fearing it — rather than fearing unfamiliar space — is a more sophisticated horror design goal, because it relies on the player's investment in familiar environments rather than on novelty.

**Lying to players as design honesty.** Olsson's argument in the gamedeveloper.com interview is that games can benefit from deliberately misleading players about how systems work — not to frustrate them but to preserve the emotional experience intended. Accurate telegraphing of what a horror game will do always undermines the horror, because horror depends on uncertainty. False signals — sounds that sometimes do not mean danger, visual cues that do not always predict events — maintain the emotional state the design depends on. This is an argument that player information and player experience are sometimes in direct conflict, and that the game should resolve that conflict in favour of experience.

**Adaptive difficulty without undermining horror.** The Bunker offers adjustable difficulty settings that alter monster behaviour and resource availability. Frictional's decision to provide these controls reflected their understanding that horror is deeply personal — what terrifies one player is manageable for another — and that difficulty options do not undermine horror if the systemic foundation is strong. The game's core tension survived even on easier settings, demonstrating that emergent horror is more resilient to difficulty adjustment than scripted horror: when the systems generate fear rather than authored events, changing system parameters does not cancel the fear, it modulates it.
