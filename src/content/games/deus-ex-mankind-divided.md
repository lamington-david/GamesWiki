## Design Notes

**Player archetype design in a hub city.** Before designing Prague, Eidos-Montréal's level design team identified distinct player archetypes based on what players actually do in open environments: explorers who investigate every corner, completionists who follow waypoints, story-followers who move through the critical path, and combat-seekers who look for conflict. Prague was then designed to give each type a distinct lane through the same space. The explorer finds hidden apartments, overheard conversations, and rewards for breaking expected paths. The completionist can follow markers through a coherent city. The story player gets a legible critical path. These lanes run through the same geometry without interfering — which is harder to build than it sounds, because a space that rewards exploration must still be navigable, and a space that guides completionists must still feel exploratory.

**Readable objects and untethered environmental narrative.** One of Prague's key design techniques was filling the world with what the team called "readable objects" — items, clues, and environmental details that tell stories not connected to any mission or quest. An evicted aug's belongings in the street, protest graffiti with its own internal logic, apartments with traces of lives interrupted — these elements make the world feel inhabited without demanding the player engage with them. The narrative value comes from their presence, not from a system pointing at them. This is environmental storytelling used as world texture rather than as tutorial — designed for the player who wanders, not the player who follows prompts.

**Deep pockets of exploration.** Prague was built with what Maurer called "deep pockets" — areas that required deliberate effort to discover and rewarded that effort with items, lore, or access. These pockets are not randomly distributed: they are placed to make the world feel like it has a consistent interior logic, where effort always yields something proportional. The technique handles a core tension in hub design: spaces that are too legible feel gamey and artificial; spaces that are too opaque feel arbitrary and punishing. Deep pockets give explorers a reason to push without making the space inaccessible to players who don't.

**Social commentary as systems design.** The game's central theme — augmented humans facing segregation and state violence, with aug passports and separate metro carriages — was not expressed primarily through cutscenes but through the systems of the world itself. Prague has aug-only districts. NPCs react differently to the player's visible augmentations. The environment carries the ideology. This approach — where a game's political themes are embedded in how the world works rather than delivered through dialogue — is substantially harder to design than conventional narrative integration, but it means players who ignore the cutscenes still experience the theme through mechanics.

**The cost of systemic ambition at AAA scale.** Mankind Divided is an instructive example of how ambitious hub design creates production challenges at scale. Prague is extraordinarily dense and coherent; the game's second major location is considerably less developed. The team's effort to build a truly systemic, multi-archetype hub consumed a disproportionate share of production resources, leaving other elements underserved. This is a recurring pattern in systemically ambitious AAA games: the design goals are sound, but the resource demands of executing them at quality are higher than conventional level design, and something often has to be sacrificed. Understanding this trade-off is as important as understanding the techniques.
