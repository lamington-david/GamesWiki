## Design Notes

**Cultural mythology as mechanic source.** The monsters in Detention — hungry ghosts drawn from Buddhist and Taoist folklore — are not aesthetic choices layered onto standard horror enemy design. They determine how the avoidance mechanics work. Hungry ghosts are governed by specific ritual logic: they respond to sound, to the smell of incense, to prayer and ritual objects. When Detention asks players to navigate around these enemies using incense burners and holding their breath, it is translating actual folk belief into interaction design. The team asked themselves "how can we combine culture with horror game mechanism?" and dug into East Asian mythology to find answers that had design consequences, not just aesthetic ones. This produces the distinctive Detention experience of horror that feels ethnographically coherent rather than borrowed from Western genre conventions.

**Historical trauma as the setting's load-bearing wall.** The White Terror period — 38 years of martial law during which approximately 4,000 people were executed for perceived opposition to the Chinese Nationalist government — is not background colour for Detention but the architectural logic of its world. The school setting, the military presence, the atmosphere of perpetual surveillance and unspoken accusation: these are all conditions of the historical moment, not invented horror conventions. Red Candle Games made a design commitment that the horror of the game should emerge from the historical reality of life under political terror rather than from supernatural threat that merely uses the setting as a backdrop. The result is a game that can disturb players even after the mechanics are understood, because the discomfort is historical rather than procedural.

**Localization as design revision, not translation.** Red Candle Games describe four major revisions to Detention's design over its two-year development — each one reshaping the game's cultural references to make them accessible to non-Taiwanese players without replacing them with generic equivalents. This is a different problem from translation: an enemy design rooted in specific folk mythology requires the player to understand enough of that mythology to recognise why it is threatening. The team developed a practice of identifying which cultural elements were legible to outsiders through visual language alone, which required contextual explanation through environmental detail, and which needed to be redesigned to communicate their function without cultural prerequisite. This process produced a game that taught Taiwanese history through play rather than exposition.

**Silent Hill as a design model.** Coffee Yao cited Silent Hill as a primary reference for Detention's puzzle logic — specifically the practice of designing puzzles that operate according to in-world logic rather than puzzle-game abstraction. A door that requires a ritual object to open is more frightening than one that requires a key, because the ritual object implies a belief system that gives the threat meaning. Detention applies this principle consistently: the solutions to its puzzles are actions that would make sense to a person who believed in the folk religion the game depicts, not actions that make sense only within a puzzle-game frame. This alignment of mechanic and world-logic is what allows the game to maintain tonal consistency even when it is asking the player to solve problems.

**Six-person debut studio development under constraints.** Red Candle Games was founded by six people from different backgrounds who initially funded Detention themselves while taking outsourced work to survive financially. The scope of the game — a short, atmospheric horror experience rather than an open-world epic — reflects this constraint, but the constraint produced a design advantage: the team was forced to achieve their effect through density and specificity rather than content volume. Every environment in Detention carries multiple layers of historical, cultural, and mechanical meaning because the team could not afford to have environments that did only one thing. The game's legacy as a design reference is inseparable from the economic conditions that shaped its scope.
