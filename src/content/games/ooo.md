## Design Notes

**One mechanic, fully explored.** Öoo is built on a single interaction: place a bomb with one button, detonate it with another. The caterpillar cannot jump. That constraint forces every traversal and puzzle challenge to emerge from bomb physics — vertical launches, horizontal blasts, temporary wall destruction, chain detonations. Takahashi has described his goal as "thoroughly experimenting with a single mechanic" while allowing a wide range of gameplay experiences within manageable development scope. The result is a game that never introduces a new verb but continuously finds new things to say with the one it has. By the end, players who entered thinking "I place bombs" have been transformed into players who think in terms of arc trajectories, detonation timing, and terrain geometry.

**Visual language over text explanation.** Öoo contains no tutorials. New mechanics — and there are many within the single-mechanic frame — are introduced by demonstrating their effects in the environment before requiring the player to replicate them. A bomb is shown near a wall; the player is shown what happens when it explodes; the player is then placed in a context where they must apply that knowledge. Takahashi has cited this "show don't tell" approach as a design commitment rather than a production shortcut. The game trusts that players can read spatial information — that seeing a breakable wall texture, a height difference, or a waiting gap correctly implies the solution — and designs every teaching moment around that trust. When the game is working, the player never notices they were taught.

**Constraint as creative amplifier.** The removal of the jump is the game's most important design decision. Jumping is the default traversal verb in platformers; its absence makes every elevation change into a puzzle. The bomb fill that absence means that gaining height requires forethought — positioning a bomb correctly, timing the detonation to the character's movement — rather than the reflexive muscle memory that traditional platforming develops. This constraint also prevents the game from becoming a pure reflex game: you cannot "spam" your way through, because each bomb requires deliberate placement. The design shows how removing a player expectation, rather than adding a new feature, can be the more interesting design move.

**Dense, intentionally short.** Öoo's campaign takes approximately 2-3 hours to complete. This is a deliberate scope target, not a budget limitation. Takahashi designs for experiences where the player's understanding deepens rapidly and then completes — where the game has said everything it wanted to say. Japanese indie developers frequently discuss the difficulty of estimating development time on ambition-driven projects, and Takahashi's design-around-constraints approach is partly a response to that problem: a single mechanic explored fully is a project whose scope can be estimated, controlled, and delivered. The 2-3 hour runtime also reflects a respect for the player's time — the game never pads, never retreads, never introduces complexity for variety's sake when depth-within-the-existing is available.

**ElecHead lineage and the value of iterating on a philosophy.** Öoo is Takahashi's follow-up to ElecHead (2021), in which a robot whose head is an electrical source must navigate environments where electrical charge both opens paths and triggers traps. Both games use the same design philosophy: one mechanic, no redundant verbs, physics-consistent consequences, short-but-dense runtimes. Öoo represents a refinement rather than a reinvention — the bomb mechanic has richer spatial dimensions than electrical charge, and the dungeon setting allows for more varied terrain geometry than ElecHead's lab environments. The games form a practice: Takahashi using each project to deepen his understanding of how to build a complete experience from a single design commitment, rather than accumulating mechanics across titles.
