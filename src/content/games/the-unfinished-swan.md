## Design Notes

**The blank canvas as an opening argument.** The Unfinished Swan begins in pure white. There are no visible objects, no horizon, no reference points — only whiteness in every direction. The player has a single action: throw a paint-filled ball. The game's opening argument is that exploration itself is the mechanic, and that revelation is a form of reward. The white void is not a loading screen or an aesthetic choice layered over conventional design — it is the game's foundational premise that the world must be earned through action, and that seeing what is already there is a form of creation. This is a design decision that only makes sense as a first-person game; no other perspective could sustain the perceptual ambiguity that makes it work.

**Prototype-driven development.** The Unfinished Swan originated in a USC student exercise where Ian Dallas made a new interactive prototype each week for his faculty advisor. The white-room paint-throwing prototype was one such exercise — not a complete design concept, but a single mechanic that felt surprising enough to warrant further exploration. The decision to build a commercial game from this prototype rather than designing the game first and then implementing mechanics is the production lesson Dallas has been most vocal about. The game's identity — exploration, revelation, blank-slate discovery — emerged from protecting and elaborating a mechanic that already worked, rather than from a design document that preceded the prototype.

**Tone as a design constraint.** Dallas has described the game's creative process as building toward a tone rather than a narrative. The team knew what they wanted the game to feel like — melancholy, wonder, slightly unsettling warmth — before they knew what it was about. The environments and traversal mechanics were designed to produce that feeling, and the fairytale story of a boy chasing a swan into an unfinished kingdom was added afterward as a narrative justification for worlds that already existed. This inversion — aesthetic precedes story — is an unusual development method that produced an unusually coherent game, where every surface and every mechanic reinforces the same emotional register.

**The cost of prototype-to-commercial translation.** Dallas's GDC account of The Unfinished Swan's production is candid about the structural difficulties of taking a two-person student project to a twelve-person commercial release. The most significant lesson was the critical need for a dedicated producer — someone whose entire role is managing interpersonal friction, shielding the creative team from operational pressures, and preventing the small conflicts that idle a studio. Dallas describes this as 'grenade-jumping': the producer identifies the explosive situations and lands on them before they detonate the team. Small studios that grow without producing infrastructure almost always discover this lesson the hard way, and The Unfinished Swan's production was no exception.

**Radical minimalism as a commercial risk.** The Unfinished Swan is short, deliberately opaque at the outset, and uses abstract mechanics that do not immediately communicate their logic. These qualities made it difficult to pitch and difficult to market, and they produced a Steam rating that reflects the polarised reaction of players who expected a conventional game. The design lesson is double-edged: radical minimalism can produce genuine wonder in players who are receptive, but it also generates frustrated or confused responses in players who aren't. Giant Sparrow's follow-up, What Remains of Edith Finch, navigated this tension by using conventional narrative structure as a scaffold for experimental mechanics, allowing accessibility and strangeness to coexist. The Unfinished Swan remains the purer, harder version of the same creative vision.
