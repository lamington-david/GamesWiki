## Design Notes

**Action blocks as a rapid prototyping discipline.** Respawn's Christopher Dionne developed the action block methodology after early Titanfall 2 campaign levels felt derivative — comfortable but uninspired. An action block is a discrete, playable prototype of a single gameplay concept, built quickly and tested in isolation before being integrated into a level. The critical insight is that building full levels to test a concept wastes too much production time to allow genuine iteration; action blocks let the team try twenty ideas at low cost to find the two that warrant full development. This methodological shift is what produced the campaign's extraordinary level variety — each mission has a genuinely novel mechanical premise because the team was able to prototype and discard concepts at speed.

**Movement as the foundation of all other design.** Titanfall 2's wall-running, double-jump, and slide mechanics aren't cosmetic additions to a conventional FPS — they're the constraint around which every level is built. Spaces are designed with multiple vertical layers, traversal paths that reward reading the geometry, and enemy placements that make movement combat rather than just movement. The mobility system creates a game where positional thinking replaces cover thinking: the optimal play is rarely to stop and wait, and level geometry is designed to reward players who treat it as a three-dimensional flow puzzle rather than a flat shooting gallery. Movement design as a foundational layer, not a feature, is the principle that separates Titanfall 2's environments from conventional FPS level design.

**"Effect and Cause" as the canonical level design case study.** The time-switch mechanic in "Effect and Cause" — a device that moves the player between two temporal states of the same space — has become a reference point for how abstract mechanical concepts can be translated into spatial experiences. The level works because the two states are legible from each other: the player can see where the same corridor will be in the past, can plan crossings with reference to both states, can learn the space through temporal layering. This is not a gimmick but a complete spatial design system explored in depth across a single level. It demonstrates the principle that the best level design conceits are those that create genuinely new ways of reading a space.

**Titan/pilot asymmetry as pacing architecture.** The alternation between pilot (fast, mobile, small-scale) and Titan (slow, powerful, large-scale) combat isn't just tonal variety — it's a pacing system that gives the player rhythmic relief from each mode's demands. Tight pilot sections where the mobility system is at full expression alternate with Titan encounters where the scale of engagement completely changes. The two modes feel categorically different, and that categorical difference is the point: each one makes the player miss the other, and the return to each state feels like reunion rather than repetition. The design lesson is that contrast can be a pacing tool as effective as conventional difficulty curves.

**Commercial timing versus design quality.** Titanfall 2 launched in October 2016 between Battlefield 1 and Call of Duty: Infinite Warfare, a release window widely attributed to EA's publishing decisions rather than Respawn's preference. The game's commercial underperformance relative to its critical reception offers a case study in how launch timing affects a game's audience even when the design is excellent — and how a game's long-term reputation can diverge dramatically from its initial commercial performance. The campaign is now frequently cited as one of the best in the genre, with a player community that grew years after release. For designers, it raises questions about how publishing decisions shape what lessons the industry draws from a game's results.
