## Design Notes

**The mechanic is also the aesthetic experience.** In most games, mechanics serve narrative or aesthetic goals; the experience of pressing a button is instrumental to something else. In Wilmot's Warehouse, the act of sorting tiles — deciding where to put a picture of a crab, whether it belongs with sea creatures or with orange things or with things that pinch — is both the game's central mechanic and its primary pleasure. Richard Hogg's description of the design process emphasises that he drew the 500 tiles before designing the game around them, which is the source of this quality: the tiles have genuine categorical richness (things that are yellow, things that are alive, things that are tools, things that are associated with summer) because they were conceived as a taxonomy first and game assets second.

**Organisation systems are personal and therefore memorable.** The game does not tell the player where to put anything. The player's filing system — grouping tiles by colour, by association, by narrative logic — is entirely self-determined. This has a mechanical consequence that is also the core design insight: you can only retrieve quickly what you remember having placed, which means your retrieval performance is a test of your own memory of your own decisions. Players who build an idiosyncratic but internally consistent system perform better than players who try to be "objectively correct." This makes Wilmot's Warehouse a rare case of a game where cognitive investment is mechanically rewarded in a direct, legible way.

**Co-operative play as a taxonomy negotiation problem.** The two-player mode introduces a design problem not present in solo play: two people building one filing system must align their organisational logic, or retrieval becomes impossible. If one player puts hammers with tools and the other puts hammers with things that are hard, the system breaks. The game does not resolve this conflict for you — it just makes it apparent when retrieval fails. This is a genuine design insight about collaboration: shared systems require shared mental models, and the cost of misalignment is concrete and immediate. Hogg and Haggett describe the co-op mode as emerging naturally from the single-player concept once they noticed that the organisational disagreements were the most interesting conversations the game created.

**The retrieval half designs itself.** Wilmot's Warehouse has two phases in each session: a warehouse period where you receive and file stock, and a customer service period where you retrieve specific items against a timer. The game does not need to design the retrieval phase in isolation — the difficulty of retrieval is entirely determined by the quality and consistency of your earlier filing. This creates a design where the player authors their own difficulty curve: a meticulously organised warehouse is easy to navigate under pressure; a chaotic one is a memory test. The elegance here is that the game creates high stakes without needing adversarial design — the challenge is self-generated.

**Mundane work as design space.** Wilmot's Warehouse belongs to a small category of games that treat work — not stylised combat or puzzle-solving, but the literal physical activity of organisation and retrieval — as the site of pleasure. Hogg's stated inspiration includes time spent working in a warehouse and a picture archive. The game's design argues that the satisfaction of a well-organised system is a genuine emotional state worth designing for, and that games are an unusual medium for exploring it because they can make the organisation consequential through time pressure and stakes. This is a wider design opportunity than Wilmot's Warehouse alone claims — the pleasure of a well-run system is available to game design wherever physical organisation, categorisation, or logistics appear.
