## Design Notes

**Hiring expertise from the community you're designing for.** nWay's most significant design decision on Battle for the Grid was hiring Daniel "Clockw0rk" Maniago — one of Marvel vs. Capcom 2's most celebrated competitive players — as Lead Combat Designer. This brought not just mechanical knowledge but community credibility: Clockw0rk understood from the inside what the fighting game community would accept or reject, what depth looked like at high level, and how competitive players evaluate a new system. The lesson is that for games targeting an existing community with established standards, design expertise from inside that community is qualitatively different from design expertise acquired from outside it.

**Designing for two audiences simultaneously.** The Power Rangers license guarantees an audience of fans who have never played a fighting game seriously. The FGC is a separate audience of players who evaluate every new fighter against Street Fighter, Tekken, and Marvel vs. Capcom. Battle for the Grid attempted to serve both by layering the system: simple inputs produce functional and satisfying combos accessible to new players, while the tag system, assist mechanics, and character archetypes create depth available to experienced players who seek it. Steve Kuroki explicitly designed the button-mashing experience to be fun rather than punished, rejecting the common fighting game philosophy of making uninformed inputs feel bad.

**Character archetypes as depth management.** The roster was designed with deliberate complexity gradients. Simpler characters have strong normals and clear combo routes that require less execution investment. Complex characters reward players who invest in learning their specific mechanics, with more expressive combo trees and assist synergies. This creates a natural on-ramp: new players gravitate toward the legible archetypes, spend time in the system, and are drawn toward more complex characters as their understanding grows. The design challenge is ensuring that simple characters remain viable at high level rather than becoming obvious stepping stones.

**Cross-play as design constraint.** Battle for the Grid launched with cross-play between PlayStation, Xbox, and Switch, which was unusual at the time and required platform negotiations that nWay treated as a design requirement rather than a feature request. For a fighting game, player population directly determines the quality of the experience: matchmaking degrades as communities fragment across platforms. This is a design lesson about identifying the conditions that make your game viable and treating them as non-negotiable from pre-production rather than as post-launch additions.

**Licensed games and mechanical identity.** The Power Rangers IP brings visual language, character expectations, and audience familiarity, but it does not bring combat design. Clockw0rk's stated goal was to lean into "crazy rather than conservative" — big damage, spectacular Zord supers, mixup-heavy pressure that matches the kinetic energy of the source material. This is a specific approach to licensed game design: rather than building a competent generic fighter and applying the license cosmetically, the team tried to make the license's visual identity define the mechanical register. The result is a game that feels like Power Rangers rather than a game that happens to have Power Rangers in it.
