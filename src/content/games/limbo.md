## Design Notes

**Death as the primary teaching mechanism.** LIMBO has no tutorial, no instructional text, and no explicit explanation of its mechanics. The game teaches through lethal failure: players encounter a trap, die, and understand the trap's logic through the death animation. Playdead designed every death to be instructional — the cause of death must be visually readable from the corpse's location, and the retry point must be close enough that the learned knowledge can be immediately applied. This is distinct from "punishing" death in action games; LIMBO's death is pedagogical. The design implication is that if a player dies without understanding why, the level design has failed, not the player.

**Ambiguity as creative position.** Arnt Jensen's founding goal for LIMBO was a game that people could interpret rather than understand — one that provided mood, imagery, and incident but withheld explanation. This is an unusual design position: most games are careful to establish clear narrative premises, but LIMBO deliberately provides none. The result is that LIMBO's story is whatever each player constructs from the imagery. This ambiguity is not the absence of design but the product of it: every visual element, puzzle, and encounter was designed to be open to interpretation rather than closed by explanation. The design lesson is that withholding explanation is a creative choice, not a limitation.

**Minimalism across every design layer.** The monochromatic silhouette aesthetic wasn't purely artistic — it was a design constraint applied consistently across every layer of the game. Two movement controls (jump and grab). No health bar. No score. No dialogue. No colour. The restraint was both technical (small team, limited budget) and creative: by limiting the number of elements in play at any moment, every element becomes more significant. The boy's white eyes against blackness communicate more emotion than a detailed face model would. This practice of achieving expressiveness through omission rather than addition — making each remaining element carry more weight by removing competing elements — is one of LIMBO's most transferable design lessons.

**Physics consistency as puzzle foundation.** Every puzzle in LIMBO operates from the same physical rules — gravity, mass, buoyancy, momentum. The game introduces no magical or arbitrary mechanics; it only introduces new objects whose physical properties create new puzzle types. This consistency means players never face a puzzle they can't solve with the knowledge they've already accumulated. The progressive difficulty comes from new combinations of known rules rather than new rules. Designing puzzles from a consistent physical system — where the player can reason from first principles — produces intellectual satisfaction that arbitrary puzzle logic cannot. The player feels clever rather than informed.

**Environmental storytelling without set-dressing.** LIMBO contains no dialogue, no text, and no cutscenes. Its environmental storytelling operates through the sequence and juxtaposition of settings: a forest, then industrial machinery, then a spider, then something deeper and stranger. Each zone change communicates narrative movement without explaining it. The transition from natural to industrial environments is a story beat told through the change in level geometry. This technique — using environment type as emotional register and transition as narrative — is available to any game, but LIMBO demonstrates it at its purest because there is nothing else in the game to carry narrative weight. The environment is the only storyteller available.
