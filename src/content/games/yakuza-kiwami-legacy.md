## Design Notes

**Remake as creative reckoning.** The development team described Yakuza Kiwami as "revenge on our ten-year-old selves" — a project to demonstrate how much better they could execute the original game's ideas with the technology, resources, and design experience accumulated over the decade since. This framing positions the remake not as a nostalgia product or a remaster but as an honest critique of the original work, followed by an attempt to correct it. The design challenge is different from making a new game: the team had to identify which elements of the original were essential to preserve, which were limitations masquerading as design choices, and which were genuinely wrong decisions that the original fanbase had learned to love. All three categories exist in Kiwami.

**Majima Everywhere as systemic character expansion.** In the original 2005 Yakuza, Majima Goro appears infrequently and has limited screen time despite being one of the series' most beloved characters. Kiwami introduced the "Majima Everywhere" system — Majima appears throughout Kamurocho in disguise and out in the open, seeking out Kiryu to fight him in order to keep his skills sharp. These encounters range from ambushes to elaborate set-pieces, and completing them deepens both Kiryu's combat abilities and the player's understanding of Majima's psychology. The system solves multiple design problems simultaneously: it addresses the original's underuse of a compelling character, provides a mechanical reason for Kiryu's fighting style to evolve during the story, and makes the open world feel more dynamically occupied.

**The problem of the returning player.** Kiwami launched after Yakuza 0, which had introduced many new Western players to the series and established specific expectations for the franchise's production quality, open-world density, and tonal register. Players coming to Kiwami from Yakuza 0 encountered a game set in a smaller, less detailed Kamurocho — the 1995 version of the district — with shorter side content and a story that the localization team had to make comprehensible to newcomers while not patronising to returnees. The design and localization challenge of a remake that must simultaneously function as an original experience and a sequel to its own prequel is one of the more unusual creative problems in the franchise's history.

**Localisation as character design.** Scott Strichart's work on Yakuza Kiwami's English localisation demonstrates how translation choices are fundamentally character design decisions. Every line of Kiryu's dialogue had to be consistent with both the character's established voice in Yakuza 0 and the original 2005 game's conception of him — but the original English release of Yakuza 1 was widely considered unsuccessful. The Atlus team essentially rebuilt the English version of Kiryu's characterisation from scratch, using Yakuza 0's characterisation as their baseline and working backwards into the 1995 timeline. This means Kiwami's English script is not a translation of the Japanese but a deliberately constructed character document that happens to correspond to the Japanese original.

**Kamurocho 1995.** Where Yakuza 0 depicts Kamurocho during the bubble economy of the 1980s — opulent, overlit, financially delirious — Kiwami's 1995 Kamurocho is quieter and more worn. The design team rebuilt the same fundamental district with different environmental emphasis, reflecting the post-bubble deflation and the beginning of the "lost decade." The setting's historical specificity is not decorative; the story of Kiryu's emergence from prison into a changed Kamurocho is inseparable from the city's specific period character. RGG Studio's consistent commitment to locating stories in specific, accurately rendered moments of Japanese social history is what gives their remakes a textural authenticity that transcends production quality.
