## Design Notes

**Proximity voice chat as a design system, not a feature.** The most transformative decision in Lethal Company is treating spatial audio as a game mechanic rather than a communication convenience. Voice attenuates with distance, echoes in tunnels, cuts out mid-sentence when a creature takes a player. This turns every player's narration of events into a horror instrument — the scream that gets swallowed mid-word tells the rest of the team more than a full explanation would. Designing the comms layer as diegetic and spatially honest means player communication becomes part of the game's horror vocabulary.

**The quota as a compression mechanic.** The looming quota — a credit target that grows with each deadline — imposes a persistent time pressure that shapes every mission. Players who might otherwise play cautiously, learn the map, and retreat safely are forced to push further and take greater risks as the deadline approaches. This is elegant systemic design: a single number creates pacing, escalates tension, and motivates the risk-taking behaviour that produces the game's best moments. The quota doesn't tell players what to do; it creates the conditions under which players choose to do interesting things.

**Emergent horror over scripted horror.** Lethal Company's creatures use behavioural AI designed to produce unpredictable, memorable encounters rather than consistent horror set pieces. The Bracken follows players and retreats when looked at directly; the Forest Keeper's enormous scale and distant footsteps create dread before it arrives. None of these produce horror reliably — but they produce horror authentically. When a creature kills a player in a way that no one expected, the group's collective reaction becomes the horror moment. The game acts as a stage director, not a storyteller.

**Procedural generation in service of social play.** The abandoned moon layouts are procedurally generated but within tight constraints that ensure navigability and threat placement. The unpredictability of each layout means that even familiar moons require the group to re-learn, communicate, and divide responsibilities differently each time. This keeps co-op communication necessary rather than optional — groups that have played a hundred hours together still need to talk, because the information the game provides is always new.

**Solo development as design constraint.** Built by a single developer over roughly ten years, Lethal Company's scope was necessarily constrained to systems that could be implemented by one person. This constraint produced a game that is mechanically simple but systemically deep — a small number of interacting systems (the quota, the creatures, the equipment, the ship) that produce enormous variety. The game is a case study in how tight scope and strong systemic design can substitute for the content volume that larger teams produce.
