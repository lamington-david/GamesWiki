## Design Notes

**Remaking as completion, not repetition.** The most significant design decision in Kiwami 2 was not to upgrade the original game but to rebuild it as a different object. The Dragon Engine — developed for Yakuza 6 — enabled seamless transitions between exploration, combat, and cutscenes in a single continuous environment without load screens, real-time geometry that reacts to combat, and a level of character fidelity that fundamentally changed how story moments could be presented. RGG Studio's position was that if the original engine couldn't support the vision for a scene, the scene should be redesigned rather than compromised. The Kiwami remakes represent a philosophy that a great game's source material deserves realisation at the highest technical level available, not just a cleaned-up version of the original.

**The Majima Saga as structural argument for remake additions.** Kiwami 2 added three entirely new chapters — the Majima Saga — that address the narrative gap between Yakuza and Yakuza 2: specifically, why Majima resigned from the Tojo Clan. This content did not exist in any previous version of the game. The addition demonstrates a particular theory of remake design: that a remake's obligation to the source extends beyond fidelity, and that remakes with development resources above a minimum threshold should use those resources to address unresolved questions in the original. The Majima Saga makes Kiwami 2 a more complete narrative object than the game it remakes, not just a better-looking version of it.

**World density in a constrained geography.** Like all Yakuza games, Kiwami 2 operates in two small urban districts — Kamurocho in Tokyo and Sotenbori in Osaka. Both can be walked across in under two minutes. Within these small maps, RGG Studio packs restaurants with real menus, substories with independent narrative arcs, minigames ranging from mahjong to hostess clubs, and NPCs with persistent schedules. The Dragon Engine's seamless environment meant that interiors that previously required loading screens were now directly accessible from the street, deepening the sense that Kamurocho is a real place rather than a game map. The lesson from the Yakuza series as a whole is that believable world density comes from authored detail per square metre, not from the size of the map.

**Combat system as parallel narrative.** The Dragon Engine introduced a new combat system that removed the style-switching mechanic from Yakuza 0 and replaced it with a single, escalating brawler framework. Environmental interactions — using furniture, incorporating location-specific objects into combos — were expanded to reflect the engine's new physical simulation capabilities. The combat in Kiwami 2 communicates the same tonal point as the broader game: Kiryu is a man who handles problems with extreme physical directness in environments that feel genuinely inhabited. Combat design in the Yakuza series is inseparable from its role as an expression of character and setting.

**The franchise entry-point problem solved by chronology.** Kiwami 2 arrived in the West at a moment when Yakuza 0's critical success had created a large new audience familiar with Kiryu's origins. This meant that Kiwami 2's design could assume more narrative knowledge than the original 2006 game could — players who had been through Yakuza 0 and Kiwami 1 arrived at Kiwami 2 with a full understanding of who these characters are and why the stakes matter. The Kiwami series demonstrates how a franchise can use chronological remakes to simultaneously honour existing fans and systematically onboard new ones — the remake schedule matches the recommended play order for new players, making the release calendar itself a form of franchise design.
