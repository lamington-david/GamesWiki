## Design Notes

**Exclusion as the core mechanic.** We Know the Devil is built around a deceptively simple structural device: in each chapter, the player chooses a scene involving two of the three protagonists while the third is left out. This choice is not between good and bad options — it is between relationships. Whoever is excluded in each chapter accumulates a wound that feeds into the ending the player receives. The mechanic maps the social experience of adolescent group dynamics — the painful arithmetic of who gets left out — directly onto choice architecture. The design lesson is that abstract social experiences can sometimes be expressed more precisely through structural mechanics than through narrative description alone.

**The fourth path as design statement.** The game has four endings: one for each possible pairing of two characters, and a true ending unlocked only by a specific sequence that doesn't consistently favour any single relationship. The true ending requires a player who refuses to simply invest in one pairing — it rewards navigating all three relationships without fully prioritising any one of them. This inverts the usual logic of visual novel routes, where completionists play through each branch in isolation. In We Know the Devil, the final truth is reached by rejecting the binary logic of pairing and exclusion that the game's structure otherwise enforces. The design argument is embedded in the structure itself.

**Queer narrative as design context, not decoration.** The game's protagonists are queer, and their relationships operate within a specifically queer register — the tension between hiding and revealing, the stakes of intimacy within hostile institutional environments, the way religious shame attaches to ordinary closeness. These aren't setting details bolted onto a generic structure; they inform the emotional weight of the exclusion mechanic. A choice about who to spend time with carries different freight when that closeness is itself transgressive. Designers interested in how identity and social context can recharge standard mechanics should study how We Know the Devil achieves this without the queer content feeling tokenistic.

**Visual identity from production constraint.** Mia Schwartz's art for We Know the Devil composites character portraits over desaturated photographs of woods and cabins — a production decision (original photography was cheaper than illustrated environments) that became an aesthetic signature. The photographic backgrounds give the game an uncanny, liminal quality that reinforces its horror register. The synth score by Alec Lambert completes the effect. This is a useful case study in how budget constraints, addressed honestly rather than fought, can produce distinctive work: the lo-fi aesthetic became part of what makes the game memorable rather than a mark of limitation.

**Writing as architecture.** Aevee Bee's dialogue for We Know the Devil serves two functions simultaneously: naturalistic teenage speech and a system of meaningful state changes. Because the game tracks which characters have been together and which have been excluded across all chapters, every line must work as dramatic writing and as information that feeds into the ending logic. This double function — where every piece of dialogue is both plausible in the scene and active in the system — is one of the game's genuine design achievements, and one of the harder things to replicate. Writing that only serves the drama, or writing that only serves the system, will feel thin in one direction or the other.
