## Design Notes

**Safe haven architecture as the primary horror tool.** Look Outside's design is built on a single spatial contrast: the apartment is safe, outside is not. Sam's apartment is where you sleep, cook, shower, craft, and manage your survival stats in familiar domestic activities. Everything beyond that boundary is where the monsters are. This binary — domestic safety vs. incomprehensible outside — does more horror work than any individual creature encounter. The apartment becomes precious, and the game generates dread by threatening to breach it rather than by sending the player into danger at every moment.

**Game jam constraints as design direction.** The entire game was built in one month for the Hawktober Horrors 2024 game jam. That constraint forced Coulombe toward a tight, vertical slice: one apartment building, turn-based RPG combat he could implement quickly, and creature designs achievable in pixel art with limited animation budgets. When Devolver Digital approached him about a commercial release, the jam version's scope was already defined. The lesson is that constraints imposed by game jams can produce designs that are fully coherent precisely because they're bounded — there's no scope creep because there's no time for it.

**Sweet Home as a structural blueprint.** Coulombe has cited the 1989 Famicom RPG Sweet Home as a direct inspiration — specifically its combination of survival horror and turn-based RPG mechanics in a confined location. Sweet Home was notable for making item management emotionally meaningful by attaching characters to specific tools; if a character died, their unique item was gone. Look Outside inherits this approach: the scarcity of food, hygiene supplies, and craft materials makes survival stats feel personal rather than merely numerical. The RPG layer isn't bolted on — it's the inheritance of a specific design lineage.

**Creature design balancing horror and absurdity.** Critics have noted that Look Outside's creatures are both genuinely disturbing and frequently darkly comic. Coulombe's pixel art approach makes this balance achievable: detailed enough to convey body horror, abstract enough to leave the imagination room to fill in gaps. The absurdity is intentional — Lovecraftian horror is traditionally effective when the incomprehensible is filtered through mundane contexts, and an apartment building full of former neighbours provides exactly that mundane frame. The comedy in the creature designs releases tension in a controlled way rather than undercutting the horror.

**Solo development as quality signal.** Look Outside was made by one developer with help from indie developer friends for polish — a genuinely small team that produced a game with high enough quality to attract a major indie publisher. The solo origin is legible in the design's tightness: there are no systems included that weren't necessary, no areas included that don't serve the spatial contrast, no mechanical complexity beyond what the experience requires. This economy of design — every element earning its place — is easier to achieve when one person makes every decision and can feel every inconsistency.
