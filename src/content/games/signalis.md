## Design Notes

**Mechanics that mirror narrative themes.** SIGNALIS's six-slot inventory is not a concession to classic survival horror convention — it is a design that enacts the game's themes at the mechanical level. The constant pressure of limited carrying capacity, the forced decisions about what to keep and what to discard, mirrors the narrative's preoccupations with constraint, loss, and what remains when everything excess is stripped away. The inventory system and the story are doing the same work.

**The PS1 aesthetic as an active horror tool.** rose-engine chose fixed camera angles and low-poly environments not out of nostalgia but because the aesthetic produces specific horror effects that higher-fidelity rendering undermines. Limited draw distances create genuine uncertainty about what lies ahead. Fixed angles prevent the player from choosing their vantage point, generating anxiety from the impossibility of surveying a space. Graphical ambiguity — can't quite read the expression, can't quite see the shape in the corner — creates the uncanny more reliably than photorealistic detail. The "limitations" are features.

**Intertextual references as optional depth.** The game is in explicit dialogue with Silent Hill, Neon Genesis Evangelion, and works by Kubrick and Lynch. Players who recognise the references find additional interpretive layers; players who don't experience a complete and coherent work. This is a model for using cultural references purposefully: they must earn their place by adding meaning rather than just signalling taste, and the game must function without them.

**Ambiguity sustained to the ending.** SIGNALIS has multiple endings, but none of them resolve the narrative cleanly. The game refuses to deliver answers to the questions it raises about consciousness, identity, and whether the protagonist's suffering is "real" in a meaningful sense. This sustains the thematic work of the game beyond the closing screen: the discomfort the player is left with is the intended experience. Designing ambiguity without designing incoherence is the difficult craft here.

**Two-person scope as design discipline.** rose-engine is two people — Yuri Stern on code and design, Barbara Wittmann on art — who worked on SIGNALIS for eight years. Every constraint was real: fixed cameras reduce environment modeling requirements; the PS1 aesthetic constrains polygon counts; limited enemy types can be polished to exceptional quality. The studio's scale forced elegance in ways that additional resources might not have. The lesson is not that small teams are inherently better, but that hard constraints on scope — held to rigorously — produce tonal consistency that larger projects often lose to feature creep.
