## Design Notes

**Audio as the game's primary design language.** In most games, music accompanies the experience. In 140, music is the experience. Every platform, obstacle, enemy, and background element pulses, shifts, and behaves in synchrony with Jakob Schmid's four-part electronic score. The game's difficulty isn't measured in reaction time but in the player's ability to internalise the music and use it to anticipate what will happen next. Carlsen built 140 around a clear proposition: if players can hear the level, they can play the level. Sound is not decoration but information.

**Minimalism as functional clarity.** The game strips every visual element to its geometric minimum — coloured rectangles, circles, and polygons against flat backgrounds. This was not purely an aesthetic choice. By removing representational detail, Carlsen eliminated the visual noise that would compete with the musical signal. Players can devote their attention to the rhythm because there is nothing else competing for it. The lesson is that minimalist design is not sparse design — 140's visual language is precise and dense with meaning. It is minimalist in the sense that every element is necessary and nothing is present for decoration.

**Synesthesia by design.** The target experience Carlsen aimed for is synesthetic: the player should hear the level as they see it, and see the music as they play it. When this works, audio and visual information become unified — the player doesn't monitor both channels independently but experiences them as a single stream. Achieving this required strict timing discipline: every beat, every visual transition, every obstacle behaviour had to be quantised to the same grid. The technical constraint of perfect synchronisation was the prerequisite for the experiential goal of synesthesia.

**IGF origins and the off-hours development model.** 140 was developed by Carlsen during evenings and weekends while he was employed at Playdead as lead gameplay designer on Limbo. This context matters: the game's compactness and focus reflect the constraints of a project built in limited time, and its mechanical rigour reflects its author's professional background. The off-hours development model — building a personal project alongside commercial work — produced something that could not have emerged from a studio brief, precisely because it had no commercial obligations. Its IGF nomination in 2013 represents the recognition of what that model can produce.

**Level design as musical composition.** Each of 140's four levels corresponds to a distinct musical track, and the level architecture is designed in dialogue with the music rather than independently of it. Obstacles appear, change form, and resolve in ways that mirror the structure of the score — verse/chorus dynamics, build and release, call and response between different sound elements. For designers interested in the relationship between systems design and audio, 140 provides a rare case where the two disciplines are not working in parallel but are genuinely unified. The level is the composition.
