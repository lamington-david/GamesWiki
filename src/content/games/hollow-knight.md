## Design Notes

**World as space, not as levels.** Team Cherry describe their approach to Hollow Knight's geography not as designing a sequence of levels but as building outward from a space. This distinction is practical: a level is designed with entry, content, and exit; a space has neighbours, history, and ecological logic. Hallownest feels like a place that existed before the player arrived and will persist after they leave, because every area was designed around what it contains and why, not what experience it delivers. This approach produces the characteristic Hollow Knight effect — of stumbling into a new biome and feeling that you are trespassing on something that was not built for you.

**Opacity as a first-class design value.** Hollow Knight tells the player very little. There is almost no tutorial, the lore is fragmented and delivered through environmental detail and sparse NPC dialogue, and many of the game's upgrades are discovered rather than announced. This is not an oversight but a design philosophy: Gibson and Pellen consistently describe the experience of not knowing as the thing they were designing for. When the game's map system requires purchasing a cartographer's map for each new area, it is making a statement about the value of geographic knowledge and the pleasure of its accumulation. The game trusts players to tolerate uncertainty in exchange for a more complete feeling of discovery.

**Combat as a precise physical language.** Hollow Knight's combat is rooted in NES-era precision — the nail has strict reach and timing, the dodge has a definite duration, the parry window is tight. This precision creates a legible combat language that scales naturally with player skill: a beginner fights defensively at range; an expert fights aggressively close, using the nail bounce and soul mechanics as integrated tools. The boss encounters are designed around this language, with each boss requiring the player to apply the movement vocabulary they have learned in exploration to a constrained arena with higher stakes. The progression from "confused by the mechanic" to "in command of the mechanic" is the primary arc of Hollow Knight's difficulty.

**Environmental storytelling through ecological design.** Hollow Knight's lore is delivered almost entirely through environment and implication. The infection that has overtaken Hallownest is visible in textures, enemy behaviour, and the postures of idle NPCs before it is named or explained. The game uses what players call "environmental storytelling" in a technically specific way — the environment is designed to be a legible document that rewards attention. Fragments of story found in different areas resolve into a picture only for players who look at all of them. This approach allows the game to have a rich, complex narrative without requiring cutscenes or dialogue dumps, and it makes the act of exploration feel intellectually engaged rather than scenic.

**Scale achieved through cultural imagination.** Hollow Knight was made by three people on a budget of around $57,000 raised through Kickstarter. Its sense of scale comes not from content volume but from the feeling that the world continues beyond what the player can reach. The bug civilisation of Hallownest has cultural artefacts — theatre, banks, religious shrines, gladiatorial arenas — that imply an entire history without depicting it. This technique — populating a world with traces of civilisation rather than depicting the civilisation itself — is both economical and artistically effective. Team Cherry's achievement is not that they built a big game but that they built a game that feels bigger than it is.
