## Design Notes

**Escape from the room.** Uchikoshi's Zero Escape trilogy (999, Virtue's Last Reward, Zero Time Dilemma) was structurally committed to locked-room puzzles — every story beat happened in a confined space. In interviews, he noted that for AI: The Somnium Files he wanted to break out of that physical constraint: to show rain, the sky, characters moving through a city. This wasn't cosmetic. A detective story requires a world larger than any single room — suspects to pursue, locations to revisit, a sense that the crime exists within a society. The formal change forced genuine structural rethinking of how to make an Uchikoshi game that still felt like one.

**Dream spaces as alternate logic.** The game's Somnium sequences — in which protagonist Kaname Date dives into the dreams of witnesses and suspects to surface subconscious memories — create puzzle environments governed by internal dream logic rather than the rules of the waking world. These sequences work because they establish upfront that different rules apply, allowing abstract puzzles to feel tonally appropriate rather than arbitrary. The design insight is that a game can support radically different puzzle modes if it provides a diegetic justification for why the rules change. Dreams, as a culturally understood space of distortion and symbolic displacement, are a particularly rich container for this approach.

**The title as multilayered meaning.** The title "AI: The Somnium Files" encodes the game's themes at every reading. "AI" refers to the AI partner character Aiba, to artificial intelligence as a running theme, and — as Uchikoshi has noted explicitly — to the Japanese word 愛 (ai), meaning love. "Somnium" is Latin for dream. The title was designed to carry multiple simultaneous meanings, and this layering reflects a design philosophy evident throughout Uchikoshi's work: surface-level content that rewards scrutiny, where the game's intentions are embedded in its own structure before the player begins.

**Tonal oscillation.** The game deliberately alternates between extremely dark content — missing eyes, dismembered bodies, traumatic backstories — and broad comedy, particularly through the protagonist's constant perverse humor and a range of absurdist side characters. Uchikoshi has described the game's "color" as rainbow, where the extremity of each pole makes the other land harder. This tonal design is difficult to execute without whiplash: the technique requires that both registers be taken seriously on their own terms, not deployed as cheap contrast. AI: The Somnium Files succeeds largely because the comedy is characterful (the jokes reveal personality) rather than purely deflective (jokes used to avoid commitment to tone).

**Adventure game accessibility as design investment.** Uchikoshi built AI: The Somnium Files to include players unfamiliar with visual novel conventions — automatic text advancement, full voice acting, Somnium difficulty options that reduce time pressure. These features reflect a belief that the genre's best design (long-form mystery, unreliable narrators, branching revelation) is worth bringing to a wider audience, but that doing so requires removing friction introduced by the genre's own history rather than the design itself. The result is an accessible entry point to Uchikoshi's style that doesn't simplify the complexity of the underlying narrative — only the process of engaging with it.
