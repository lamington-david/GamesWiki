## Design Notes

**Minimal objective as emotional anchor.** BABBDI has exactly one task: obtain a train ticket and leave. This objective can be completed in under ten minutes without exploring most of the game's content. Rather than framing this as a failure of scope, the developers use the easy exit as a counterpoint — the city is designed to make you want to stay, and the tension between that pull and the characters' desperate desire to leave becomes the game's subject. The objective functions as a pressure valve rather than a driver.

**Movement items as perceptual lenses.** The baseball bat, pickaxe, and motorcycle scattered throughout the city each change how the player physically interacts with the space. These aren't upgrades in any conventional sense — they don't make the game easier or unlock inaccessible areas. Instead, they alter momentum, rhythm, and speed in ways that reframe familiar paths. The motorcycle that felt too fast for narrow alleyways opens up the outskirts in unexpected ways. Each item teaches you something new about a space you thought you understood.

**Lo-fi aesthetic as emotional distance.** The game's compressed, low-resolution textures and low-polygon geometry are intentional rather than a budget constraint. The visual style places the player at an uncanny remove from the world — people are legible as human but just blurred enough to feel alien, which amplifies the sense of being an outsider in a community with its own inscrutable history. This deliberate roughness also evokes a specific era of game design where players were trusted to imaginatively complete what the hardware could not render.

**Brutalist architecture as implicit narrative.** The town of Babbdi consists almost entirely of grey concrete blocks, narrow passages, and structures that feel built for function long abandoned. This isn't incidental — the oppressive built environment communicates the same thing the NPCs say directly: everyone here wants to leave and nobody can. The designers trust players to read architecture as emotional text, making exposition unnecessary.

**Freeware as design decision.** Releasing BABBDI for free on itch.io and Steam removes the transactional framing that surrounds most games. There is no completion pressure, no sense of needing to get value from a purchase. This matches the game's sensibility perfectly — you explore because you want to, you leave when you're ready, and nothing asks anything of you that you don't want to give.
