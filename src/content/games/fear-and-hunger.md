## Design Notes

**Designing for hopelessness.** Miro Haverinen created Fear & Hunger with the explicit goal of making the player feel genuine helplessness — not the performative dread of mainstream horror games, but a structural sense that survival is improbable and escape unlikely. Every mechanic reinforces this: combat is lethal and frequently unwinnable by design; characters can lose limbs permanently from a single bad encounter; resources are scarce in ways that feel like the world itself is withholding. The dungeon does not escalate towards a challenge — it begins at a level of threat that most games reserve for their hardest moments and stays there. This is not difficulty design in the conventional sense. It is a tonal commitment.

**RPG mechanics repurposed as horror tools.** The game's most striking quality is that it uses the complete vocabulary of classic turn-based RPGs — stat management, party composition, inventory weight, status effects — while inverting their usual function. In most RPGs these mechanics create a satisfying sense of growing power; in Fear & Hunger they create an illusion of preparedness that the game then violates. The familiar structure generates a specific horror: the player recognises the mechanics, knows how they should work, and watches them fail anyway. This approach is more effective than using unusual mechanics because it corrupts something known rather than asking the player to fear something alien.

**Permadeath with cumulative revelation.** Characters die permanently and cannot be revived, but the dungeon is the same in every run — hand-crafted, coherent, and full of lore that rewards repeated exploration. Each playthrough reveals new information about what happened in this place, who built it, and why. Permadeath becomes a mechanism for pacing a narrative across multiple playthroughs rather than just punishing failure. Players who lose their party and restart do not lose their understanding of the world; they re-enter with more knowledge, able to avoid mistakes they now understand. Death is a cost that buys information.

**The dungeon as a coherent nightmare.** Unlike procedural roguelikes, Fear & Hunger's dungeon was placed deliberately — every room, monster encounter, and environmental horror was designed to produce a specific experience. The world operates on internal logic that rewards investigation: factions have histories, monsters have origins, the architecture tells a story. This consistency makes the cruelty feel earned rather than arbitrary. Players who understand why the dungeon works the way it does feel less like victims of random misfortune and more like people trapped in a situation with comprehensible rules, which is its own particular kind of horror.

**Academic origins as creative freedom.** Haverinen's thesis project origins gave Fear & Hunger a freedom that commercial game development rarely permits. Developed without a publisher, without commercial pressure, and without a team to constrain or moderate his choices, Haverinen could explore subject matter and mechanics that no studio would risk. The game is genuinely transgressive rather than comfortably dark — it crosses lines that most horror games treat as off-limits. This willingness to pursue genuine discomfort rather than marketable grimness is what separated Fear & Hunger from its contemporaries and made it a cult object among players who felt that mainstream horror had grown safe.
