## Design Notes

**Discovery as the product.** Frog Fractions is, on its surface, a game where a frog catches insects and earns fruit — the kind of minimal educational browser game that would otherwise be immediately forgettable. The design insight is that this framing is a mechanism for delivering the actual game: a series of increasingly improbable genre transformations that reward the player for pressing buttons that appear to have no function. Jim Crawford's intent was to make a game whose entire value is in the experience of discovering what it actually is. This makes Frog Fractions essentially impossible to describe in a way that preserves its effect, and deliberately so — the game can only be experienced once in the way it was designed, which is an unusual design stance in an era of replayable games.

**Mystery is better than revelation.** Crawford's 2014 GDC talk is organised around a single idea: people usually find mysteries more satisfying than their solutions, because it is much easier to ask a thrilling question than to construct a thrilling answer. Frog Fractions extends this insight into a design principle: withhold information not as a difficulty mechanism but as a pleasure mechanism. The player who does not know that a mechanic exists cannot be frustrated by not understanding it; they can only be surprised when it appears. This is the opposite of conventional tutorial design, which aims to ensure the player understands every mechanic before encountering it. Crawford's approach asks whether understanding is necessary for pleasure, and answers no.

**The ethics of spoilers.** The existence of Frog Fractions created a specific cultural situation: players who discovered its true nature immediately wanted to share it, but sharing it destroyed the experience for anyone who hadn't played it. Crawford has spoken about this as a genuine design ethics question — the game's pleasure is non-transferable, which means that every piece of coverage that described what the game actually was subtracted from the pool of people who could experience it as intended. The design decision to release the game for free was partly motivated by this: the lower the barrier, the more players could reach the experience before the spoilers did. Discovery-based design creates an adversarial relationship with public discourse that no amount of spoiler warnings can fully resolve.

**Genre subversion requires genre fluency.** Frog Fractions works because players recognise the genres it passes through — the educational game, the text adventure, the space shooter, the resource-management sim — and because those genres carry strong behavioural expectations. Each transition is legible only to a player who has internalised the conventions being subverted. The humour and surprise of each shift depend entirely on the player's genre literacy. This is a design approach that can only function for a culturally specific audience, which is simultaneously Frog Fractions' greatest limitation and its greatest strength: the game is perfectly calibrated for players who have already played enough games to find their own assumptions absurd.

**The sequel problem.** Frog Fractions 2 was hidden inside an entirely different game as a DLC expansion, then found through a community ARG — an escalation of the original's concealment mechanic to a meta level. Crawford's challenge was designing surprise for players who now expected to be surprised, which he described as an unsolvable design problem that he chose to solve by changing the frame of the game entirely rather than trying to out-weird the original. The lesson is that games built entirely around a discovery are consumed by the discovery, and sequels must either abandon the original mechanic or change the target of the surprise. Frog Fractions 2 chose both simultaneously.
