## Design Notes

**The art gallery as horror architecture.** Ib's setting is an art museum, and the choice is load-bearing for the horror design. Galleries are already liminal spaces — places of silence, orderly movement, and contemplation of preserved things behind glass. Kouri takes this existing architecture and inverts its safety: the glass breaks, the preserved things move, and the orderly movement becomes flight. The gallery's floor plan is navigated repeatedly as the player discovers new passages, and each return makes the familiar strange in a new way. The horror comes not from new environments but from the same environment made progressively unsafe — a technique that produces sustained dread rather than isolated shock because the player cannot escape to somewhere they have not already been.

**Color as simultaneous aesthetic and mechanic.** The protagonist Ib carries a red rose throughout the game; her companion Garry carries a blue rose. These roses function as health systems — petals fall as harm is taken — but they are also character symbols in a setting where color is narrative language. The gallery is divided by color, with different sections featuring color-coded art that is both aesthetic and functional: red rooms contain different threats than blue rooms. This use of color as simultaneous aesthetic expression and mechanical information is an economy of design that produces a cohesive atmosphere while keeping the system readable. The single design choice — color as identity — carries both the emotional and the functional layers.

**Companion mechanics as externalized vulnerability.** Garry functions as a companion who protects and is protected, whose behavior changes in response to repeated frightening events, and who can be permanently separated from the player through certain decisions. The companion mechanic is not primarily about combat or resource management but about emotional stakes: the player cares about Garry's safety in a way that makes certain horror moments devastating. Kouri uses the companion to externalize vulnerability — a player who might not feel scared on their own behalf feels scared on behalf of a character they have come to care about. This technique creates horror through attachment rather than threat, which is more durable and more affecting than jump-scare design.

**RPG Maker as intentional constraint.** Ib uses RPG Maker 2000, a highly constrained engine with limited visual and audio capability. The choice to work within these constraints is not a limitation but an approach: the engine's austerity matches the horror strategy of implication over depiction. Limited sprite resolution means monster designs are suggested rather than rendered in detail, which is frequently more frightening than photographic clarity. Simple audio means the sound design works through suggestion and silence. Constraint forced a design discipline — show less — that turns out to be exactly right for horror. The lesson is not that constraint is inherently good but that formal constraints can align with content requirements in ways that produce coherence.

**Multiple endings as mechanical kindness.** Ib features multiple endings determined by the player's relationship with Garry and their choices throughout. The best endings require discovering non-obvious acts of care — actions that register as consideration for another character without being flagged as game-mechanically significant decisions. This design — where kindness is rewarded but not announced as a mechanic — requires the player to act on emotional impulse rather than optimization logic. The multiple-ending structure also means the game is designed for replay, with each run potentially discovering that earlier decisions had consequences that were not visible at the time. This structure rewards players who play as characters rather than players who play as optimizers.
