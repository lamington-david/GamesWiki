## Design Notes

**Dimension-shifting as tactile contrast.** Quantum Conundrum's four dimensions — Fluffy (objects become nearly weightless), Heavy (massively dense), Slow Motion, and Reverse Gravity — were each chosen for the clarity and physicality of their contrast. Swift's guiding principle was that switching dimensions should feel immediately and viscerally different: the player should feel the physics shift, not just notice a colour change. Each dimension was designed so its affordances were self-evident from the first interaction, without requiring a tutorial card. The design lesson is that mechanics earn their place by being perceptually distinct, not just functionally different.

**Player psychology over instruction.** Swift's foundational insight from Portal was that human beings are animals shaped by millions of years of environmental reading, and that game design can exploit these instincts to guide players without any explicit instruction. Quantum Conundrum extends this — rooms are lit, coloured, and furnished to pull the eye toward the next action. Objects are scaled to communicate their role. The player's instinct to grab a box and throw it is triggered by how the box is placed, not by a prompt. Swift's approach treats the environment as a language that players already speak, and designs exclusively within that language.

**Democratic development and rapid prototyping.** At Airtight Games, Swift ran what she called a democratic development environment — every designer had equal standing to propose or challenge a mechanic, and no idea survived without prototyping. The four dimensions were each independently built and tested before integration, and several candidate dimensions were discarded when playtesting revealed that the contrast or the logic was unclear. This process of building and testing in isolation before combining was the structural discipline that kept the final game coherent — each mechanic had to prove itself independently before it was allowed to interact with the others.

**Accessibility as a design multiplier.** Swift tested Quantum Conundrum with players as young as eight and as old as her parents, deliberately seeking a wide skill range before committing to any puzzle design. The insight was that making puzzles accessible to a broader audience is not the same as making them trivial — it means refining the logic until it is genuinely learnable, not just solvable by experts. Puzzles that stumped casual players were almost always signposting failures, not difficulty calibrations. When the spatial logic was sufficiently clear, players across all skill levels could follow it; when they couldn't, the puzzle was sending the wrong visual message.

**The weight of influence.** Designing Quantum Conundrum in the shadow of Portal was a conscious creative challenge. Swift has been open about the difficulty of escaping the Portal comparison while working in the same genre with similar mechanics. Her response was to commit fully to what was distinct: the dimension-switching mechanic rather than portal placement, a cartoonish tone rather than clinical sterility, and a focus on physical object manipulation rather than spatial redirection. The game illustrates how a designer can draw on their most influential work without simply repeating it — by identifying which specific design decisions were personal versus which were incidental to the previous game's setting.
