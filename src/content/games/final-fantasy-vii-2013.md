## Design Notes

**The pre-rendered background compromise.** Square's solution to rendering a cinematic 3D world on PlayStation hardware was to paint detailed backgrounds as static images and place animated polygon characters on top of them. This technical workaround produced an unintended aesthetic benefit: the fixed camera angles forced compositions that felt authored rather than arbitrary. Every room was designed as a tableau. The technique constrained the player's perspective in ways that film directors use deliberately, and turned limitation into visual identity.

**Materia as a classless system.** The Materia system replaced traditional JRPG job classes with physical objects that any character can equip. Magic is not owned by a character — it's owned by a stone that can be transferred, combined, and levelled independently of the character holding it. This design decision has two downstream effects: no character becomes obsolete as party composition changes, and experimentation carries no penalty because the Materia retains its progress. The system rewards curiosity about combinations without punishing commitment to a build.

**Midgar as structural misdirection.** FF7 opens in Midgar, a mechanised city-state that in any other JRPG would constitute the entire game's setting. Square spends the equivalent of eight to ten hours establishing it as the world before the player escapes to discover the actual planet below. This structure trains the player to believe they understand the game's scope, then doubles it. Every subsequent revelation — the depth of Shinra's reach, the existence of other civilisations, the age of the world — is amplified by having established such a concrete frame to shatter.

**Aerith's death as a design statement.** The death of Aerith Gainsborough is the most studied narrative event in JRPG history not because it was unexpected, but because Square refused to soften its consequences. She cannot be revived. The game does not allow the player to avoid it through preparation or skill. This intransigence was a deliberate design decision to demonstrate that player attachment to characters could carry genuine dramatic weight — that games could mourn, not just narrate loss. The decision established emotional commitment as a valid design goal.

**Active Time Battle as controlled urgency.** The ATB system added a real-time pressure element to turn-based combat — enemy gauges fill even when the player is selecting commands. This creates a psychological tension that pure turn-based systems lack, without sacrificing the strategic planning that made JRPGs accessible. The system has been iterated on for decades precisely because it solved a genuine problem: how to make structured, menu-driven combat feel dangerous.
