## Design Notes

**The one-screen rule.** Every challenge in Celeste is contained within a single screen, and each screen teaches exactly one mechanic before the next extends it. This principle — what Maddy Thorson calls designing at the "micro" level — produces a game that is simultaneously one of the hardest platformers ever made and one of the most learnable. Players who fail a screen hundreds of times do so understanding exactly what they are trying to do and exactly what they are getting wrong, never because the goal was unclear or the test was unfair. This legibility is the foundation of Celeste's difficulty: hard games feel unfair when the failure is opaque; Celeste makes every failure transparent.

**Assist Mode as a design value, not an afterthought.** Celeste's Assist Mode — which allows players to slow game speed, add extra dashes, grant invincibility, and adjust difficulty along multiple axes — is notable not because it exists but because of how it was framed. The mode asks players to adjust specific parameters rather than flipping a binary "easy mode" switch, and it carries no in-game stigma: achievements and the story are unchanged. Thorson's public writing on Assist Mode makes clear this was a deliberate design position — the game is for everyone who wants to experience it, and accommodating different players is part of what the game is about, not a concession to players who couldn't handle the "real" version. It offers a model for how difficulty accommodation can be built into a game's identity.

**Mental health themes integrated into mechanics.** Celeste's narrative — Madeline climbing a mountain while struggling with depression and anxiety — is not illustrated by the mechanics; it is the mechanics. The dash, the protagonist's primary ability, depletes and must be recovered from contact with the environment. This rhythm of expenditure and recovery, of pushing against limits and finding rest, is both the core gameplay loop and the game's psychological thesis. The themes didn't come first; Thorson has discussed how they emerged from their own experience during development. The design achievement is that the themes are not added by text or cutscenes — they are built into the kinesthetics of how the character moves through the world.

**Speedrunning and accessibility coexisting.** Celeste was designed with both ends of the skill spectrum in mind simultaneously. Assist Mode accommodates players who struggle with precision; the B-side cassette tapes and C-sides form a hidden layer of challenge among the most mechanically demanding platforming ever designed. Crucially, the routes used by speedrunners — the fastest paths through each screen — are designed to be visible and achievable for expert players without closing off the standard route for regular players. This coexistence of accessibility and extreme depth in the same levels is a design achievement most games don't attempt. The one-screen structure enables it: each screen is a contained problem with multiple solutions at different skill levels.

**Prototype-to-full-game development discipline.** Celeste was originally made in four days as a prototype for a game jam, then developed into a full game over roughly a year. The speed of the original prototype demonstrates how much can be established through rapid exploration: core mechanic, aesthetic direction, and tonal register were all fixed in four days. The discipline in the expansion phase — knowing which elements of the prototype to preserve, which to extend, and what new systems would be needed — produced a game that feels as coherent as something designed top-down from the start. The lesson is that jam games and rapid prototypes can be legitimate starting points for commercial releases, provided the expansion is driven by understanding what the prototype was actually doing right.
