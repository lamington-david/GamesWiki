## Design Notes

**Posture as the new combat grammar.** The most consequential design decision in Sekiro is the replacement of stamina with posture — a meter that fills when strikes are blocked or deflected rather than when attacks are made. Where Souls combat rewards patience and stamina conservation, Sekiro's posture system rewards pressure: stopping attacking allows both the player's and the enemy's posture to recover. This mechanical inversion forced FromSoftware to rebuild its combat from first principles. Bosses designed around posture breaking have a completely different flow from Souls bosses designed around stamina attrition — they're closer to rhythm encounters than endurance tests. Players trained on Soulsborne had to unlearn their defensive instincts, which is a remarkable design ask of an existing fanbase.

**The grappling hook as vertical level design.** Sekiro's shinobi grappling hook wasn't a traversal gadget added to an existing level design — it was the premise around which the maps were built. The game's environments are designed with grapple points, high vantage positions, and multi-level enemy arrangements that only make sense if the player has the ability to reposition vertically on demand. This produced a style of level design FromSoftware had never attempted: spaces read differently from above than from below, ambush opportunities require three-dimensional thinking, and the same room can be navigated through multiple vertical paths. The hook also gave boss encounters a spatial vocabulary absent from Souls games, allowing bosses with aerial or elevated phases that respond to the player's grappling.

**The fixed protagonist as a design tool.** Every prior FromSoftware game from the Souls lineage used a silent, customisable player-character. Sekiro introduces Wolf: a character with a face, voice, and relationship to the story's events. This was not an aesthetic choice — Miyazaki describes it as a structural decision to tell a more concentrated story, one where the player's emotional investment is in a specific person rather than a self-projected avatar. The tradeoff is that Sekiro is the least replayable of FromSoftware's recent output from a build-diversity standpoint. The gain is narrative and tonal coherence that the Souls games' ambiguity-by-design couldn't achieve.

**Resurrection and the management of death.** The resurrection mechanic — Wolf can rise from death once per life — is one of Sekiro's cleverest design moves because it maintains the fear of death while removing some of its friction. Players who die recklessly exhaust their resurrection, then face permanent death with less information about how to survive. Players who use resurrection wisely extend dangerous runs without making the game easy. The Dragonrot system, which penalises reckless overuse of resurrection by afflicting NPCs, creates a broader cost for death that reframes the mechanic as a resource to be managed. The net effect is a death system that sustains Soulslike tension without the full punishing reload of its predecessors.

**Refusing the comforts of a proven template.** Sekiro was FromSoftware's most commercially risky recent release precisely because it dismantled what the Soulsborne community had been trained to expect. No stamina management, no build variety, no multiplayer, no gradual levelling — these weren't oversights but deliberate removals that concentrated the game's design energy into a single, mastery-focused combat system. The game won Game of the Year at multiple outlets and sold millions of copies, demonstrating that genuine mechanical novelty can be commercially viable even for a studio with established audience expectations. The design lesson is that template-breaking, done with conviction and craft, is not a risk but an opportunity.
