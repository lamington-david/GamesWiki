## Design Notes

**Exploration without instruction as design philosophy.** Pietro Righi Riva argued that designers must actively resist the impulse to explain — that a game's job is to create conditions for player discovery, not to tutor players through it. MirrorMoon EP provides no instruction beyond the controls themselves; what each mechanic does and why must be inferred from play. This deliberate withholding forces players into a mode of attention that most games never demand: genuine curiosity about the system rather than competent execution of understood rules. The lesson is that players fill the absence of explanation with investment and interpretation — that ignorance, if designed carefully, can be the source of the game's meaning.

**True sci-fi as design framework.** Santa Ragione anchored MirrorMoon EP in hard science fiction literature rather than in video game conventions of what sci-fi looks like. The solar systems are procedurally generated and described through minimalist observation; the narrative is told in fragments that require interpretation. This distinguishes the game from sci-fi games that use the aesthetic of space without the intellectual content of the genre. The design takeaway is that genuine genre source material — actual literature, not just its visual trappings — can be a productive constraint that forces decisions conventional game design wisdom would forbid.

**Purposeful discovery through systemic mystery.** The game creates a sense of discovery not through authored set-pieces but through the inherent mystery of interacting with systems whose rules are only partially revealed. Players find that actions have consequences across sessions and solar systems — that a shared map connects individual players in ways that initially seem like coincidence. This creates moments of genuine surprise that authored content rarely can, because the player cannot predict them. The design insight is that systemic mysteries — those arising from the interaction of rules rather than from scripted surprises — feel more personal to the player because they arise from their own choices.

**Games happen in the minds of players.** Righi Riva's central thesis is that truly great games aren't defined by their rules, but emerge naturally if designers give players freedom to experiment. The rules of MirrorMoon EP are sparse; the experience of playing it is not. This points to a distinction between the designed system and the player's interpretation of it — and suggests that the game worth making is not the system but the experience it occasions. Designers who focus on maximising what they put into a game risk crowding out what players bring to it.

**Low rating as honest signal.** MirrorMoon EP's modest Steam rating reflects its refusal to compromise: the game is inaccessible by design, and players who expect genre conventions or clear objectives will be confused and disappointed. Santa Ragione did not soften the experience to broaden its audience, and the game's critical reception among those who appreciated it was unusually intense as a result. This represents a legitimate design decision — to make a game for a small specific audience rather than a large general one — and the rating reflects the gap between that decision and the expectations of a wider Steam audience. The question it poses to designers is whether breadth of audience is always the right optimisation target.
