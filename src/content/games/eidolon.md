## Design Notes

**A third path between systems and story.** Eidolon was built as a deliberate argument. At the time of its development, the indie game discourse framed exploration games as either systems-driven (survival mechanics, resource loops) or story-driven (forward-pointing narrative, authored plot). Maxon's thesis was that there was a third position: backward-pointing authored history, where the narrative had already concluded and the player's role was archaeological reconstruction rather than participation. The player is not a protagonist who changes the world — they are a witness piecing together what the world was. This is a different relationship to narrative than most games establish.

**The unspecified player character as a design choice.** Eidolon gives the player no character — no name, no inventory interface, no defined body. This was not an omission but a deliberate design decision to prevent player goal conflict. A character with a defined identity creates expectations: they should want things, do things, have opinions. Maxon's observation was that an unspecified player allows the exploration to be genuinely open-ended, because there is no narrative pressure on what the player should investigate or how long they should dwell. The game is structurally committed to letting the player's attention be their only motivation.

**Impermanence as thematic architecture.** The game's title comes from Walt Whitman's poem "Eidolons," which Whitman uses to describe the ghostly essences that outlast material forms. Maxon structured the game around a thematic opposition: the transience of human civilization versus the permanence of nature. The Pacific Northwest setting — forests reclaiming human structures, wildlife returning to former cities — is not background but argument. The survival mechanics were intentionally de-emphasized so that the forest does not feel threatening but inevitable. What was built by humans is temporary; what existed before will persist.

**Survival mechanics as texture rather than loop.** Most survival games make resource gathering and death avoidance the core mechanical loop. Eidolon includes crafting, animal trapping, and fishing but strips them of urgency. The player rarely faces meaningful survival pressure. Maxon's intention was to use survival mechanics as texture — to make the world feel inhabitable and materially real without turning that inhabitation into a management game. This is an unusual design position: using genre conventions for verisimilitude while refusing to let them become the game's purpose.

**Thesis as design methodology.** Eidolon began as an academic project at Western Washington University, which gave Maxon a framework that few commercial games have: articulating a design argument before building the game. The requirement to justify the project's existence in writing — the "Why Eidolon Needs to Exist" post predates the release — meant the game had a theoretical foundation that could guide decisions when the design became ambiguous. Games built as arguments about what games can do tend to be more consistent than games that accumulate features; the academic origin enforced a conceptual discipline that persisted through commercial release.
