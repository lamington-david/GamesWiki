## Design Notes

**The four-pillar architecture.** Human Revolution's foundational design principle is that every encounter in the game must be solvable through at least two of four approaches: direct combat, stealth, hacking, and social engineering. No single mission or objective should block a player who has committed to one approach. This required extraordinary level design discipline — every room was designed with multiple entry points, alternative routes, hackable terminals that open doors, and NPC schedules that reward patient observation. The pillars are not just character build options; they are lenses through which every piece of content must be reviewed before it ships.

**The soul document as production anchor.** Before a line of code was written, the Eidos Montréal team spent months writing what they called the game's 'soul document' — a dense, written definition of what Deus Ex must feel like, what it must allow, and what it must never do. This document was distributed to the entire team and used as a reference in every design disagreement. When a mechanic or level section was proposed, the first question was not "is this fun" but "is this consistent with the soul." Pre-production design artefacts that encode intent rather than feature lists are chronically underused in game development; Human Revolution's coherence is partly the product of having one.

**Player agency as the non-negotiable value.** The directive the team gave themselves was "thou shalt not force the player." This manifested in specific mechanics: the cover system could not be mandatory; conversations could be won through multiple argument paths; augmentations could be maxed in different branches without penalising alternate builds. The famous boss fight controversy — three combat-only encounters outsourced to another studio that broke stealth playthroughs — demonstrated what happens when this principle is violated. The negative reception to those bosses was not merely about their quality; it was that they violated the game's core promise. A broken commitment to player agency damages the entire game's credibility.

**The cyber-renaissance as a coherent visual thesis.** Art director Jonathan Jacques-Belletête built the game's aesthetic around a single unifying idea: the Renaissance was the last time humanity's ambition outpaced its wisdom, and the game's cyberpunk future repeats that moment at a civilisational scale. This thesis generated the specific visual decisions — gold and black palette, da Vinci-inspired augmentation designs, neo-gothic architecture — that gave the game its distinctive look. The design lesson is that a strong conceptual metaphor produces more visual coherence than any style guide, because it tells artists not just what the world looks like but what it means.

**Moral weight without morality systems.** Human Revolution makes extensive use of conversation choices and mission decisions that have consequences in the world, but it deliberately avoids a karma meter or morality score. The designers believed that quantifying ethics reduces it to optimisation — players would choose actions to maximise the score rather than to make a choice that felt right. Instead, consequences manifest as world state changes, dialogue shifts, and story developments that communicate the effect of player decisions without assigning a numerical value to them. The absence of a visible morality system forces players to evaluate their decisions on their own terms.
