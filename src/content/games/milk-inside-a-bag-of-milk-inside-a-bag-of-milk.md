## Design Notes

**Minimalism as deliberate choice, not limitation.** Milk inside uses extremely simple geometric shapes and near-monochromatic visuals — this is not a constraint from limited resources but a deliberate design decision. Stripping the visual layer forces player attention onto text and audio, which carry the full emotional weight. Kryukov's original soundtrack is as integral as the writing; the spare visuals create a canvas where the moments of colour, movement, or visual distortion read as significant. The lesson is that reducing one sensory channel can amplify others — minimalism is not simplicity but a form of focus.

**The player as internal voice.** The player character is not the protagonist but a voice inside her head. Choices presented aren't player actions but internal responses to her situation — things "you" say to her as she tries to complete a mundane task. This framing creates an unusual empathy mechanism: the player simultaneously observes the protagonist's distress from outside and participates in her inner monologue. The second-person construction — "you can hear the fluorescent lights" — places the player in the protagonist's sensory experience while the choice system positions them as a separate consciousness. This split is how the game achieves its particular discomfort: you are complicit in her experience without being her.

**Interface as expressive material.** Milk inside uses the visual novel format's conventional elements — dialogue boxes, choice menus, progress indicators — and subtly distorts them to reflect the protagonist's mental state. Standard interface components contain unexpected text; descriptions of mundane actions use fractured, recursive language; the visual novel's fourth wall is unreliably maintained. By treating the medium's own conventions as material for distortion rather than neutral delivery vessels, Kryukov builds psychological horror through form rather than content alone. This approach — using interface expectations as something to violate — is available to any genre that has established conventions.

**Accessibility as emotional design.** The game includes an optional mode that softens the more distressing content. Rather than simply censoring material, this option reframes the player's relationship to the protagonist's difficulty — the experience is still communicated, but the player can choose their degree of immersion in it. In horror contexts, designer intent and player protection are often treated as opposites: either you experience the horror or you don't. Kryukov's approach treats accessibility as a design problem requiring the same care as any other system — the option is built into the experience rather than bolted on as an afterthought.

**Duration as genre signal.** Completing Milk inside takes 15 to 20 minutes. This is not a limitation but a statement about what the game is. The brevity makes replay plausible and the game's ambiguous ending actively encourages it. The short runtime positions the work as closer to a short story or poem than a conventional game experience, with the corresponding expectation that every line is doing work. The question of how long a game should be is one most developers answer by default rather than deliberately; here the answer is clearly intentional, and the game's emotional impact is partly a function of its refusal to overstay.
