## Design Notes

**Radical redesign as design education.** RiME spent five years in development during which it was transformed almost completely. The original game featured a Spartan warrior protagonist, a world based on Greek mythology, and combat systems. Tequila Works eventually recognised that the island itself was the interesting element — not the combat, not the mythology, not the character. They stripped everything away that didn't serve the experience of being a small figure in a large, silent, sun-drenched world. This kind of pivoting requires a clear sense of what the core experience is meant to feel like, and the willingness to abandon substantial work when it serves the wrong goal.

**Art direction as emotional language.** The decision to study Joaquín Sorolla — a late 19th-century Spanish painter whose work is defined by the quality of Mediterranean light on water and white fabric — gave the art team a precise model for what RiME should look like. Sorolla's paintings are warm, open, and suffused with a melancholy that underlies their apparent brightness. Tequila Works replicated this tonal duality: the island is beautiful and the light is gorgeous, but something is wrong, and the visual beauty makes the wrongness more disturbing rather than less. Art direction used to communicate emotional subtext rather than decorative preference is a skill; RiME is a detailed case study in how it works.

**The child protagonist as design decision.** Replacing the Spartan warrior with a small child was not merely a narrative choice — it was a systematic design decision that changed every other element of the game. A child cannot fight. A child's relationship to a large, uncanny environment is one of vulnerability and wonder rather than combat competence. This single change determined the absence of combat, the scale of the environments, the nature of the puzzles, and the emotional register of the whole experience. Character design as systemic constraint — where the protagonist's nature determines what the game can be — is a design pattern worth understanding independently of this specific case.

**Environmental storytelling without text.** RiME has no dialogue, no collectible notes, no written lore. The story must be communicated entirely through what the player finds, what changes as they progress, and how the environment responds to their presence. This creates a design challenge that most games resolve by adding text — RiME chose the harder path of designing spaces, sequences, and symbolic imagery that could carry narrative weight without verbal explanation. The approach requires players to engage actively rather than passively, assembling meaning from visual evidence rather than receiving it directly. The tradeoff is ambiguity; the reward is that what players understand, they understand more deeply.

**The island as protagonist.** The game's creative director described wanting players to feel they were exploring a place they were not supposed to be in — a forbidden paradise. This framing treats the island itself, rather than the boy exploring it, as the game's central presence. Every design decision — the scale, the lighting, the responsiveness of animals to the player's presence, the way ruins suggest a civilization that predated the player — serves to make the world feel prior, complete, and self-sufficient. The player is a visitor. This inversion of conventional game design, where the world exists to serve the player, is what gives RiME its specific quality of melancholy.
