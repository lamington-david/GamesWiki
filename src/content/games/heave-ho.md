## Design Notes

**One mechanic, infinite variety.** Heave Ho's entire design rests on a single physical interaction: grip with the left trigger, grip with the right trigger, release. Every level in the game is the consequence of applying this constraint to increasingly complex geometry and physics environments. The minimalism of the verb set is a design feature, not a limitation — because the physics engine provides endless situational variation, the game never needs to introduce new mechanics to stay interesting. Players who understand the core mechanic after five minutes can still be surprised by it ninety minutes later.

**Physics as co-op communication medium.** When two or more players grip each other's limbs, their bodies become the communication channel between them. The physics system translates intention into consequence in ways that can surprise both players simultaneously, creating genuine shared uncertainty. Unlike co-op games that generate tension through information asymmetry (player A knows something player B doesn't), Heave Ho generates tension through physical uncertainty — what will happen when you let go is unknown to everyone, including the player making the decision.

**Failure as primary content.** Heave Ho was designed so that falling — failing — is funnier than succeeding. The slapstick deaths, flailing character animations, and screams from co-players watching someone drop into the abyss are the memorable moments the game is producing. Successful level completions are satisfying but brief; failures play out elaborately and become stories. This inversion of the traditional difficulty assumption — that failure should be minimised and success prolonged — transforms the game's entire emotional register into one built around comedy.

**Couch co-op as the true medium.** Every design decision in Heave Ho reflects that the game is built for people in the same physical space. Players must coordinate through spoken language, gesture, and shared observation — communication modalities that online play strips of their natural urgency through latency and the absence of presence. The game originated in a game jam, and the game jam context was physically co-located play. This social contract is embedded in the design: the chaos only produces its intended comedy when the people experiencing it can see and hear each other's reactions.

**Game jam origins as lasting design foundation.** Heave Ho began at a game jam with the theme of "street workout" — the designers wondered what a game would feel like if characters had arms but no legs. This single constraint produced the character design and the core mechanic simultaneously: limbless figures that can only interact with the world through arm movement. The best game jam origins remain legible in the final product, and in Heave Ho the origin is essentially the whole game. The studio's discipline in not breaking from this premise across a full commercial release is a rare example of constraint maintained.
