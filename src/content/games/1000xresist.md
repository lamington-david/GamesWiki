## Design Notes

**Specificity as universal design strategy.** 1000xRESIST is explicitly a game about the Chinese-Canadian diaspora experience, COVID-era alienation, and the particular grief of watching grandparents die while borders are closed. Remy Siu and the sunset visitor team made no attempt to sand down the cultural specificity of the story into a more accessible universalism. The critical and audience response — a Peabody Award, overwhelmingly positive Steam reviews, wide recognition as one of 2024's most important games — validated the opposite approach: the more specific and personally rooted the material, the more universally it resonated. This is a significant design lesson, counterintuitive to many game developers who fear that specificity narrows audience, when in practice it often deepens engagement.

**Theater training as game design methodology.** The sunset visitor team's background was primarily in theater rather than game development, and this shaped the game's construction at every level. Dialogue was written with a theatrical understanding of rhythm, subtext, and the relationship between character and space. Blocking — where characters stand, how they move, what the camera shows — was approached as staging rather than animation direction. The game's 3D environments function more like theater sets than game levels: spaces designed to support performance rather than player navigation. This cross-disciplinary background produced a game whose storytelling craft is legible to anyone familiar with stage performance, and whose particular strengths — nuanced character work, precise dialogue — are exactly what game narrative most frequently lacks.

**Nonlinear narrative tracked through system rather than script.** 1000xRESIST's story spans multiple timelines and perspectives, with the player gradually assembling a complete picture from fragmented scenes. The team managed this nonlinear structure using a MIRO board tracking chronology, themes, and scene connectivity across the entire script. This organisational discipline — treating the narrative as a system with dependencies and relationships rather than a document to be written sequentially — is what made the nonlinear structure coherent rather than arbitrary. The game demonstrates that nonlinear narrative is not primarily a writing problem but a structural engineering problem, requiring tools and methods appropriate to the complexity of the system being built.

**Small team, full creative vision.** The sunset visitor core development team was four people, with additional contractors working part-time. The game's ambition — hours of voice-acted narrative, multiple playable characters, a fully realised science fiction world — vastly exceeded what a team of that size would conventionally attempt. The enabling factors were a shared background that gave the team efficient communication, clear creative ownership from a single creative director, and a production model that staged the work across a long timeline. 1000xRESIST demonstrates that the size of a team and the scale of its creative ambition are not tightly coupled, provided the team has the discipline and shared vision to operate efficiently.

**Peabody recognition and the expanding definition of game criticism.** 1000xRESIST won a Peabody Award — an honour previously associated with broadcast journalism and documentary — alongside a Nebula Award for science fiction writing. These recognitions from outside game industry institutions reflect how the game positioned itself: as a work of literature and social commentary that uses the game medium rather than as a game that incorporates literary or social elements. The design implication is about ambition of intent: sunset visitor built 1000xRESIST to be evaluated as a meaningful cultural artefact, and the game's construction — its willingness to prioritise emotional and intellectual weight over play mechanics — reflects that intent at every level.
