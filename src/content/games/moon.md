## Design Notes

**Subversion as the entire premise.** moon begins with the player watching a conventional JRPG hero murder creatures and loot dungeons — and then places you in that world after the hero has left, tasked with healing the damage. Every design decision flows from this inversion: instead of levelling by killing, you level by performing acts of kindness for the game's inhabitants. The anti-RPG concept is not a layer on top of the game; it is the game's entire structural logic.

**Love as a mechanical resource.** The central mechanic — gaining "Love Points" by helping characters rather than defeating them — is not simply an aesthetic swap. It forces the player into a fundamentally different relationship with the game world. NPCs have routines, feelings, and needs that exist independent of the player's presence. To gain love, the player must observe, understand, and respond to those needs. This makes attentiveness the primary skill, rather than resource optimisation or reaction time.

**The world as prior text.** moon is structured around the idea that a conventional RPG story already happened before you arrived. The ruined villages, grieving animals, and chaotic systems are all consequences of the hero's actions. This creates a game designed around reading and interpretation — understanding what the world was before you got there — rather than around action and reaction. The player's role is closer to archaeologist or therapist than warrior.

**Opacity and ambient storytelling.** Love-de-Lic designed moon's world with information deliberately withheld. The game does not explain its systems fully, does not mark optimal paths, and does not confirm whether you are doing well. This opacity encourages players to treat the world as genuinely alive rather than as a puzzle with a known solution. The strangeness is meant to be inhabited, not solved.

**Legacy beyond Japan.** moon remained Japan-exclusive for 22 years partly because its publisher ASCII was cautious about how western audiences would respond to its unconventional design. Its belated localisation coincided with a wave of games — including Undertale, Disco Elysium, and others — that had independently arrived at similar anti-RPG ideas. The game now reads as an origin point for a design lineage that would not discover it for decades, making it a rare case of a work whose influence spread without the work itself being accessible.
